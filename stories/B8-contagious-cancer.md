# B8: Cancer That Catches

*Category: Dangerous Biology*

## The Story

Cancer is, in the standard biological model, a disease of one individual. A cell acquires mutations, escapes normal growth controls, and divides without limit—but within the confines of its host. The idea that cancer cells could become infectious, jumping from one body to another like a pathogen, seems like science fiction. It is not. It has happened at least three times in nature, and one of those cases is currently driving a marsupial species toward extinction.

The Tasmanian devil (*Sarcophilus harrisii*) is an intensely social, intensely aggressive animal. Devils bite each other constantly—during competition for food, during mating, during territorial disputes. This habit made it possible for something almost unprecedented to occur. In the 1980s, a single devil's cancer cell acquired the ability to survive being transferred via bite wounds from one individual to another. The resulting clone, devil facial tumor 1 (DFT1), has now spread across Tasmania and caused approximately 80% population decline. The Tasmanian devil is now listed as endangered.

DFT1 is a transmissible tumor derived from Schwann cells—cells that insulate nerve fibers. It grows as large masses on the face and in the mouth, metastasizes rapidly, and kills its host within months of appearance by blocking eating or spreading to internal organs. The tumor cells are allografts: they are genetically foreign to every devil they infect. Normally, an immune system would destroy foreign cells instantly. DFT1 evades this by downregulating MHC class I molecules—the surface proteins that flag cells as "self" or "other"—making itself effectively invisible to the immune system. The tumor doesn't just spread. It hides.

A second, genetically distinct transmissible cancer in Tasmanian devils—DFT2—was discovered in 2015 in a small population on the southern peninsula. DFT1 and DFT2 arose from completely different cells, proving that Tasmanian devils are somehow unusually prone to generating transmissible cancers—possibly because their island geography and intense fighting behavior create ideal conditions for cell transfer, or because they have reduced MHC diversity that makes immune evasion easier.

The only other well-studied transmissible cancer in mammals is canine transmissible venereal tumor (CTVT), also called Sticker's sarcoma. CTVT is ancient: genomic analysis places its origin at somewhere between 4,000 and 11,000 years ago, making it one of the oldest continuously existing somatic cell lineages on Earth. The founder dog's genome has been largely replaced in CTVT cells, but the tumor lineage itself has survived, spreading globally through mating. Unlike DFT1, CTVT usually regresses spontaneously after a few months as the host immune system eventually recognizes and destroys it—but not before the tumor has had time to transmit to a new host.

Together, these transmissible cancers represent a category of biology that was not previously thought possible: a cancer that has escaped its host and become, in effect, an organism in its own right. CTVT's genome carries the mutations of 11,000 years of evolution. It has outlasted the individual dogs it infected by orders of magnitude. It no longer has a single host—it is its own lineage, adapting to survive in whatever body it can reach.

The implications extend beyond zoology. Understanding how these cancers evade immune destruction—particularly DFT1's MHC downregulation—has direct relevance to understanding how human cancers evade immune surveillance. Tasmanian devils are teaching us something about cancer immunology that we couldn't have learned any other way.

## Key Facts

- Devil facial tumor disease (DFT1) originated from a single devil's nerve-sheath cell in the 1980s and has caused ~80% population collapse across Tasmania
- DFT1 evades immune detection by downregulating MHC class I molecules, making tumor cells invisible to host T cells
- A second, independently arising transmissible cancer (DFT2) was discovered in Tasmanian devils in 2015
- Canine transmissible venereal tumor (CTVT) is estimated to be 4,000–11,000 years old—among the oldest continuously surviving cell lineages on Earth
- Only three naturally transmissible cancers are known in mammals: DFT1, DFT2, and CTVT; a fourth type infects soft-shell clams
