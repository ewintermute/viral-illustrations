# F2: The Parasite That Makes You Love Cats

*Category: Stranger Than Fiction*

## The Story

*Toxoplasma gondii* is a single-celled parasite that infects roughly one-third of the global human population. It is transmitted through contact with cat feces or through consumption of undercooked meat containing tissue cysts. In immunocompetent adults, infection is typically asymptomatic—the parasite forms quiescent cysts in muscle and brain tissue, where it can persist indefinitely without apparent harm. This is the standard story: a common, benign infection that most people carry without knowing it.

The less standard story begins with what *Toxoplasma* does to rats.

In 1994, Joanne Webster and colleagues at Oxford published a finding that has since been confirmed by multiple independent laboratories: rats infected with *Toxoplasma* lose their innate fear of cat urine. Normal rats, when exposed to the distinctive odor of feline urine, immediately show fear behavior—they retreat, freeze, spend time in protected areas. Infected rats behave differently. In some experiments, infected rats not only fail to fear cat urine but actually spend more time near it. They appear attracted to the smell.

The evolutionary logic is elegant and disturbing. *Toxoplasma*'s sexual reproduction can only occur inside the feline gut—cats are the parasite's definitive host. Intermediate hosts (rodents, humans, other warm-blooded animals) harbor asexual stages but cannot complete the parasite's reproductive cycle. A *Toxoplasma* parasite inside a rat that makes the rat more likely to be eaten by a cat is, from the parasite's perspective, a successful manipulation.

The mechanism involves the brain's reward circuitry. Infection shifts activity in rat brain regions: instead of activating defensive circuits in the ventromedial hypothalamus when cat urine is detected, infected rats activate regions associated with sexual arousal—particularly the posterodorsal medial amygdala, which normally responds to sex pheromones. The cat smell is, neurologically, processed as attractive rather than threatening. Dopamine antagonists block the effect, suggesting that dopamine elevation is part of the mechanism. Notably, the manipulation appears specific to feline odors: infected rats show normal fear responses to non-feline predator odors, suggesting a targeted rewiring rather than a general suppression of fear.

The more disturbing question is what *Toxoplasma* does to humans. Studies across multiple countries have found associations between *Toxoplasma* seropositivity and increased risk-taking behavior in humans: higher rates of traffic accidents, elevated impulsivity, greater entrepreneurial risk tolerance. Infected individuals of both sexes show personality changes, though the direction differs: infected men tend toward suspicion, jealousy, and reduced compliance; infected women toward warmth, generosity, and increased confidence. Both patterns involve altered dopamine signaling—consistent with the mechanism proposed in rodents.

The link to schizophrenia is the most sobering. More than 100 published studies and several meta-analyses have found that patients with schizophrenia have significantly elevated rates of *Toxoplasma* antibodies—roughly 1.9 times the rate of uninfected controls. The association has been reported across cultures, countries, and diagnostic systems. The mechanism proposed involves astrocyte activation, kynurenic acid production, and disruption of NMDA receptor signaling—the same glutamatergic pathway implicated in the neurobiology of schizophrenia. Cat ownership in childhood has been associated with roughly double the risk of schizophrenia in some studies.

Correlation is not causation. None of this is proof that *Toxoplasma* causes schizophrenia or turns infected humans into risk-takers in any reliable individual sense. But the accumulation of associations is striking enough that the Stanley Medical Research Institute has funded dedicated research programs to investigate it. A parasite that lives silently in one-third of human brains, persisting for decades, altering dopamine signaling in measurable ways: this is not nothing. What it actually is remains one of the more unsettling open questions in infectious disease.

## Key Facts

- *Toxoplasma gondii* infects ~1/3 of the global human population; sexual reproduction requires the feline gut, making felines the definitive host and rodent behavior manipulation adaptive
- Infected rats show fear extinction specifically for cat urine and may show attraction to it—activating sexual arousal brain circuits (posterodorsal medial amygdala) instead of fear circuits
- In humans, infection associates with increased risk-taking, impulsivity, traffic accidents, and entrepreneurial behavior; personality effects differ by sex
- 100+ studies find elevated *Toxoplasma* antibodies in schizophrenia patients (approximately 1.9× the rate of controls); mechanism proposed involves dopamine/NMDA pathway disruption
- Causality in humans is not established; all human associations are correlational
