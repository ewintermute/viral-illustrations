# F8: The Wasp That Reprograms an Architect

*Category: Parasitic Manipulation*

## The Story

The orb-weaving spider *Cyclosa argenteoalba* is a skilled engineer. Each day it constructs a precise, radially symmetrical web — the classic spiral structure that has made orb weavers famous — and uses it to catch prey. It is a behavior encoded across millions of years of evolution, refined to a high degree of efficiency. And then a wasp shows up and rewrites the program entirely.

*Reclinervellus nielseni* is an ichneumonid parasitoid wasp with an unusual strategy for protecting its offspring. The female wasp finds a *Cyclosa* spider already engaged in normal life and lays an egg on the spider's abdomen. The wasp larva hatches and attaches to the outside of the spider, feeding on its hemolymph — blood — while the spider goes about its daily activities. For a time, nothing unusual happens. The spider builds its normal orb web. The wasp larva feeds quietly.

Then, about a week before the larva is ready to pupate, something changes. The spider's behavior begins to shift. Instead of building its usual prey-catching web, the spider starts constructing something different: a densely reinforced structure called a cocoon web. The cocoon web is built from much thicker silk threads than a normal web, with tightly packed spiral turns and heavily decorated stabilimenta — the extra silk structures that Cyclosa spiders typically use to camouflage themselves. In the cocoon web, these decorations are intensified, creating a dense platform of silk.

When the spider finishes the cocoon web, the wasp larva kills it, consumes the remains, and uses the silk structure as a platform to spin its own pupal cocoon. The cocoon web turns out to be almost perfectly optimized for protecting a wasp pupa: its reinforced silk withstands wind and rain far better than a normal orb web, and its thick stabilimenta provide camouflage for the cocoon hanging within it. Studies measuring tensile strength have confirmed that cocoon webs resist physical deformation significantly better than ordinary webs.

The manipulation is surgical in its timing. Until the critical week before pupation, the wasp larva allows the spider to behave normally — building normal prey-catching webs, catching food, maintaining its body. The spider's skills as a builder are preserved until they are needed. Only then does the parasite issue its override commands, presumably through chemical signals injected during feeding, redirecting the spider's behavior toward a single, specific construction task.

Researchers studying *R. nielseni* have shown that the cocoon web incorporates behavioral elements from the spider's normal repertoire — the same web-building movements, the same silk types — but assembled in a new configuration. The parasite does not install new behaviors. It repurposes existing ones, recombining the spider's behavioral modules into a structure the spider would never build on its own. It is less like programming from scratch and more like editing.

When the wasp larva dies experimentally before inducing the behavioral change, the spider returns to building normal orb webs — demonstrating that the manipulation is actively maintained by chemical signals, not a permanent alteration. The spider is not broken. It is temporarily running different software.

## Key Facts

- *Reclinervellus nielseni* wasp larvae attach to *Cyclosa argenteoalba* spiders and feed externally for about a week before killing their host
- The parasite induces the spider to build a "cocoon web" — a structurally reinforced, heavily decorated platform ideal for protecting a wasp pupa
- Cocoon webs use the same silk and movements as normal webs but recombine them into a new architecture the spider would never build independently
- The manipulation is chemically maintained: spiders return to normal web-building if the larva is removed before the behavioral change completes
- The cocoon web provides significantly better mechanical resistance to wind and rain than a normal orb web
