# A5: The Genome That Breaks the Rules

*Category: Genomics & Genetics*

## The Story

There's a quiet assumption built into how most people think about DNA: more complex organisms have more of it. Humans are sophisticated; bacteria are simple; therefore humans have more DNA. The logic seems sound. It is also, demonstrably, wrong.

In 2010, scientists at the Jodrell Laboratory at Kew Gardens announced a discovery that upended this assumption as cleanly as any finding in modern genomics. They had measured the genome of *Paris japonica*, a rare, delicate white-flowered plant native to the woodlands of Japan, and found it contained **152.23 picograms of DNA per cell** — corresponding to approximately **149–150 billion base pairs**. The human genome contains around 3 billion base pairs. *Paris japonica* has roughly **50 times more DNA** than a human being.

The measurement was made using flow cytometry — a technique that estimates genome size by staining DNA with a fluorescent dye and measuring the intensity of fluorescence, which scales with DNA quantity. The result was verified against known standards and published in the *Botanical Journal of the Linnean Society*. It was, at the time, the largest genome ever measured in any eukaryote.

To grasp the scale: if you unspooled the DNA from a single *Paris japonica* cell, the resulting strand would be more than **100 meters long**. The DNA in a single human cell, by comparison, stretches about 2 meters. A cell nucleus barely visible to the naked eye is packing more than 100 meters of genetic material.

The obvious question is: why? What does a woodland flower need with 50 times the genetic information of a human? The short answer is that it doesn't need it, at least not in any functional sense. The vast majority of the extra DNA is **non-coding** — sequences that do not encode proteins. This includes enormously repetitive stretches of DNA: satellite repeats, transposable elements, and other sequences that replicate and accumulate without obvious benefit. *Paris japonica* has roughly the same number of protein-coding genes as a human — perhaps 25,000–30,000. The difference is not in the genes. It's in everything surrounding them.

This is called the **C-value paradox** (or the **genome size paradox**): there is no consistent relationship between genome size and organismal complexity. Onions have more DNA than humans. Salamanders have more DNA than salamanders' ancestors. The lily family, to which *Paris japonica* belongs, tends toward very large genomes. The plant is also an **octoploid**, meaning its cells contain eight complete sets of chromosomes (2n = 8x = 40) rather than the human diploid two. Polyploidy — the duplication of entire chromosome sets — is a common route to genome expansion in plants.

Since 2010, the record has been updated. In 2024, a team from the Natural History Museum of Geneva and Kew Gardens announced that a small New Caledonian fern, *Tmesipteris oblanceolata*, had been found to carry a genome of **160.45 billion base pairs** — approximately 7% larger than *Paris japonica*'s — making it the current record holder and earning a Guinness World Record. This fern's DNA, uncoiled, would stretch over 105 meters per cell. The mechanisms driving these extreme genome expansions remain poorly understood. No one has yet identified what benefit, if any, this accumulation provides.

*Paris japonica* thus holds a more modest claim now: the largest genome of any **flowering plant** (angiosperm). But the broader point stands and is, if anything, more interesting for the fern discovery. Biology does not require complexity to accumulate information. DNA can expand, drift, and accumulate through processes that have nothing to do with adaptation or benefit — and a small woodland flower quietly carries more of it in each cell than almost any creature on Earth.

## Key Facts

- *Paris japonica*, a rare Japanese flowering plant, was measured in 2010 to have a genome of ~150 billion base pairs — approximately 50 times larger than the human genome.
- Its genome size reflects massive accumulation of **non-coding, repetitive DNA** rather than more genes; its protein-coding gene count is similar to humans.
- Uncoiled, the DNA in a single *Paris japonica* cell exceeds **100 meters** in length (vs. ~2 meters for a human cell).
- It is an **octoploid** (8 sets of chromosomes), and genome expansion via polyploidy and repetitive element accumulation is the likely driver.
- In 2024, the current overall record passed to *Tmesipteris oblanceolata*, a New Caledonian fern at 160.45 Gbp; *Paris japonica* retains the record among flowering plants.
