# H2: Treating Infection with Stool

*Category: The Microbiome*

## The Story

The idea of treating a patient by giving them someone else's feces sounds like a punchline. It is, in fact, one of the most effective therapies in modern medicine for one of medicine's most stubborn infections — and it took decades of resistance from the medical establishment before it was accepted.

*Clostridioides difficile*, better known as C. diff, causes a profoundly unpleasant and potentially fatal intestinal infection. It thrives when the normal gut microbiome is disrupted — typically by antibiotics — filling the ecological void with toxin-producing bacteria that cause severe diarrhea, inflammation, and in serious cases, life-threatening colitis. The standard treatment is more antibiotics, which is somewhat paradoxical: the disease arises from antibiotic-mediated disruption, and the treatment deploys more of the same tool. It works about 70% of the time for a first infection. For patients who relapse — and roughly 20–30% do — successive rounds of antibiotics yield diminishing returns, with some patients cycling through infections for years.

Fecal microbiota transplantation, or FMT, attacks the problem differently. Instead of killing C. diff with more antibiotics, FMT repopulates the patient's gut with a healthy donor's microbial community, which then outcompetes C. diff for resources and restores the ecological balance that prevents recurrence.

The cure rates are extraordinary. Multiple clinical trials and systematic reviews have documented 80–95% success rates for recurrent C. diff using FMT — compared to roughly 25–30% for standard antibiotic therapy in the same population. A pivotal Dutch randomized trial in 2013 comparing FMT to vancomycin in recurrent C. diff found such a large advantage for FMT (81% resolution vs. 31% with antibiotics) that the trial's independent data safety monitoring board stopped it early on ethical grounds — it would have been wrong to continue withholding FMT from the control group.

For two decades after those early successes, FMT occupied an awkward regulatory position in the United States. Stool is technically a biologic product under FDA regulations, which meant FMT was technically an unapproved drug. Gastroenterologists performed it anyway under enforcement discretion policies, typically using stool from screened family member donors. The process was messy, unstandarized, and occasionally risky — a 2019 safety incident in which two immunocompromised patients received FMT from a donor with drug-resistant bacteria resulted in one death, prompting the FDA to tighten oversight.

In November 2022 and again in April 2023, the FDA approved two standardized, commercially manufactured FMT products for recurrent C. diff — Rebyota (a rectally administered preparation) and Vowst (an oral capsule formulation). Both are derived from rigorously screened donor stool processed into pharmaceutical-grade products. The clinical trial data behind both approvals showed efficacy in the 70–90% range, and the standardized manufacturing resolved the major safety concerns around undiscreened donors.

The approvals opened the door to a different question: what else might FMT treat? The gut microbiome is now implicated in conditions ranging from inflammatory bowel disease to autism to cancer immunotherapy response. Clinical trials are underway for ulcerative colitis, melanoma treatment augmentation, and metabolic syndrome. FMT has become the thin end of a much larger wedge — the realization that many diseases may be partially microbiome diseases, and that treating the community rather than the pathogen is sometimes the more powerful intervention.

## Key Facts

- FMT achieves 80–95% cure rates in recurrent *C. difficile* infection, compared to roughly 25–30% for repeated antibiotic therapy
- A 2013 Dutch randomized trial was stopped early because FMT's advantage over vancomycin (81% vs. 31%) was too large to ethically continue withholding the treatment
- The FDA approved two commercial FMT products in 2022–2023 (Rebyota and Vowst), resolving a decades-long regulatory ambiguity
- FMT works by replacing the disrupted gut community with a healthy donor microbiome that outcompetes *C. difficile*
- Clinical trials are now exploring FMT for ulcerative colitis, cancer immunotherapy augmentation, and metabolic disease
