# C4: The Plague That Rewrote Us

*Category: Dangerous Biology*

## The Story

Between 1347 and 1353, the Black Death killed between 30% and 60% of Europe's population—somewhere between 25 and 50 million people in five years. No event in recorded human history has killed so large a fraction of a civilization so quickly. The bacterium responsible, *Yersinia pestis*, arrived from Central Asia via Silk Road trade routes, transmitted by the fleas of black rats and, under some conditions, directly through the air. By the time it burned through the continent, entire cities were depopulated. Entire family lines vanished.

The survivors carried something the dead did not: a genome that was better suited to fighting the plague. And we can now read the signature of that selection in the DNA of living Europeans.

In 2022, researchers at the University of Chicago and the University of Copenhagen published an analysis of ancient DNA extracted from pre-plague, during-plague, and post-plague burials in London, Denmark, and other European sites. The study examined immune gene variants in approximately 200 to 360 individuals across these time periods, looking for alleles that changed in frequency faster than genetic drift could explain. What they found was stark: a variant called rs2549794 in the gene *ERAP2* rose approximately 10 percentage points in frequency within just three to four human generations following the Black Death.

ERAP2 encodes a protein involved in trimming peptide fragments for presentation by MHC class I molecules—the surface proteins that allow immune cells to survey other cells for infection. The protective allele produces a version of ERAP2 that functions more effectively at recognizing and presenting *Yersinia pestis* peptides to macrophages. In laboratory experiments, human immune cells expressing the protective ERAP2 variant showed enhanced killing of *Y. pestis* bacteria. The researchers estimated that carrying two copies of the protective variant increased survival odds by approximately 40% compared to carrying none—one of the strongest selection coefficients ever measured in the human genome.

The evolutionary calculation this represents is sobering. A 40% survival advantage, applied across 60% population mortality, acting for five years: the signal is about what you'd expect to see. The Black Death was a natural experiment in selection, run at a scale and speed that human genetics rarely experiences. In less than a century—three to four generations—the European immune gene pool shifted measurably.

There is a cost. The ERAP2 variant that protected against plague appears to increase susceptibility to modern autoimmune diseases, including Crohn's disease. This is a classic evolutionary trade-off: a gene that was advantageous in the medieval plague environment became somewhat disadvantageous in a post-plague world where the immune threats changed. The same immune hypersensitivity that helped macrophages detect *Yersinia pestis* may, in a modern low-infection environment, direct immune activity against the body's own tissues.

The study's findings have been scrutinized and partially contested—a 2025 reanalysis pointed to statistical issues with the permutation tests used to confirm selection, and the debate about exactly which alleles were selected and by how much continues. But the broad conclusion—that the Black Death exerted measurable selective pressure on immune gene frequencies in European populations in a historically short time—is supported by the data and consistent with evolutionary theory.

We are, in part, the descendants of the immune survivors of a catastrophe that happened 675 years ago. Our immune systems were partly shaped by a bacterium that no longer routinely kills us—but that left its mark, for better and worse, in every European genome on Earth.

## Key Facts

- The Black Death (1347–1353) killed 30–60% of Europe's population; ancient DNA analysis confirms it left measurable selection signatures in immune gene frequencies
- The ERAP2 gene variant rs2549794 rose ~10 percentage points in frequency within 3–4 generations post-plague; carrying two copies increased survival odds by ~40%
- ERAP2 encodes a peptide-trimming enzyme; the protective allele enhances macrophage recognition and killing of *Yersinia pestis*
- The selection coefficient for the ERAP2 variant (~0.39) is among the strongest ever documented in the human genome
- The same protective ERAP2 allele is associated with increased risk of autoimmune conditions including Crohn's disease—a trade-off still visible in modern European populations
