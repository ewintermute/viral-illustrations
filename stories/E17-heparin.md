# E17: The Drug That Comes from Pigs and Has No Substitute

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

Every year, roughly 500 million doses of heparin are administered to patients worldwide. Heparin is the anticoagulant that prevents blood from clotting in dialysis machines, in catheters, in heart-lung bypass circuits, and in the veins and arteries of patients recovering from surgery or at risk of thromboembolism. It is one of the oldest drugs still in widespread use—discovered in 1916—and one of the most critically important. Without it, modern surgery and critical care medicine would not be possible in their current form.

Heparin is made from pig intestines. More precisely, it is extracted from the intestinal mucosa of pigs—the lining of the small intestine, which is particularly rich in the polysaccharide polymer that constitutes heparin. It takes roughly 1,500 to 2,000 individual pig intestines to produce one kilogram of heparin. There is, as of 2026, no approved synthetic substitute for pharmaceutical heparin. The world's supply of a critical anticoagulant depends entirely on slaughterhouse byproducts from a single species.

China's pig herds supply approximately 80% of the global heparin API (active pharmaceutical ingredient). This geographic concentration and biological dependency created the conditions for one of the most serious pharmaceutical disasters of the 21st century.

In late 2007, pig prices in China increased sharply due to a disease outbreak in the swine industry. Crude heparin prices spiked by over 100%. At some point in the supply chain—at a consolidation facility that was acquiring crude heparin from small, unregulated workshops extracting it from slaughterhouse mucosa—someone introduced a contaminant: oversulfated chondroitin sulfate (OSCS), a semi-synthetic compound that mimics heparin in standard purity tests. OSCS is cheap to produce and economically indistinguishable from genuine heparin using the quality control methods then in use.

The contaminated batches reached Baxter International, one of the largest heparin manufacturers in the United States. Between January and March 2008, reports began accumulating of patients experiencing severe allergic reactions—hypotension, nausea, shortness of breath, some progressing to anaphylaxis—shortly after receiving heparin. By the time the contamination was identified and massive recalls initiated, the FDA had attributed 81 deaths and 785 serious injuries in the United States to the adulterated product. Additional deaths were reported in Germany, Canada, Australia, Japan, and other countries.

The OSCS had evaded standard quality tests because it was chemically similar enough to heparin to produce false-positive results. New tests, including NMR spectroscopy and capillary electrophoresis, had to be developed and mandated. The FDA attempted to inspect the Chinese manufacturing facility and discovered that a naming confusion had led them to inspect the wrong building; the actual facility had never been inspected at all.

The crisis exposed the fragility of a supply chain in which a critical, life-saving, non-substitutable drug is entirely dependent on biological material from a specific species in a specific country, processed through largely unregulated networks of small workshops. African swine fever in 2018–2019 killed approximately 150 million pigs in China—about 40% of the country's pigs—and caused heparin price spikes, though no shortage reached patients. A pandemic, an outbreak, a supply disruption: these are the fault lines that run under every dialysis patient's treatment.

Scientists have been working for years on a fully synthetic heparin that could replace the pig-derived version. The chemistry is extraordinarily difficult—heparin is a complex, highly sulfated polysaccharide with biological activity dependent on specific structural features. As of 2026, no synthetic version has been approved. The pig, for now, remains indispensable.

## Key Facts

- Heparin is derived from pig intestinal mucosa; ~1,500–2,000 intestines per kilogram; no approved synthetic substitute exists
- China supplies ~80% of global heparin API, creating extreme supply chain concentration
- The 2008 heparin adulteration crisis: oversulfated chondroitin sulfate (OSCS) was introduced as a heparin mimic, causing 81 deaths and 785 serious injuries in the US alone
- The contaminant evaded standard quality tests; new NMR and capillary electrophoresis methods were mandated after the crisis
- African swine fever (2018–19) killed ~40% of China's pig population and caused heparin price volatility, demonstrating ongoing supply chain vulnerability
