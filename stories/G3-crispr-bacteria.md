# G3: Bacteria Invented Gene Editing. We Just Figured Out How to Steal It.

*Category: Origins & Identity*

## The Story

In 1987, a Japanese researcher named Yoshizumi Ishino was studying a gene in *E. coli* and noticed something strange in the flanking sequence: a series of short repeated DNA elements separated by spacers of non-repetitive sequence. He published it, noted it was unusual, and moved on. No one knew what it meant.

The structure was later named CRISPR: clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats. Over the next decade and a half, similar sequences were found in bacteria and archaea around the world. By the early 2000s, a Spanish microbiologist named Francisco Mojica noticed that the spacers between the repeats matched sequences from bacteriophages — viruses that infect bacteria. He proposed, in 2005, that CRISPR was a form of adaptive immunity: bacteria storing memories of past viral infections as a molecular record in their own DNA.

The proposal was initially rejected by several journals. It was eventually published, and it turned out to be correct.

When a bacterium survives a viral attack, it clips a fragment of the virus's DNA and inserts it into its CRISPR array as a new spacer. The next time that virus attacks, the bacterium transcribes its CRISPR array, producing guide RNAs that match the viral sequence. These guide RNAs direct a protein — Cas9, in the best-studied system from *Streptococcus pyogenes* — to find and cut the matching viral DNA. The virus is destroyed. The bacterium survives. The immune memory persists, heritable by the bacterium's descendants.

Bacteria had invented a programmable, sequence-specific DNA cutter. They'd been running it for hundreds of millions of years.

In 2011, Emmanuelle Charpentier, working at Umeå University in Sweden, identified a second RNA component of the system — tracrRNA — essential for the CRISPR-Cas9 machinery to function properly. She contacted Jennifer Doudna at UC Berkeley. Their collaboration, published in *Science* in June 2012, demonstrated that Cas9 could be reprogrammed in the lab: by designing a synthetic guide RNA, you could direct Cas9 to cut DNA at any sequence you chose. The bacterial scissors had been turned into a universal gene-editing tool.

The key simplification was fusing crRNA and tracrRNA into a single guide RNA — a design decision that made the system far easier to use than previous gene-editing technologies like zinc finger nucleases or TALENs. Within months, multiple labs had shown CRISPR-Cas9 worked in human cells. Within two years, it was being used in organisms across the tree of life.

Doudna and Charpentier received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2020. A parallel patent claim from Feng Zhang at the Broad Institute, who showed CRISPR working in eukaryotic cells, triggered one of the most consequential intellectual property disputes in the history of science — still not fully resolved. The stakes are enormous: CRISPR-based therapies for sickle cell disease, beta-thalassemia, and certain cancers are now FDA-approved or in late-stage trials. Agricultural applications are in commercial deployment. And the same tool that might cure genetic disease has already been used, controversially, to edit human germline embryos.

All of it traces back to a strange repetitive sequence in *E. coli* that nobody understood in 1987, and to bacteria that have been fighting viruses for a billion years.

## Key Facts

- CRISPR was first noticed in *E. coli* in 1987; its function as a bacterial immune system was proposed in 2005 by Francisco Mojica.
- The mechanism: bacteria store viral DNA fragments as spacers, then use guide RNAs to direct Cas9 to cut matching DNA on subsequent infections.
- Doudna and Charpentier's 2012 *Science* paper showed Cas9 could be reprogrammed with a synthetic guide RNA to cut any sequence.
- Doudna and Charpentier were awarded the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2020.
- CRISPR-based therapies for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia received FDA approval in late 2023.
