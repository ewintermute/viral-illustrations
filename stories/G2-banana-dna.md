# G2: You Share 60% of Your DNA With a Banana

*Category: Origins & Identity*

## The Story

You share 98.8% of your DNA with a chimpanzee. You share roughly 94% with a mouse. About 72% with a zebrafish. And somewhere around 25-60% with a banana, depending on precisely how you measure it.

The banana figure is the one that stops people cold. It is also the one that requires the most careful interpretation — because what it actually reveals is something more profound than a simple percentage suggests.

The commonly cited claim that humans share "more than 60% of DNA with bananas" typically refers to sequence similarity in protein-coding regions — the portions of the genome that encode proteins rather than the vast stretches of regulatory, repetitive, and non-coding sequence that makes up roughly 98% of the human genome. When researchers use a stricter analysis — identifying orthologous genes, meaning genes that genuinely descend from a common ancestral gene and perform equivalent functions — the figure drops to approximately 20-25% of genes shared.

But that number, far from being deflating, is actually the more interesting one. Because what those shared genes encode is the molecular machinery of being a eukaryotic cell. DNA replication. Cell cycle control. Cell division. The basic biochemistry of energy metabolism. These processes were solved, evolutionarily, before the lineage leading to animals and the lineage leading to plants diverged — about 1.5 billion years ago. And the solutions were good enough that both lineages have been running them, largely unchanged, ever since.

When researchers at the Dessimoz Lab at University College London analyzed human-banana gene orthologs in detail, they found the shared genes were overwhelmingly enriched for "housekeeping" functions: the metabolic processes that any eukaryotic cell needs to stay alive and divide. The machinery for making proteins. The machinery for packaging DNA. The machinery for managing energy. A banana and a human cell, at the level of these fundamental processes, are running nearly identical programs.

What distinguishes us — the roughly 75-80% of genes present in humans but not bananas, or present in forms too divergent to recognize as the same gene — encodes the specializations of animal life: nervous systems, immune responses, developmental programs, limb formation, sensory organs. The things that make a human a human rather than a plant are a fraction of the total genomic information required to simply be a living eukaryotic cell.

The DNA that makes humans different from bananas is a small modification layered on top of a vast shared platform.

The comparison with chimpanzees is telling in a different way. The 98.8% figure — or closer to 95-96% when accounting for insertions, deletions, and duplications across the whole genome rather than just coding sequence — reflects 6-7 million years of divergence from a common ancestor. Speciation, in genomic terms, is a surprisingly fine operation. The enormous biological differences between a human and a chimpanzee — language, abstract reasoning, technological culture, complex social organization — arise from changes in roughly 1-5% of the genome.

We are, beneath the surface, mostly the same as everything else. The specializations that define us are recent, narrow, and built on a foundation assembled over the first 1.5 billion years of eukaryotic life.

The banana shares that foundation. So does everything else alive.

## Key Facts

- Humans share ~98.8% DNA sequence similarity with chimpanzees (diverged ~6-7 million years ago).
- Humans share ~94% of genes with mice, ~72% with zebrafish.
- The "60% with bananas" figure refers to protein-coding regions; orthologous gene analysis puts true shared functional genes at ~20-25%.
- Shared human-banana genes encode fundamental eukaryotic cell biology: DNA replication, cell division, energy metabolism.
- Humans and bananas last shared a common ancestor ~1.5 billion years ago.
