# B5: The Animal That Cannot Die

*Category: Hidden Complexity*

## The Story

It is half a millimeter long, has eight stubby legs, and looks like a gummy bear designed by someone who had never seen a bear. It has survived every mass extinction in the history of animal life on Earth. It has been to space and come back. Under a microscope, it waddles through a film of water with an air of complete indifference to physics.

The tardigrade—water bear, moss piglet, take your pick of affectionate names—achieves its legendary indestructibility through a process called cryptobiosis. When conditions become hostile, the tardigrade does something no other animal can do with such completeness: it stops being alive in any conventional sense. It pulls its legs inside its body, expels nearly all its water until it contains as little as 1–3% of its normal moisture, and enters a state called the "tun." Metabolic activity drops to less than 0.01% of normal. The organism is not dead—but it is not meaningfully alive, either. It is suspended in a kind of molecular stasis that can last for decades.

Inside the tun, a suite of tardigrade-specific proteins takes over. Intrinsically disordered proteins (IDPs)—including families called CAHS, SAHS, and the now-famous Dsup—act as molecular scaffolding, replacing the water that normally cushions cellular structures and forming a protective glassy matrix around every sensitive molecule. DNA is coiled and bound, shielded from the reactive oxygen species that radiation and desiccation would otherwise generate. The whole cell becomes, essentially, a glass bead: rigid, inert, chemically stable.

In this state, tardigrades can survive conditions that would incinerate, freeze, crush, or irradiate anything else. They tolerate temperatures from -272°C (one degree above absolute zero) to over 150°C. They survive doses of ionizing radiation more than 500 times the human lethal threshold. In 2007, an experiment on the Russian FOTON-M3 spacecraft exposed dehydrated tardigrades to the raw vacuum and solar ultraviolet radiation of low Earth orbit for 10 days. They survived. Rehydrated, they walked away.

Cryptobiosis evolved independently in multiple tardigrade lineages, with fossil evidence suggesting the trait dates back 175 to 430 million years—well before the Permian extinction that wiped out 96% of marine species. By some analyses, tardigrades have now survived all five of the mass extinctions that define the boundaries of Earth's geological periods. Models published by Oxford University Press calculate that nothing short of an event that boils the oceans—a gamma-ray burst, an asteroid 10 times larger than the Chicxulub impactor, or the eventual expansion of the Sun—could reliably kill all of them. Tardigrades, the models conclude, are the most likely surviving animal when Earth's biosphere finally ends.

They are not immortal. Their active lifespan, when food and water are available, is just a few months to a couple of years. But they are extraordinarily patient. A tardigrade can dry out in a moss cushion on a rooftop for 30 years, be rehydrated, and resume walking, eating, and reproducing within hours. In 2016, a Japanese team revived a tardigrade that had been frozen in Antarctic moss collected in 1983—more than 30 years in the freezer.

Evolution produces specialists. The cheetah is fast. The deep-sea snailfish survives pressure that would crush a submarine. The tardigrade has specialized in one thing: not dying. It has become, across half a billion years of practice, extraordinarily good at it.

## Key Facts

- Tardigrades enter cryptobiosis by reducing body water to 1–3%, dropping metabolism to <0.01% of normal, and forming a glassy protective matrix around cellular structures
- In the 2007 FOTON-M3 mission, dehydrated tardigrades survived 10 days of open space exposure, including vacuum and solar UV
- Tardigrade-specific proteins (CAHS, SAHS, Dsup) physically shield DNA from radiation and oxidative damage, providing protection no known mammalian protein can match
- Tardigrades tolerate radiation doses >500x the human lethal threshold and temperatures from -272°C to over 150°C
- Models predict tardigrades would outlast all other animals in astrophysical catastrophe scenarios, surviving until the Sun's expansion boils Earth's oceans
