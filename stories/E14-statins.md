# E14: The Mold That Stopped Heart Attacks

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

In 1971, a Japanese biochemist at the pharmaceutical company Sankyo named Akira Endo set out to test a hypothesis that most of his colleagues considered unpromising. He reasoned that because fungi compete intensely with bacteria for nutrients, and because bacteria depend on the same cholesterol biosynthesis pathway as mammals, some fungus somewhere in nature must have evolved a compound that inhibits HMG-CoA reductase—the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis—as a chemical weapon. If such a compound existed, it would be, theoretically, a cholesterol-lowering drug.

Endo and his team screened more than 6,000 microbial cultures over two years. In 1973, from a sample of the blue-green mold *Penicillium citrinum*, they isolated a compound they called compactin (later known as mevastatin). It was a potent HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor in the laboratory—exactly what the hypothesis had predicted. In chickens and dogs, compactin lowered blood cholesterol dramatically. In rats, it did not—a mystery that would later be explained by differences in hepatic cholesterol metabolism between species.

Endo went to clinical trials in 1978. In patients with familial hypercholesterolemia—a genetic condition causing dangerously high LDL levels for which no effective treatment existed—compactin reduced LDL cholesterol by up to 30%. The results were medically significant. Then, in 1980, Sankyo halted development. Dogs given compactin at very high doses developed lymphomas. Regulatory caution prevailed, and the program stopped despite clinical promise.

Working independently at Merck, a team led by Alfred Alberts had been screening the same class of compounds and in 1979 isolated lovastatin from the fungus *Aspergillus terreus*. Merck characterized the compound, conducted their own toxicology, concluded that the lymphoma risk seen with compactin was dose-related and not present at therapeutic doses, and proceeded to clinical trials. In 1987, the FDA approved lovastatin (Mevacor) as the first commercially marketed statin. It was capable of reducing LDL cholesterol by 20–40% with a safety profile vastly better than any previous cholesterol-lowering agent.

The impact was enormous. Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death worldwide. Elevated LDL cholesterol is a major, modifiable risk factor for heart attack and stroke. Statins reduced that risk with a side effect profile that most patients tolerated without difficulty. Subsequent generations of more potent statins—simvastatin, pravastatin, atorvastatin, rosuvastatin—were developed. By the 2010s, statins were among the most prescribed drugs in the world. Atorvastatin (Lipitor) alone generated over $125 billion in cumulative sales—more than any other drug in pharmaceutical history.

Akira Endo, who never commercially benefited from his discovery (the patents belonged to Sankyo, and compactin was never approved), received the Lasker Award in 2008, often considered a near-Nobel Prize in medicine. He died in 2024, having spent his late career reflecting on the path from mold to medicine. Millions of people are alive because a Japanese researcher in 1971 had a hypothesis about fungi, competitive biochemistry, and cholesterol—and was willing to screen 6,000 cultures to test it.

## Key Facts

- Akira Endo discovered compactin (mevastatin) from *Penicillium citrinum* in 1973 after screening 6,000+ microbial cultures—the first statin, proving HMG-CoA reductase inhibition lowers cholesterol
- Lovastatin from *Aspergillus terreus* was isolated independently by Merck in 1979 and became the first FDA-approved statin (1987), reducing LDL by 20–40%
- Statin development was driven by fungal natural products; Endo's hypothesis was that fungi had evolved HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors as competitive weapons against bacteria
- Atorvastatin (Lipitor) generated over $125 billion in cumulative sales—more than any other pharmaceutical product in history
- Endo received the 2008 Lasker Award; he never commercially benefited from his discovery as the patents belonged to his employer Sankyo
