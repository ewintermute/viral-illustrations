# J11: Evolution's Spare Parts Get a Second Life

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

The palmaris longus is a thin, flat muscle that runs from the elbow to the wrist on the palm side of the forearm. In other primates — and in our ancestors — it contributed to gripping and climbing behavior, flexing the wrist and tensing the palmar fascia for a power grip on branches. In modern humans, it contributes almost nothing. The hand functions identically with or without it. When surgeons need to perform reconstructive surgery that requires a tendon graft — repairing a torn ligament, reconstructing a severed tendon, building a new thumb — the palmaris longus tendon is the first thing they take, precisely because removing it has no functional consequence.

Roughly 14% of people don't have a palmaris longus at all. The muscle is absent in one or both forearms, with no apparent reduction in hand strength, grip function, or manual dexterity. Studies comparing surgical outcomes in patients with and without the palmaris longus find no differences. The 86% of people who have it carry a functional evolutionary relic.

The palmaris longus is the most commonly absent vestigial structure in the human body, but it is far from the only one. The plantaris muscle in the leg — another vestigial climbing aid — is absent in roughly 8% of people. The auricular muscles, which in most mammals allow independent ear movement to direct hearing toward sounds, are present in nearly all humans but non-functional; the few people who can wiggle their ears do so through pathways that are themselves evolutionary holdovers. Goosebumps, produced by arrector pili muscles attached to hair follicles, are the vestige of a piloerection response that in furry ancestors raised the fur coat to trap heat or signal threat — in hairless humans, they produce only the familiar useless bumps.

What makes the palmaris longus an interesting evolutionary story is not its uselessness but its repurposing. The tendon's architecture — long, narrow, easily harvested without causing deficit at the donor site — makes it ideal for transplant applications. Orthopedic surgeons use it to reconstruct the anterior cruciate ligament, rebuild the flexor tendons of the hand, reconstruct the ulnar collateral ligament (the famous Tommy John surgery in baseball), and recreate the extensor mechanism of the thumb after injury. A structure retained by evolutionary inertia, unable to be fully eliminated because too many developmental and anatomical pathways are built around it, has been seconded to a medical role that never existed in its evolutionary context.

The broader category of vestigial structures in humans reveals something important about evolution: it does not design organisms from scratch. It modifies existing structures, leaves dead-end pathways partially intact, and creates bodies full of evolutionary sediment — remnants of solutions to problems that no longer exist, sometimes adapted to new purposes, sometimes quietly carried along for no particular reason.

The appendix, once considered purely vestigial, is now understood to serve a function in gut immune development and microbial reservoir maintenance. The coccyx — the human tailbone — is a vestige of the primate tail but now serves as an anchor for pelvic floor muscles. Evolution recycles everything. What looks like waste may be raw material.

## Key Facts

- The palmaris longus is absent in ~14% of people with no loss of hand function — a classic vestigial muscle with no current role in normal human anatomy
- Surgeons preferentially harvest the palmaris longus tendon for reconstructive procedures (ACL reconstruction, Tommy John surgery, thumb reconstruction) because removal has zero functional consequence
- Other vestigial human structures include the plantaris muscle (~8% absent), non-functional auricular muscles, and the arrector pili (goosebumps) response
- Vestigial structures persist because evolution eliminates only what has net negative fitness effects; neutral structures accumulate and are sometimes co-opted
- The appendix and coccyx — once considered purely vestigial — have known functional roles, demonstrating the danger of declaring any structure truly useless
