# E5: The Woman Whose Cells Changed Medicine

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

On February 8, 1951, a doctor at Johns Hopkins Hospital in Baltimore took a biopsy from a cervical tumor in a 31-year-old Black woman named Henrietta Lacks. The cells were delivered to researcher George Gey, who was running a laboratory tissue culture program and had spent years trying, without success, to keep human cells alive outside the body. Every previous culture had died within days. These cells did not die.

They divided. And they kept dividing. And they kept dividing again.

HeLa cells—named using the first two letters of Henrietta Lacks's name, though for decades her identity was obscured—turned out to be effectively immortal. Human cells normally have a finite replicative lifespan; after a certain number of divisions, they enter senescence and stop. HeLa cells bypassed this limit through a mutation that hyperactivates the enzyme telomerase, which rebuilds the protective telomere caps on chromosome ends that normally shorten with each division. They could be grown indefinitely in a laboratory flask, shipped to other institutions, and used for experiments that would have been impossible with any previous cell system.

The impact was immediate and enormous. Within months, Gey shared HeLa cells with Jonas Salk's laboratory, where they were used to test the polio vaccine. HeLa cells were sent to the first U.S. cell production facility, which manufactured them by the ton for vaccine testing. In the decades that followed, they were used in cancer research, genetics, virology, toxicology, and pharmacology. HeLa cells were aboard early space missions. They were used to develop techniques for freezing human cells, which underpins modern cell banking. They helped establish the role of the human papillomavirus (HPV) in cervical cancer—work that earned a Nobel Prize in 2008.

Henrietta Lacks never consented to any of this. She died on October 4, 1951, eight months after her diagnosis. Her family was never notified that her cells were in use. They were not compensated. They did not know, for more than two decades, that her cells existed—until a journalist began asking questions in the early 1970s. When members of her family were eventually approached for blood samples to help characterize the HeLa line, they were not told what the research was for and did not understand the implications.

The commercialization of HeLa cells proceeded in parallel with the family's ignorance. Companies sold HeLa cells. Researchers used them in thousands of studies, many producing commercially valuable results. The cells themselves were not covered by intellectual property law as a naturally occurring human biological material—a legal framework established in the 1990 California Supreme Court case *Moore v. Regents of the University of California*, which held that patients do not retain property rights over cells removed from their bodies for clinical purposes.

The HeLa case became the central example in bioethics debates about informed consent, the commercialization of biological materials, and the historical exploitation of Black patients in American medicine. Rebecca Skloot's 2010 book *The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks* brought the story to a wide audience. In 2023, the Lacks family reached a legal settlement with Thermo Fisher Scientific, which had been selling HeLa-derived products without consent—the first successful legal action of its kind.

The cells that could not die outlasted every institution that originally used them, and eventually forced a reckoning that is still unfolding.

## Key Facts

- HeLa cells, taken from Henrietta Lacks's cervical tumor in 1951, were the first human cells to survive and divide continuously in laboratory culture
- Their immortality results from hyperactive telomerase, which prevents the normal shortening of chromosomal telomeres that limits cell division
- HeLa cells were used to test the Salk polio vaccine (1952), study HPV-cancer links (Nobel Prize 2008), develop cell-freezing techniques, and in thousands of biomedical applications
- Lacks never consented; her family was not informed or compensated for decades; the commercialization of her cells proceeded without their knowledge
- The 2023 settlement between the Lacks family and Thermo Fisher Scientific was the first successful legal action over HeLa cell commercialization
