# H5: The Gut Bacteria That Decide If Chemotherapy Works

*Category: The Microbiome*

## The Story

Immune checkpoint inhibitors are among the most exciting developments in cancer treatment of the past two decades. By releasing the brakes the immune system normally places on T-cell activity, drugs like pembrolizumab, nivolumab, and ipilimumab can unleash a powerful anti-tumor immune response — in some patients, producing durable remissions in cancers that were previously untreatable. In metastatic melanoma, a disease with a historically grim prognosis, checkpoint inhibitor therapy now yields five-year survival rates above 50% in some cohorts.

The problem is that checkpoint inhibitors work in only a minority of patients. Roughly 40–60% of melanoma patients respond, and response rates for other cancers — pancreatic, colorectal, prostate — are often far lower. Oncologists have struggled to predict who will respond and why. Tumor mutational burden, PD-L1 expression, and other molecular markers provide some guidance, but they are imperfect. A key part of the answer, it turns out, may be in the gut.

The microbiome-immunotherapy connection was established through a pair of landmark studies published simultaneously in Science in 2018. One group, at MD Anderson Cancer Center, analyzed the gut microbiomes of metastatic melanoma patients before checkpoint inhibitor therapy. Patients whose microbiomes were enriched in certain bacteria — particularly *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* and related Ruminococcaceae — were significantly more likely to respond to treatment. Patients enriched in *Bacteroidales* species were significantly less likely to respond. A second group in Europe found complementary results, with *Bifidobacterium longum* enrichment associating with better responses to anti-PD-1 therapy.

Mechanistically, the connection runs through the immune microenvironment. Beneficial gut bacteria appear to stimulate systemic immune activation — producing SCFAs and other metabolites that enhance T-cell function, promoting differentiation of CD8+ cytotoxic T-cells, and reducing regulatory T-cell suppression within tumors. The gut microbiome shapes the immune system's baseline tone, and checkpoint inhibitors work best when that tone is already primed.

The most direct test of this hypothesis came from trials of FMT before checkpoint inhibitor therapy. In patients with metastatic melanoma who had failed prior immunotherapy, transplanting gut bacteria from immunotherapy responders produced remarkable results: several patients who had previously not responded to checkpoint inhibitors converted to responders after FMT from super-responder donors. The 2021 trials, published in Science, demonstrated that this microbiome transfer could rescue non-responders — essentially transferring immunotherapy susceptibility from one human gut to another.

For gastrointestinal cancers, results from 2024 trials suggest FMT combined with checkpoint inhibitors can overcome treatment resistance in previously refractory patients. The approach is still experimental but is moving through clinical trials across cancer types.

The implication is profound: a patient's cancer treatment outcome may depend significantly on the community of bacteria living in their intestine. Antibiotics given before checkpoint inhibitor therapy — a common scenario, since cancer patients frequently receive prophylactic or therapeutic antibiotics — have been associated with significantly worse outcomes in multiple retrospective studies. The bacteria destroyed by those antibiotics may have been a key part of the treatment infrastructure.

## Key Facts

- Gut microbiome composition before treatment strongly predicts response to immune checkpoint inhibitors in melanoma and other cancers
- Enrichment of *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* and Ruminococcaceae species associates with better response; *Bacteroidales* enrichment associates with non-response
- Fecal transplant from immunotherapy "super-responders" converted non-responders to responders in 2021 clinical trials
- Gut bacteria enhance immunotherapy by promoting T-cell activation and reducing immunosuppressive signaling within tumors
- Antibiotic use before checkpoint inhibitor therapy is associated with significantly worse outcomes in retrospective studies
