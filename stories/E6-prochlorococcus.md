# E6: The Microbe That Runs the Ocean

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

In 1986, Sallie Chisholm and Robert Olson were running a flow cytometer—an instrument that measures cells passing through a laser beam—to survey ocean microbial populations in the Sargasso Sea. Flow cytometers had been used to count marine bacteria before, but the researchers noticed an anomalous signal: a population of particles with a distinctive fluorescent signature, smaller than any known photosynthetic organism, occurring at densities nobody had expected to find in what were supposed to be nutrient-poor, biologically sparse waters.

The particles were alive. The first culture was isolated in 1988 from that original Sargasso Sea sample. The organism was named *Prochlorococcus*, from the Greek for "first" and "round berry"—a reference to its extraordinarily small size (0.5–0.7 micrometers) and its phylogenetic relationship to ancestral cyanobacteria. The discoverers had found the most abundant photosynthetic organism on Earth, one that had been invisible to science until flow cytometry made it detectable.

*Prochlorococcus* is remarkable in almost every dimension. A single milliliter of ocean surface water typically contains 100,000 to 200,000 cells in tropical and subtropical regions. The global population is estimated at roughly 10^27—more than a billion times the number of grains of sand on Earth. The cells are tiny enough that their photosynthetic chlorophyll uses a structurally unusual form: divinyl chlorophyll *a* and *b* instead of the monovinyl forms found in plants and most other cyanobacteria. This allows them to capture the blue wavelengths of light that penetrate deepest into the ocean, enabling survival at depths down to 135 meters—far below where most other photosynthetic organisms can function.

The contribution of *Prochlorococcus* to global oxygen production is substantial. Estimates vary with methodology and ocean models, but the organism is credited with producing approximately 20% of the oxygen in Earth's atmosphere—one in five breaths you take is, in some sense, provided by *Prochlorococcus*. The carbon it fixes amounts to roughly 4 gigatons annually. These numbers are staggering for an organism that was entirely unknown to science as recently as 1986.

Ecologically, *Prochlorococcus* populations are not monolithic. The genus contains a series of ecotypes—genetically distinct populations adapted to different light intensities and nutrient regimes. High-light adapted strains dominate the surface ocean; low-light adapted strains populate the depth where direct sunlight rarely reaches. The genomic diversity within the genus is remarkable—different ecotypes differ substantially in gene content, a form of distributed genetic diversity that may help the population as a whole respond to environmental variability.

This matters enormously for the future. As ocean surface temperatures rise, tropical regions expand, and nutrient cycling changes, *Prochlorococcus* populations in the warmest waters face potentially lethal conditions. A 2025 MIT study found that above approximately 30°C (86°F), Prochlorococcus growth rates showed a nearly threefold reduction and cell abundances halved. Projections based on current warming trajectories suggest populations in the tropical ocean could decline by up to 51% by 2100. The atmosphere-ocean carbon and oxygen exchange system—the one that has quietly kept the planet habitable for at least the last few hundred million years—is partly run by an organism discovered 40 years ago in a routine oceanographic survey.

## Key Facts

- *Prochlorococcus* was discovered in 1986 by Sallie Chisholm and colleagues using flow cytometry; first culture isolated 1988—it had been undetectable with prior methods
- It is the most abundant photosynthetic organism on Earth, with estimated global population ~10^27 cells, occurring at densities of 100,000–200,000 per milliliter in tropical surface waters
- *Prochlorococcus* is estimated to produce ~20% of Earth's atmospheric oxygen and fix ~4 gigatons of carbon annually
- Its unique divinyl chlorophyll captures blue light, enabling photosynthesis at depths down to 135 meters
- Warming oceans threaten the organism: populations show near-threefold growth rate reductions above 30°C; tropical populations could decline 51% by 2100
