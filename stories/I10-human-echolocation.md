# I10: Seeing With Sound

*Category: Extraordinary Senses*

## The Story

Daniel Kish lost both eyes to retinal cancer before his first birthday. He learned to navigate the world by making sharp clicking sounds with his tongue and listening to the echoes. By his teens, he was riding a bike through city streets. He has since taught the technique, which he calls FlashSonar, to hundreds of blind individuals worldwide, and he has been the subject of scientific studies that revealed something extraordinary about what happens to the human brain when it learns to see with sound.

When Daniel makes a click, the sound travels outward, reflects off surfaces — walls, parked cars, doorways, trees — and returns to his ears. The returned echoes carry information about the distance, size, and material of the surfaces they reflected from. This information is, in principle, there for any brain to process. The question researchers asked was whether blind echolocators were actually processing it visually — using the visual cortex — or through some other pathway.

The answer, provided definitively by fMRI studies at the University of Western Ontario and subsequently replicated by multiple groups, is that blind echolocators activate the primary visual cortex (V1) when processing echolocation clicks. The same regions of the brain that process visual edges, distances, and shapes in sighted people process acoustic echo information in blind echolocators — the brain has repurposed visual processing machinery for a different sensory modality.

The reorganization is extensive. Different regions within the visual cortex in blind echolocators respond to different aspects of the acoustic scene — near vs. far echoes activate different cortical zones, paralleling the retinotopic organization (near vs. far in the visual field) found in sighted people. The spatial organization of processing is preserved; only the input modality has changed.

This cortical repurposing is not an arbitrary outcome. Cross-modal plasticity — the ability of sensory deprivation in one modality to trigger colonization of that cortex by other modalities — is a general feature of mammalian brain development. The visual cortex appears to preferentially take on spatial processing tasks regardless of the input modality: deafblind individuals show tactile Braille reading activating visual cortex; deaf individuals show sign language activating auditory areas. The cortex cares about computation type, not input format.

The implications for rehabilitation are significant. Kish's organization, World Access for the Blind, has demonstrated that FlashSonar can be taught to blind individuals across a wide age range, with experienced users able to identify object size, shape, distance, and in some cases material from echoes alone. Trained echolocators can navigate unfamiliar rooms, identify objects on a table, and in demonstration contexts, ride bicycles — tasks previously thought to require vision.

Sighted people can also learn basic echolocation. Studies have shown that sighted individuals trained with blindfolds can learn to detect the presence of walls and large objects using tongue clicks within a few weeks of practice, though the performance of congenitally blind echolocators trained from childhood remains superior. The visual cortex's co-option of echolocation processing appears most extensive in individuals with early-onset blindness, though later-blinded individuals show the effect as well.

## Key Facts

- Blind echolocators like Daniel Kish activate the primary visual cortex (V1) when processing echolocation click echoes, demonstrated by fMRI
- The visual cortex's spatial organization is preserved: near and far echoes activate different cortical zones mirroring retinotopic organization
- Cross-modal plasticity drives this repurposing — the visual cortex preferentially takes on spatial computation regardless of input modality
- Experienced echolocators can identify object size, shape, distance, and material from echoes; some navigate city streets and ride bicycles
- Sighted people can learn basic echolocation; performance is highest in congenitally blind individuals trained from childhood
