# D6: The Merger That Made Complex Life

*Category: Evolutionary Marvels*

## The Story

About 2 billion years ago, something happened in the history of life that happened, as far as we can tell, exactly once. A cell engulfed another cell and, instead of digesting it, formed a permanent partnership. The engulfed bacterium kept its own genome, kept dividing on its own schedule, kept generating energy through oxidative phosphorylation—but now it did so inside a host cell, sheltered and provisioned. Over hundreds of millions of years, the two organisms became one. Every mitochondrion in every cell of your body is the descendant of that original bacterial guest.

This is the theory of endosymbiosis, and it was articulated in modern form by Lynn Margulis in her 1967 paper "On the Origin of Mitosing Cells," published in the *Journal of Theoretical Biology* after being rejected by roughly 15 other scientific journals. Margulis argued that eukaryotic cells—cells with nuclei and membrane-bound organelles—arose through symbiotic mergers between distinct prokaryotes, not through gradual modification of a single ancestral cell. Her hypothesis was considered heterodox, even eccentric. It became the consensus of modern biology when genomic sequencing in the 1970s and 1980s confirmed every prediction.

The evidence is now overwhelming. Mitochondria have their own circular DNA, distinct from the cell's nuclear chromosomes, encoding a small set of proteins. That DNA falls, in phylogenetic analyses, within the alpha-proteobacteria—a group of bacteria that includes today's *Rickettsia* and other intracellular organisms. Mitochondria have two membranes: an outer one derived from the host cell's engulfment vesicle, and an inner one that is the remnant of the original bacterial membrane. They replicate by binary fission, like bacteria, rather than by the mitosis used by the nucleus. Their ribosomes are bacterial in character, not eukaryotic—they respond to the same antibiotics that kill bacteria and are resistant to the drugs that block eukaryotic ribosomes.

Over the ~2 billion years since the original engulfment, massive gene transfer has occurred. The mitochondrial genome has shrunk dramatically as genes were transferred to the nucleus: in human mitochondria, only 13 protein-coding genes remain in the mitochondrial DNA, down from the thousands present in the ancestral bacterium. The nucleus now encodes most of the proteins that are imported back into the mitochondrion to function there. The two genomes have become deeply intertwined.

Why did this merger produce complex life? The current leading hypothesis, developed by evolutionary biologist Nick Lane and others, is energetic. Eukaryotic cells have mitochondria to generate enormous amounts of ATP—up to 18 times more energy per gene than bacteria can produce. This energy windfall liberated the nucleus to encode a vastly larger and more complex genome, supporting the protein diversity needed for multicellularity, specialized tissues, and large complex organisms. Bacteria stayed small and simple not because they lacked evolutionary opportunity but because they lacked the energy to do otherwise.

The merger was singular. All eukaryotes—every plant, animal, fungus, and protist—trace back to the same ancestral engulfment event. No other successful eukaryote has ever been demonstrated to have arisen independently from a second mitochondrial merger. The accident that made complex life happen appears to have happened once, in one cell, in one moment ~2 billion years ago.

## Key Facts

- Mitochondria are the descendants of alpha-proteobacteria engulfed by an archaeal host cell approximately 2 billion years ago—evidenced by their circular DNA, bacterial-type ribosomes, binary fission replication, and double membranes
- Lynn Margulis published the modern endosymbiosis hypothesis in 1967 after ~15 journal rejections; genomic sequencing confirmed it within 15 years
- Over 2 billion years, most bacterial genes transferred to the host nucleus; only 13 protein-coding genes remain in human mitochondrial DNA
- The energy gain from mitochondria (~18× more ATP per gene than bacteria) may have been the key that unlocked the genomic complexity required for multicellular life
- The mitochondrial merger appears to have occurred exactly once in the history of life—all eukaryotes descend from the same ancestral event
