# J1: The People Who Feel No Pain

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

In 2006, a six-year-old boy in Pakistan became briefly famous in medical literature. He performed street shows walking on hot coals and stabbing his arms with knives. He felt nothing. He had never felt pain in his life. He died shortly before his fourteenth birthday from injuries sustained after jumping from a roof — not from the jump itself, but from untreated complications that a person without his condition would have been warned of by pain long before they became fatal.

His condition, congenital insensitivity to pain, was eventually traced to a mutation in the gene *SCN9A*, which encodes a protein called Nav1.7 — a voltage-gated sodium channel found in high concentrations in the peripheral pain-sensing neurons called nociceptors. Nav1.7 is the molecular switch that allows nociceptors to fire action potentials in response to tissue damage. Without functional Nav1.7, nociceptors cannot generate the electrical signals that the brain interprets as pain. The person is otherwise normal: normal intelligence, normal motor function, normal touch and temperature sensitivity in most ranges. They simply cannot be hurt in the conventional sense.

The Nav1.7 story has two sides. Loss-of-function mutations cause the complete absence of pain. Gain-of-function mutations in the same gene cause the opposite: devastating chronic pain syndromes, including inherited erythromelalgia, where patients experience burning pain in the extremities severe enough to be completely disabling. The sodium channel is a rheostat for pain, and the two ends of the spectrum are equally well represented in human genetics.

The pharmaceutical interest in Nav1.7 is enormous. Chronic pain affects roughly 20% of adults in developed countries and is one of the most expensive and difficult-to-treat medical problems. Current analgesics — opioids in particular — are effective but carry devastating addiction and overdose risks. A drug that specifically blocked Nav1.7 in peripheral nociceptors would, in theory, provide pain relief without central nervous system effects, since Nav1.7 loss-of-function humans are otherwise healthy and non-addicted.

The obstacles to translation have been significant. Nav1.7 belongs to a family of nine sodium channels (Nav1.1–Nav1.9), several of which are expressed in the heart and nervous system. Early Nav1.7 inhibitors caused cardiac arrhythmias because they also blocked closely related channels. Developing selectivity sufficient to target Nav1.7 while sparing Nav1.4 (muscle) and Nav1.5 (cardiac) has required structural biology approaches — solving the crystal structure of the channel to identify binding sites accessible only to Nav1.7 inhibitors.

Clinical trials of selective Nav1.7 inhibitors are ongoing as of 2025. Several compounds have advanced through phase I and II trials for conditions including erythromelalgia and postherpetic neuralgia (shingles pain). The results are promising but not yet conclusive. The human genetics is unambiguous — Nav1.7 controls pain perception in people — but translating that genetic fact into a drug requires solving a selectivity problem that has so far eluded complete resolution.

The Pakistani boy who felt no pain paid the ultimate price for his condition: without pain as a guardian, injuries that should have been warnings became lethal. The goal of Nav1.7 pharmacology is to take only the volume down, not silence the alarm entirely.

## Key Facts

- Loss-of-function mutations in *SCN9A* (Nav1.7) cause complete congenital insensitivity to pain while preserving other sensations and normal intelligence
- Gain-of-function mutations in the same gene cause severe chronic pain syndromes, including inherited erythromelalgia
- Nav1.7 is expressed specifically in peripheral nociceptors, making it a theoretically ideal drug target for pain relief without central nervous system side effects
- Early Nav1.7 inhibitors failed due to cross-reactivity with cardiac and muscle sodium channels; structural biology is guiding development of selective compounds
- Phase I/II clinical trials of selective Nav1.7 inhibitors are underway as of 2025 for conditions including erythromelalgia and postherpetic neuralgia
