# E21: The Man Who Saved a Billion Lives

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

In 1944, a young plant pathologist from Iowa named Norman Borlaug arrived in Mexico with a mission from the Rockefeller Foundation: to help develop new wheat varieties that could protect Mexican farmers from a devastating stem rust disease. Nobody, at the time, could have predicted that this modest agricultural mission would evolve into a program that saved more human lives than any other project in the twentieth century.

The problem Borlaug faced was not just disease. It was also yield. Traditional wheat varieties were tall—waist-high or higher—and when grown with enough fertilizer to produce heavy grain heads, they toppled under their own weight in a process called lodging. A lodged wheat crop cannot be harvested mechanically and loses most of its yield. More fertilizer meant taller plants that fell over; agronomists were stuck in a loop. The breakthrough came from a dwarf wheat variety developed in Japan—Norin 10—that carried genetic alleles for shorter, stiffer stems. Borlaug crossed Norin 10 dwarfing genes into high-yielding Mexican wheat varieties, spent years developing stable, rust-resistant semi-dwarf lines, and by the early 1960s had varieties that could stand up under heavy grain loads and respond to fertilizer with dramatically increased yield rather than with stem elongation.

By 1963, 95% of Mexican wheat was planted with Borlaug's semi-dwarf varieties. Mexico had been a net importer of wheat in 1944; it became self-sufficient in 1956 and a net exporter shortly after. Wheat production had multiplied sixfold.

Then came India and Pakistan. The mid-1960s brought catastrophic monsoon failures and looming famine. In 1965, against resistance from domestic agricultural bureaucracies, Borlaug arranged for 35 tons of Mexican semi-dwarf wheat seed to be shipped to South Asia. The results were historic: Indian wheat harvests rose 60% within a few years. By 1968, Pakistan had achieved self-sufficiency in wheat. The term "Green Revolution" was coined to describe the transformation. Global grain production more than doubled between 1950 and 1992.

Borlaug received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1970. In his Nobel lecture, he was clear about the stakes and the limits: the Green Revolution had provided a "breathing space" rather than a permanent solution, a respite from famine that could be extended only if population growth slowed and agricultural technology continued to advance. He was right on both counts. The Green Revolution required massive inputs of synthetic nitrogen fertilizer (Haber-Bosch process) and irrigation water. It generated environmental concerns about intensive agriculture, soil degradation, and water table depletion that persist today.

But the arithmetic of the 20th century is hard to argue with. An estimated 1 billion people who might have starved in famines during the latter half of the century did not. Malthusian predictions of mass starvation failed to materialize, partly because of improved governance and food distribution, partly because of slower population growth than feared, and substantially because a plant pathologist from Iowa and a Japanese dwarf wheat variety combined in Mexico to produce plants that could, reliably and abundantly, make grain.

## Key Facts

- Borlaug crossed the Japanese Norin 10 dwarfing genes into Mexican wheat varieties, creating semi-dwarf, high-yield, disease-resistant strains that could respond to fertilizer without lodging
- Mexican wheat production multiplied sixfold 1944–1963; Mexico became wheat self-sufficient by 1956
- Indian wheat harvests rose 60% within years of introducing semi-dwarf varieties in 1965–1966; Pakistan achieved wheat self-sufficiency by 1968
- Borlaug received the Nobel Peace Prize in 1970; his work is credited with preventing famine for an estimated 1 billion people
- The Green Revolution required synthetic nitrogen fertilizers and intensive irrigation—benefits accompanied by environmental costs still being managed today
