# I6: The Animal with 16 Colors That Can't See Colors

*Category: Extraordinary Senses*

## The Story

The mantis shrimp has become one of biology's most retold stories: 16 types of photoreceptors, compared to the three found in human eyes, suggesting an almost incomprehensible richness of color experience. Popular accounts describe a creature swimming through a watercolor explosion of perception, experiencing colors no human can imagine. The actual science is stranger and more interesting than that.

Mantis shrimp are genuinely extraordinary visual organisms. Their compound eyes, mounted on independently moving stalks, can see ultraviolet and infrared light invisible to humans, detect circular polarized light — a feat no other animal is known to achieve — and process the spatial world through 16 photoreceptor types. The visual processing power is remarkable. But the color discrimination story has a twist: mantis shrimp are actually poor at color discrimination.

When behavioral experiments were designed to test how well mantis shrimp could tell different colors apart — not just detect them, but distinguish between nearby wavelengths — the results were startling. In 2014, a study by Hanne Thoen and colleagues at the University of Queensland measured mantis shrimp color discrimination thresholds. Human subjects can distinguish wavelength differences of about 1–5 nanometers in the visible range. Mantis shrimp, with 16 receptor types, could only reliably discriminate wavelengths 12–25 nanometers apart. They were performing far worse at the fine-grained discrimination task that humans excel at.

The explanation has to do with how the receptors are used. In human vision, color discrimination works through opponent processing: the brain compares the relative stimulation of three receptor types and extracts color information from the ratios. This comparison process, which happens in neural circuits behind the retina and in the visual cortex, enables extremely fine-grained wavelength discrimination from just three receptor types. Mantis shrimp do not appear to use this comparative strategy. Instead, each photoreceptor channel operates as an independent threshold detector — signaling simply whether its specific wavelength range is present or absent, without comparing against other channels.

This "spectral sampling" strategy trades discrimination fineness for processing speed. A mantis shrimp's visual system can make a color categorization decision in the time it takes to process a single spike from each receptor channel — essentially one neural processing step. A human visual system requires multiple comparisons and takes proportionally longer. For a mantis shrimp, which hunts by rapid-strike and makes prey-versus-not-prey decisions in milliseconds, speed matters more than color sophistication.

The 16 receptor types also serve non-discriminative functions. They provide coverage across a wide spectral range including UV, so the mantis shrimp can detect signals encoded in UV patterns on other shrimp — UV-reflective patches used in communication and mate assessment — without needing to discriminate UV shades finely. The system is designed for categorization across many spectral bands, not for fine discrimination within any of them.

It is, in short, a completely different solution to color vision: not a richer version of human color perception, but an orthogonal architecture designed for speed over precision.

## Key Facts

- Mantis shrimp have 16 photoreceptor types including UV and IR detectors, plus the ability to detect circular polarized light
- Despite 16 receptor types, mantis shrimp color discrimination threshold is 12–25 nm — compared to 1–5 nm for humans
- Each receptor channel works as an independent threshold detector, not through comparative opponent processing — enabling fast categorization rather than fine discrimination
- The system is optimized for speed: single-step categorization across 16 spectral bins rather than the multi-step comparison processing of human color vision
- UV-reflective body patches used for intraspecific communication can be detected and categorized without fine UV discrimination
