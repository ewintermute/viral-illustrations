# F3: You Are 8% Virus

*Category: Stranger Than Fiction*

## The Story

The standard story of inheritance is vertical: genes flow from parents to offspring, down through the generations. Mutations accumulate slowly, over millions of years, creating the gradual divergence of lineages that we trace in phylogenetic trees. This is the model that Darwin described, Mendel quantified, and Watson and Crick gave a molecular basis. It is true, and it is essential—and it is incomplete.

Genes also move horizontally. They jump sideways between organisms, sometimes between organisms separated by enormous evolutionary distances, bypassing the vertical tree entirely. In bacteria, horizontal gene transfer (HGT) is a dominant force in evolution: bacteria exchange plasmids carrying antibiotic resistance genes, metabolic pathways, and virulence factors at rates that make the inheritance model look quaint. But HGT is not limited to prokaryotes. It has shaped eukaryotic genomes too, including your own.

Approximately 8% of the human genome consists of endogenous retroviruses (ERVs)—sequences derived from retroviruses that infected the germline of our ancestors and were permanently incorporated. Retroviruses replicate by reverse-transcribing their RNA genome into DNA and inserting it into the host cell's chromosomes. When this happens in a germline cell—a cell that gives rise to eggs or sperm—the insertion becomes heritable. Over evolutionary time, the retroviral sequence is passed on like any other gene. The virus becomes, in a very real sense, part of the organism.

These integrations happened repeatedly throughout primate evolution. Some are ancient, shared across all mammals; others are specific to primates; some are found only in humans and our closest relatives. They are detectable by their characteristic retroviral structure—long terminal repeats flanking coding sequences—and by their presence at orthologous positions in related species. When two species share a retroviral integration at the same chromosomal location, it demonstrates that a retrovirus infected a common ancestor. The shared ERV becomes a molecular fossil of ancient viral infection.

What do these 8% of viral-derived sequences do in the human genome? For most of the time since their insertion, they accumulated mutations that disrupted their original function. Most ERV sequences are now degenerate—they cannot produce infectious virus or even functional proteins. But natural selection does not ignore 8% of a genome indefinitely. Some ERV sequences have been co-opted for host functions. The syncytins—proteins essential for the formation of the placental syncytiotrophoblast (the cell layer that fuses to form the maternal-fetal barrier in placental mammals)—are derived from the envelope proteins of ancient retroviruses. Every mammal that forms a placenta uses a co-opted viral protein to do it. The evolutionary origin of placental reproduction has a viral component.

Other co-opted ERV sequences function as gene regulatory elements, enhancers, and insulators—shaping the expression of nearby genes. Some are transcribed into non-coding RNAs with regulatory functions. A few may protect against new retroviral infections by occupying cellular receptors.

Beyond ERVs, horizontal gene transfer from bacteria to eukaryotes has been documented in multiple lineages. Bdelloid rotifers—a class of tiny freshwater invertebrates—have acquired hundreds of bacterial genes through HGT. Some plant immune genes appear to have bacterial origins. The picture that emerges from modern genomics is not a clean phylogenetic tree but a tangled graph, with genetic material moving not just downward through generations but sideways across the tree of life, mediated primarily by viruses.

You are not a pure product of your evolutionary lineage. You are, among other things, the residue of ancient viral infections—the fossils of encounters between your ancestors and pathogens that shaped your genome and persist in every cell of your body.

## Key Facts

- ~8% of the human genome consists of endogenous retroviruses (ERVs)—sequences from retroviruses that infected ancestral germline cells and became heritable
- ERVs are identified by their retroviral structure and by shared insertions at orthologous chromosomal positions in related species—molecular fossils of ancient viral infections
- Syncytin proteins, essential for placental formation in all placental mammals, are co-opted retroviral envelope proteins—viral sequences are required for mammalian reproduction
- Other co-opted ERV sequences function as gene regulatory elements (enhancers, insulators) and non-coding RNAs
- Horizontal gene transfer from bacteria to eukaryotes is also documented; bdelloid rotifers carry hundreds of bacterial genes acquired by HGT
