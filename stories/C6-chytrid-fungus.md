# C6: The Extinction Pathogen

*Category: Dangerous Biology*

## The Story

The International Union for Conservation of Nature maintains a list of the most destructive invasive species in history. Near the top sits a fungus that most people have never heard of, infecting a class of animals that most people rarely think about. *Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis*—Bd, or the chytrid fungus—has caused more vertebrate species extinctions than any other pathogen in recorded history. By any measure, it is the most catastrophic wildlife disease outbreak known to science.

Bd is a water mold from the chytrid division of fungi, an ancient lineage that mostly decomposes organic matter in aquatic environments. Something about its evolutionary path equipped it to infect the keratin-containing skin cells of amphibians with deadly efficiency. The fungus spreads as free-swimming zoospores—microscopic, flagellated reproductive cells that can survive in water for weeks. When they encounter an amphibian, they encyst in the outer layers of skin, germinate, and establish an infection that spreads across the entire surface of the animal.

Amphibian skin is not merely a protective layer. It is an organ of gas exchange and, in many species, of osmotic regulation—the mechanism by which animals maintain appropriate salt and water balance. Bd disrupts this function by thickening the keratinized skin layers it colonizes, impairing the ion transport proteins embedded in the skin. The result is a slow electrolyte imbalance. Sodium and potassium levels in the blood decline. The heart arrhythmias caused by this imbalance, combined with immune disruption, lead to cardiac arrest and death. The animal essentially suffocates and starves chemically through its own skin.

The scale of the damage is staggering. A global assessment published in *Science* in 2019 found that Bd had caused the decline of at least 500 amphibian species and the extinction of 90. Eighty percent of all amphibian extinctions recorded since the 1980s have been attributed to Bd. The pathogen has been detected in 55% of nearly 2,500 sampled species and in 69% of 134 sampled countries. It is genuinely global. In some frog communities in Panama, the Andes, Australia, and California, populations that had been stable for centuries or millennia were functionally extinct within years of Bd's arrival.

The ecological consequences extend beyond the amphibians themselves. Frogs, salamanders, and caecilians occupy a central position in most terrestrial and freshwater food webs. They consume enormous quantities of invertebrates—controlling insect populations—and are in turn consumed by birds, snakes, mammals, and fish. Where Bd has caused mass die-offs, studies have documented cascading effects: changed vegetation structure from reduced herbivorous insect control, declining populations of species that feed on amphibians, altered nutrient cycling in streams where decomposing amphibian larvae previously processed organic matter.

The fungus is thought to have originated in Asia, where native amphibian species appear to have evolved some tolerance to it. Its global spread was enabled primarily by the international trade in live amphibians: laboratory frogs (especially the African clawed frog *Xenopus laevis*, formerly used in pregnancy testing), pet trade animals, and food trade all moved Bd around the world, often faster than wildlife health authorities could track it.

Control has proved nearly impossible in wild populations. There is no effective environmental treatment that kills Bd without destroying the aquatic ecosystems amphibians need. Captive breeding programs have preserved some species on the edge of extinction. Research into probiotic skin bacteria—particularly *Janthinobacterium lividum*, which produces compounds inhibitory to Bd—offers some hope for reintroduction efforts. But the 90 extinctions are permanent. The ecological communities that evolved around those species have changed permanently. The fungus that nobody saw coming rewrote the vertebrate extinction record in a generation.

## Key Facts

- *Batrachochytrium dendrobatidis* (Bd) has caused the decline of 500+ amphibian species and extinction of at least 90—more species extinctions than any other pathogen in recorded history
- Bd kills by colonizing amphibian skin, thickening keratin layers, and disrupting ion transport, leading to electrolyte imbalance and cardiac arrest
- 80% of all amphibian extinctions since the 1980s are attributed to Bd
- The fungus spread globally through international trade in live amphibians, particularly the African clawed frog (*Xenopus laevis*)
- Cascading ecological effects include disrupted insect control, altered food web dynamics, and changed stream nutrient cycling where amphibian populations collapsed
