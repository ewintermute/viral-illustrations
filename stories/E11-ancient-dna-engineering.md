# E11: Bringing Back the Dead (Sort Of)

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

The woolly mammoth (*Mammuthus primigenius*) went extinct approximately 4,000 years ago, when its last population—isolated on Wrangel Island in the Arctic Ocean—finally died out after the end of the last ice age. For most of the twentieth century, "extinct" was assumed to be permanent. Then came ancient DNA sequencing, and then genomic engineering, and now a company in Texas is inserting mammoth genes into elephant cells, and the question of what "extinct" means has become genuinely complicated.

The science underlying these efforts begins with permafrost. Siberian and Alaskan permafrost has preserved mammoth remains—bones, skin, hair, and occasionally soft tissue—for tens of thousands of years. The cold, dry, low-oxygen environment of permafrost slows the chemical reactions that normally degrade DNA, sometimes preserving readable genetic material for over 100,000 years. Beginning in the 2000s and accelerating through the 2010s, researchers at institutions worldwide began sequencing mammoth genomes from permafrost specimens. By 2015, a near-complete mammoth genome had been assembled.

The comparison to the Asian elephant genome—the mammoth's closest living relative, differing from it by only about 0.6% of coding sequence—revealed which specific gene variants were responsible for mammoth-specific traits: the heavy fur, the extra subcutaneous fat, the cold-adapted hemoglobin that releases oxygen more efficiently at low temperatures, the smaller ears and modified circulatory system. Approximately 1.4 million DNA positions differ between the mammoth and the Asian elephant. The adaptive changes are a small fraction of that.

George Church's laboratory at Harvard began using CRISPR-Cas9 to introduce mammoth gene variants into elephant cell cultures beginning around 2015—not to clone a mammoth, but to create an elephant with mammoth adaptations. The goal of the eventual company that emerged from this work, Colossal Biosciences (founded 2021), is not to resurrect an exact mammoth but to produce a "cold-adapted elephant" carrying enough of the mammoth's key adaptations to potentially survive and function in northern ecosystems where the Asian elephant currently cannot.

The ecological argument is that woolly mammoths maintained the grassland steppe ecosystem of the Arctic through grazing behavior—trampling mosses, knocking over trees, and fertilizing grasses. This grassland ecosystem reflected more sunlight and insulated the permafrost better than the mossy tundra that replaced it. Reintroducing a functional mammoth analog could, theoretically, help preserve permafrost and slow the release of enormous quantities of frozen carbon as the Arctic warms.

In 2025, Colossal and collaborators reported reconstructing the chromosome-level genome of the woolly mammoth with high fidelity using Hi-C sequencing data from ancient specimens. The project has not yet produced a living animal. Significant hurdles remain: the Asian elephant gestation period is 22 months, and current embryo development and reproductive technologies for elephants are extremely limited. Colossal is also pursuing artificial womb technology for elephants—a far more technically challenging goal than genomic editing.

Whether this constitutes "de-extinction" is a philosophical question as much as a scientific one. The animal that would result would be an elephant, not a mammoth—with some key mammoth genes inserted. Its ecology, its behavior, and its immune system would be elephant-derived. But it might walk on frozen tundra, and it might look, from a distance, like something that died 4,000 years ago.

## Key Facts

- Woolly mammoth genomes have been assembled from permafrost specimens, revealing ~1.4 million differences from Asian elephant sequence; key cold-adaptations involve fewer than 100 loci
- CRISPR editing of mammoth gene variants into elephant cells began around 2015 at Harvard; Colossal Biosciences (founded 2021) is pursuing creation of a cold-adapted elephant
- Ecological argument: mammoths maintained Arctic grassland steppe, which reflects more sunlight and insulates permafrost better than current moss tundra
- 2025: near-complete chromosome-level mammoth genome reconstructed via Hi-C sequencing of ancient specimens
- Key challenges: 22-month elephant gestation, limited elephant reproductive technology, need for artificial womb systems
