# K3: The Skin That Bacteria Can't Grip

*Category: Biomimetics & Materials*

## The Story

Shark skin is not smooth. Run your hand from tail to head against the grain, and you will feel the roughness — a surface covered with tiny, tooth-like scales called dermal denticles, each a few hundred micrometers wide, oriented in precise patterns aligned with water flow. The function of shark skin has been studied for decades in the context of swimming hydrodynamics, and it has inspired a wave of biomimetic engineering — though the actual physics turns out to be more complicated than early accounts suggested.

The original claim was straightforward: shark denticles reduce hydrodynamic drag by disrupting boundary layer turbulence, allowing water to flow more smoothly over the surface. This idea drove the development of Speedo's Fastskin swimsuit series and, later, the LZR Racer worn at the 2008 Beijing Olympics, where 23 of 25 world records fell and athletes wearing the suit won 94% of all swimming events. FINA, the international swimming governing body, subsequently banned many technical swimsuits, classifying LZR-equivalent designs as performance enhancing in a manner inconsistent with fair competition.

However, the physics of actual shark skin is more nuanced. A 2014 study measuring drag on real shark skin found that the denticles actually increase drag in some flow conditions — the skin was drag-reducing only when oriented in the flow direction, and increased drag when oriented against it. The denticles may function less as simple drag reducers and more as devices that control flow separation and prevent stall at high swimming speeds and angles of attack. The biomimetic principle in swimsuits may have been simpler than the biological reality.

The antibacterial story is less ambiguous. Shark skin's surface topology — the spacing and geometry of the denticles — creates a surface that bacteria and biofilm-forming organisms find extremely difficult to colonize. The mechanism is physical rather than chemical: bacteria that attempt to attach to the sharkskin surface can contact only the peaks of the denticle structures, reducing the available surface area for attachment to a fraction of a percent of the geometric area. Without sufficient contact points, bacteria cannot anchor, cannot form biofilm, and are swept away by fluid flow.

Sharklet Technologies commercialized this principle, producing a synthetic surface texture — the Sharklet pattern — that replicates the denticle-inspired micro-topology on polymer films. In laboratory testing, the Sharklet pattern reduced bacterial colonization by 67–97% compared to smooth surfaces, with efficacy against methicillin-resistant *Staphylococcus aureus* (MRSA), *E. coli*, and other hospital pathogens. The mechanism requires no antibiotic chemicals — it is purely physical inhibition of attachment.

Hospital-acquired infections kill roughly 99,000 people per year in the United States and represent a major driver of antibiotic-resistant organism proliferation. Surfaces — door handles, IV ports, bed rails, catheter surfaces — are primary reservoirs for pathogen transmission. An antibiotic-free surface texture that prevents bacterial attachment without creating selection pressure for resistance addresses this problem without the arms race dynamics of chemical antimicrobials.

The sharkskin principle demonstrates a broader theme in biological surfaces: topology is function. The shark didn't need antibiotics. It needed geometry.

## Key Facts

- Shark skin is covered with tooth-like dermal denticles that physically prevent bacterial attachment by reducing available contact area to a fraction of a percent
- The Sharklet antimicrobial surface pattern, inspired by shark denticles, reduces bacterial colonization by 67–97% against MRSA, *E. coli*, and other pathogens with no chemical agents
- Speedo's LZR Racer, loosely inspired by sharkskin texture, was associated with 23 of 25 world records at the 2008 Beijing Olympics and was subsequently banned
- Real shark skin denticles may function primarily to prevent flow separation (anti-stall) rather than simple drag reduction — the physics are more complex than early biomimetic accounts suggested
- Physical topology-based antibacterial surfaces avoid the arms race dynamics of chemical antimicrobials and do not select for resistance
