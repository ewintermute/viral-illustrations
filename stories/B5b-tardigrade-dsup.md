# B5b: The Protein That Shields DNA

*Category: Hidden Complexity*

## The Story

In 2016, a team at the University of Tokyo made a discovery that upended assumptions about radiation biology. They had been sequencing the genome of *Ramazzottius varieornatus*, one of the most radiation-tolerant tardigrade species, when they found a gene encoding a protein unlike anything in any other organism's genome. They named it Dsup—short for "damage suppressor." When they expressed this single tardigrade gene in human cultured cells, the cells became significantly more resistant to X-ray radiation. The gene didn't repair radiation damage after the fact. It prevented the damage from forming in the first place.

Dsup is classified as an intrinsically disordered protein—a molecule without a fixed three-dimensional structure, flexible and adaptable rather than folded into a rigid shape. What Dsup does have is a nucleosome-binding domain structurally similar to a class of proteins called HMGNs, found across many organisms. Dsup uses this domain to associate physically with chromatin—the DNA-protein complex that packages chromosomes—wrapping itself around the nucleosome like a molecular blanket. When X-rays or reactive oxygen species arrive, they encounter Dsup first, and Dsup absorbs or deflects the damage before it can break a DNA strand.

The numbers from the original study were striking: human HEK293 cells transfected with the Dsup gene showed approximately 40–50% less DNA strand breakage after X-ray exposure compared to untreated controls. They also showed reduced DNA fragmentation when exposed to hydrogen peroxide. Crucially, cells expressing Dsup maintained their ability to divide and proliferate even after exposure to 4 Gray of X-rays—a dose that would normally halt cell division in unprotected human cells. The protection was not hypothetical. It was measurable, functional, and transferable.

Subsequent work refined the mechanism. Dsup's C-terminal region is the critical domain for DNA binding. The protein appears to suppress break formation not through enzymatic activity but through physical occupancy—it is simply there, in the way, when radiation strikes. This passive shielding mechanism is both elegant and robust.

The translational implications attracted immediate attention. Radiation therapy for cancer works precisely because ionizing radiation damages DNA—killing tumor cells. But it also damages healthy tissue surrounding the tumor, causing the notorious side effects: mucositis in the mouth, proctitis in the rectum, radiation sickness in the bone marrow. If Dsup could protect those healthy tissues while leaving tumor cells unshielded, it could dramatically improve the tolerability of radiotherapy.

In 2025, researchers at MIT, Brigham and Women's Hospital, and the University of Iowa published results showing that polymer-lipid nanoparticles carrying mRNA encoding Dsup could be injected into oral or rectal tissue in mice before radiation exposure, reducing double-stranded DNA breaks by roughly 50% at the injection site. The protection remained localized—the protein didn't reach the tumor. The key remaining obstacle is immunogenicity: the tardigrade protein is foreign enough that it would likely trigger an immune response in humans. Modified versions with reduced immune visibility are now in development.

A protein from a half-millimeter animal that has been desiccating and reviving on rooftop moss for 500 million years may soon be shielding cancer patients from the worst effects of their treatment. Evolution, as usual, solved the problem long before we thought to ask it.

## Key Facts

- Dsup (damage suppressor) is a tardigrade-unique protein that binds to chromatin and physically shields DNA from radiation before damage occurs, rather than repairing it afterward
- Human cells expressing Dsup show 40–50% less DNA strand breakage after X-ray exposure compared to controls
- Dsup-expressing cells continued to divide after 4 Gray of X-rays—a dose that halts division in normal human cells
- In 2025, MIT researchers demonstrated that nanoparticle-delivered Dsup mRNA reduces double-stranded DNA breaks by ~50% in mouse oral and rectal tissue before radiation exposure
- The main barrier to human clinical use is immunogenicity—the tardigrade protein may trigger immune rejection, requiring modified versions
