# A3: The Humongous Fungus

*Category: Extreme Biology*

## The Story

If you were standing in Oregon's Malheur National Forest, about 11 miles east of Prairie City in the Blue Mountains, you would see trees. You would see fir forests and open sky and the ordinary-seeming landscape of the American West. What you would not see — could not see — is the largest known organism on Earth, growing quietly beneath your feet.

It is a fungus. Specifically, it is a single genetic individual of *Armillaria ostoyae*, the honey fungus, and it covers **2,385 acres** — roughly 3.7 square miles, larger than 1,800 football fields placed side by side. It weighs somewhere between 7,500 and 35,000 tons, an estimate equivalent to more than 200 gray whales. It may be between 2,000 and 8,650 years old. It is, by any reasonable measure, the largest single living thing ever documented.

The word "single" is doing important work here. The forest above ground is home to hundreds of individual trees, separate organisms, genetically distinct. The fungus below is different. DNA testing across the entire 2,385-acre site has confirmed that every strand of mycelium, every microscopic filament woven through the soil and into tree roots across this vast area, carries the exact same genetic code. One organism. One individual. Continuous.

For most of its existence, this organism is invisible. The vast underground network is composed of **mycelium** — masses of thread-like hyphae — and **rhizomorphs**, black, root-like structures that look remarkably like shoelaces (earning the fungus one of its common names: "shoestring root rot"). The rhizomorphs extend outward at a rate of between 0.7 and 3.3 feet per year, slowly, relentlessly spreading through the soil. When they contact a conifer root — especially grand fir, the preferred host in this forest — they penetrate the bark and begin to parasitize the tree from within. The fungus colonizes the cambium, the living tissue just beneath the bark, disrupting the tree's water and nutrient transport. The tree weakens, then dies. Its wood becomes the fungus's next meal.

For a brief period each autumn, the organism surfaces. Small clusters of honey-colored mushrooms appear at the bases of affected trees, caps measuring 2 to 5 inches across, golden-brown and unremarkable-looking. These are the fruiting bodies — the reproductive organs of the organism. For most people who happen to see them, they're just mushrooms. The vast network that produced them remains out of sight, its true scale unimaginable from the surface.

The fungus was first identified in this location in 1992, when researchers initially estimated it covered about 400 acres. Further genetic analysis over subsequent years expanded that estimate to more than 2,000 acres, and more careful work eventually fixed the figure at approximately 2,385 acres. The original 1992 study was already remarkable; the follow-up findings made global headlines. The existence of a creature this large challenges intuitions about what an "organism" even is.

The *Armillaria* genus appears in other parts of the world — there is a notable specimen in Michigan's Upper Peninsula that covers about 91 acres, which was itself considered extraordinary before the Oregon discovery — but none approaches the scale of the Malheur organism. Rival candidates for "largest organism" include Pando, the clonal quaking aspen grove in Utah (a network of stems connected underground by a shared root system), and *Posidonia australis*, a seagrass clone in Australia that may cover 77 square miles, though its age and single-organism status are debated. The honey fungus claim rests on solid, reproducible genetic evidence.

From a forest management perspective, *Armillaria ostoyae* is classified as a pathogen. It has killed significant areas of conifer forest across the Pacific Northwest. But pathogen is too limited a frame for what this organism represents. It is a record-holder by mass, by area, by longevity — a single, continuous life form that has been slowly, invisibly eating a forest for thousands of years.

## Key Facts

- The Oregon *Armillaria ostoyae* covers 2,385 acres (3.7 square miles) and is confirmed by DNA testing to be a single genetic individual.
- It weighs an estimated 7,500–35,000 tons — more than 200 gray whales — and may be up to 8,650 years old.
- The organism grows via black "shoestring" rhizomorphs that extend through the soil at 0.7–3.3 feet per year, killing conifer trees by parasitizing their roots.
- Only visible above ground in autumn as small clusters of honey-colored mushrooms at the bases of affected trees.
- Initially documented in 1992 as ~400 acres; expanded to 2,385 acres through subsequent genetic analysis.
