# B11: The Animal That Doesn't Get Cancer

*Category: Hidden Complexity*

## The Story

A mouse lives two years and gets cancer with depressing reliability. A naked mole rat lives up to 41 years—ten times as long as any other rodent of comparable size—and in captive colonies containing thousands of individuals observed over decades, cancer has essentially never been documented. Not rarely. Never, or nearly so. This is not a statistical artifact. It is one of the most profound anomalies in mammalian oncology, and the mechanism behind it has turned out to be a molecule found in every vertebrate body, doing a job nobody knew it could do.

The molecule is hyaluronan, a long-chain sugar polymer (a polysaccharide) that forms part of the extracellular matrix—the structural scaffolding surrounding cells. All mammals produce hyaluronan, but naked mole rats produce a version that is five to ten times larger than the human or mouse equivalent. Where typical mammalian hyaluronan has a molecular weight of around 1 megadalton, naked mole rat hyaluronan reaches 6 to 12 megadaltons. This unusually high molecular mass results from elevated activity of hyaluronan synthase 2 (HAS2), combined with low levels of the degrading enzyme HYAL2.

In a series of experiments by Vera Gorbunova and Andrei Seluanov at the University of Rochester, published in *Nature* in 2013, the researchers discovered that this high-molecular-mass hyaluronan (HMW-HA) was the key to the naked mole rat's cancer resistance. When they grew naked mole rat fibroblasts in culture, they found that the cells would stop dividing when they came into contact with neighbors—a phenomenon called contact inhibition, which is one of the normal brakes on uncontrolled cell growth. Critically, naked mole rat cells initiated this contact inhibition at much lower cell densities than mouse cells—what the researchers called "early contact inhibition," mediated through both p16 and p27 tumor suppressor pathways working in tandem, unlike mice which rely primarily on p27.

When the researchers degraded the HMW-HA by either knocking out HAS2 or overexpressing the degrading enzyme HYAL2, this protection vanished. Naked mole rat cells could now be transformed by oncogenes and caused tumors in mice. The HMW-HA was not merely correlated with cancer resistance—it was causally responsible for it.

The molecule works through the cell-surface receptor CD44. HMW-HA binds CD44 with high affinity, triggering a signaling cascade that reinforces cell cycle arrest and promotes contact inhibition. The larger the polymer, the more effectively it clusters CD44 molecules on the cell surface, creating a more powerful stop signal.

In 2023, a research team went further: they introduced the naked mole rat HAS2 gene into transgenic mice. These mice, producing larger-than-normal hyaluronan, showed reduced cancer incidence and extended healthy lifespan. The trait transferred. Evolution had solved a problem in the naked mole rat, and the solution could be transplanted.

What makes this discovery remarkable is not just the mechanism but the implications for human medicine. Hyaluronan is already a known molecule—used in joint injections, cosmetic fillers, eye surgery. Its cancer-protective role at high molecular weight suggests a pharmaceutical avenue: maintaining or restoring HMW-HA in tissues might reduce cancer risk or slow tumor progression. The naked mole rat, one of the strangest-looking mammals alive, may be pointing toward one of the most important drug targets in oncology.

## Key Facts

- Naked mole rats live up to 41 years—10x longer than similarly sized rodents—and show near-zero cancer incidence in captive colonies
- Their fibroblasts produce hyaluronan with molecular weight 6–12 megadaltons, five or more times larger than human or mouse hyaluronan
- This high-molecular-mass hyaluronan (HMW-HA) triggers early contact inhibition through dual p16/p27 tumor suppressor pathways via CD44 receptor signaling
- Degrading HMW-HA in naked mole rat cells abolishes cancer resistance; oncogenes can then transform the cells
- Transgenic mice engineered to produce naked mole rat-style HMW-HA showed reduced cancer incidence and extended healthspan
