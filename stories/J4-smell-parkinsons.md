# J4: The Woman Who Smelled Parkinson's

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

In 1982, Joy Milne, a retired nurse in Perth, Scotland, noticed that her husband Les had developed an unusual smell — a faint musky, slightly soapy odor that she had never noticed before. She thought nothing of it for years. Then, in 1986, Les was diagnosed with Parkinson's disease. Much later, attending a Parkinson's support group, Joy noticed the same distinctive smell on several other people in the room.

The implication crystallized slowly: she could smell Parkinson's disease.

When she brought this observation to researchers at the University of Edinburgh, the response was initially skeptical. Human olfactory abilities are impressive in some domains and poor in others, and the idea that a disease would produce a detectable odor signature seemed improbable. But Joy Milne was not an ordinary smeller. She is a "super smeller" — an individual with unusually high olfactory sensitivity and discrimination — and her claim was specific, consistent, and testable.

Researchers at Edinburgh designed a blinded study. They gave Joy Milne 12 T-shirts worn by participants: 6 people with Parkinson's disease and 6 healthy controls. She identified 11 correctly. The one person she misidentified as having Parkinson's — a healthy control — was later diagnosed with the disease eight months after the study ended. Her accuracy, in retrospect, was 12 out of 12.

The scientific implication was significant: if Joy Milne could detect Parkinson's disease by smell, there must be a molecular signal in the skin or breath of Parkinson's patients that was chemically distinct from healthy controls. Identifying that signal could lead to a non-invasive biomarker test detectable years before motor symptoms appear — the window when therapeutic intervention might prevent the worst neurodegeneration.

Researchers at Manchester Metropolitan University, using mass spectrometry, analyzed sebum samples — the oily secretion produced by sebaceous glands in the skin — from Parkinson's patients and controls. Their 2019 paper identified specific volatile organic compounds, including hippuric acid, eicosane, and several other sebum-derived metabolites, that were significantly elevated in Parkinson's patients compared to controls. The compound profiles matched what Joy Milne described as the characteristic odor. The study provided a molecular inventory of the olfactory signal she had been detecting for decades.

Parkinson's disease involves the accumulation of misfolded alpha-synuclein protein in neurons throughout the nervous system, including the autonomic nerves controlling skin sebaceous glands. The disrupted autonomic signaling alters sebum composition, changing the mixture of volatile organic compounds released from the skin surface. These volatiles diffuse into the immediate environment — reaching a super-smeller's nose at close range.

The practical goal is a skin swab test that can be processed by mass spectrometry to detect Parkinson's disease before symptoms appear. Current diagnosis depends on motor symptoms — tremor, rigidity, slowness — that appear only after roughly 60–80% of the dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra have already been destroyed. A pre-symptomatic biomarker test derived from Joy Milne's observation could shift diagnosis by years, potentially enabling neuroprotective therapies during the window when they would do the most good.

## Key Facts

- Joy Milne detected Parkinson's disease by smell in her husband years before his diagnosis, and later identified 12/12 Parkinson's patients and controls correctly in a blinded T-shirt test
- Mass spectrometry of Parkinson's patient sebum identified distinctive volatile biomarkers including hippuric acid and eicosane — the molecular basis of the detectable odor
- The volatiles arise from altered sebum composition driven by Parkinson's-disrupted autonomic nerve function in sebaceous glands
- Parkinson's motor symptoms appear after 60–80% of substantia nigra dopamine neurons are already lost; a pre-symptomatic skin biomarker test could enable earlier intervention
- Clinical validation of a sebum volatile biomarker panel for Parkinson's screening is ongoing
