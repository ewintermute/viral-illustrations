# J12: The Gene That Built the Human Brain

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

Of all the questions in human evolution, perhaps the most compelling is this: what happened in the genome roughly 3 to 5 million years ago that ultimately produced a brain three times larger than that of our nearest living relatives? The great apes — chimpanzees, gorillas, orangutans — are intelligent, socially complex animals with substantial brains. But the human neocortex, the outer layer of the brain responsible for abstract reasoning, language, and consciousness, is folded into a deeply convoluted surface area that no other primate approaches. Something in our genome caused the outer brain to grow dramatically and fold extensively. In 2015, researchers identified one of the key molecular culprits: a gene called *ARHGAP11B* that exists only in humans.

*ARHGAP11B* is not an ancient gene. It arose approximately 3.5 million years ago — after the divergence of the lineage leading to modern humans from the lineage leading to chimpanzees — through a partial duplication of an older gene called *ARHGAP11A*. During the duplication, a small segment of DNA sequence changed in a way that fundamentally altered the protein's function. The original *ARHGAP11A* is a GTPase-activating protein involved in cytoskeletal regulation; the duplicated *ARHGAP11B* gained a new activity: stimulating the self-renewal of basal radial glia, a type of neural stem cell in the developing neocortex.

Basal radial glia are the progenitor cells that produce most of the neurons of the outer neocortex. In species with small, smooth (lissencephalic) brains — mice, for example — basal radial glia are sparse. In species with large, folded (gyrencephalic) brains — humans, great apes, and dolphins — basal radial glia are abundant. The number of these progenitors during fetal development essentially sets the upper bound on neocortical neuron production. *ARHGAP11B* expands the pool of basal radial glia by causing them to divide more times before differentiating into neurons, producing more neurons, more neocortex, and ultimately more folding to accommodate the expanded surface area.

The causal relationship between *ARHGAP11B* and brain expansion was tested directly. Researchers introduced the human gene into mouse embryos — animals that normally have smooth, unfolded neocortices. The mice developed noticeably larger neocortices with increased folding. The same experiment in ferrets, which naturally have mildly folded neocortices, produced even more pronounced gyrification. Neither mouse nor ferret normally expresses *ARHGAP11B*. The gene was sufficient to drive neocortical expansion in a foreign genetic context.

Humans express *ARHGAP11B* robustly during fetal neocortical development. Chimpanzees do not have the gene. Neanderthals and Denisovans, based on ancient DNA analysis, did carry it — suggesting that the brain-expansion capacity of *ARHGAP11B* was shared across the broader Homo lineage, not uniquely modern human. Our large brain may have been developing for over 3 million years, driven in part by this single gene duplication.

What makes the *ARHGAP11B* story powerful is its simplicity. A single partial duplication, a small sequence change, a new protein activity targeting a specific neural progenitor — and the result was a cascade of developmental change that ultimately produced the organ responsible for language, art, mathematics, and the capacity to ask questions about its own origins.

## Key Facts

- *ARHGAP11B* is a human-specific gene that arose ~3.5 million years ago through partial duplication of *ARHGAP11A*, after the divergence from chimpanzee ancestors
- It stimulates self-renewal of basal radial glia — the neural progenitor cells responsible for most neocortical neuron production
- Introducing *ARHGAP11B* into mouse and ferret embryos causes enlarged, folded neocortices in animals that normally have smooth brains
- Both Neanderthals and Denisovans carried *ARHGAP11B*, suggesting the brain-expansion mechanism is shared across the broader Homo genus
- The gene acts specifically during fetal development; its expression correlates with the basal radial glia expansion that distinguishes human neocortical development
