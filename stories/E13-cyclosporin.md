# E13: The Fungus from Norway That Saved Transplant Medicine

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

In 1969, a Sandoz Pharmaceuticals employee traveling in Norway collected a small bag of soil from the Hardanger Vidda plateau—a high, windswept plateau above the fjords of western Norway, cold and nutrient-poor, the kind of environment where only specialists survive. The soil was one of thousands of samples collected as part of Sandoz's ongoing natural product screening program, launched in 1957 to systematically search microbial communities for pharmacologically active compounds. Most soil samples yielded nothing. This one yielded everything.

From the Norwegian soil, microbiologists isolated a fungus eventually identified as *Tolypocladium inflatum*. The fungus produced a family of cyclic peptide metabolites—ring-shaped chains of amino acids—with modest antifungal activity but nothing remarkable. By the criteria of the screening program, it was a borderline result. Standard procedure would have been to move on.

What saved the compound was a reorganization of the Sandoz screening program in 1972 that added immunosuppression testing. When researchers tested the cyclosporine extracts against immune cells, they found something unexpected: the compound inhibited the proliferation of T lymphocytes—the immune cells responsible for rejecting transplanted organs—without the general cytotoxicity that characterized earlier immunosuppressants. This was a genuinely new pharmacological action.

Jean-François Borel, the Sandoz researcher who characterized the immunosuppressive properties, was so intrigued that he reportedly tested the compound on himself before it had gone through formal toxicology. His survival, and the early animal data, justified proceeding. Clinical trials in organ transplant patients began in the late 1970s, showing that cyclosporine dramatically improved one-year graft survival rates compared to the existing standard of care. In 1983, Sandoz received FDA approval for cyclosporine (Sandimmune) for the prevention of rejection in kidney, heart, and liver transplants.

The mechanism, identified later, is precise and elegant. Cyclosporine binds to a cellular protein called cyclophilin. The cyclosporine-cyclophilin complex then inhibits calcineurin, a phosphatase enzyme that is required to activate the transcription factor NFAT (nuclear factor of activated T cells). Without calcineurin, NFAT cannot enter the nucleus and drive expression of interleukin-2 (IL-2), the cytokine that stimulates T cell proliferation. The result: T cells cannot mount the rejection response, but most other immune functions are preserved. The selectivity—T cells over other immune components—is what made cyclosporine so revolutionary.

Before cyclosporine, organ transplantation was a procedure with high short-term failure rates and severe management challenges. The immunosuppressive drugs available (azathioprine, high-dose steroids) were blunt instruments with severe toxicity profiles. After cyclosporine, one-year success rates for kidney transplants improved by roughly 20 percentage points. Heart and liver transplantation, previously experimental procedures, became established clinical practice. The current system of transplantation medicine—in which over 140,000 organ transplants are performed annually worldwide—was built on the foundation of a compound from Norwegian soil.

## Key Facts

- Cyclosporine was isolated from the fungus *Tolypocladium inflatum*, found in soil from the Hardanger Vidda plateau in Norway, as part of a Sandoz pharmaceutical screening program initiated in 1969
- Its immunosuppressive properties were discovered by Sandoz researcher J.F. Borel in 1976; FDA approval as Sandimmune came in 1983
- Mechanism: cyclosporine-cyclophilin complex inhibits calcineurin → blocks NFAT → prevents IL-2 production → T cells cannot mount rejection responses
- One-year graft survival rates for kidney transplants improved by ~20 percentage points with cyclosporine vs. prior standard of care
- Heart and liver transplantation became established clinical procedures largely because of cyclosporine's development
