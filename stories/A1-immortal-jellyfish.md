# A1: The Immortal Jellyfish

*Category: Extreme Biology*

## The Story

Somewhere in the warm waters of the Mediterranean, an ant-sized jellyfish is doing something no other animal on Earth can do: it is growing younger.

*Turritopsis dohrnii* — the immortal jellyfish — has cracked a problem that has defeated every other multicellular creature in the history of life. When it gets old, injured, or stressed, it doesn't die. It reverses. The mature adult medusa — the bell-shaped, tentacled form you'd recognize as a jellyfish — collapses into a blob, reorganizes its cells over 24 to 72 hours, and transforms back into a polyp. A juvenile. The beginning of the life cycle. From there, it can grow up again, produce new medusae, age, and reverse again. And again. Potentially forever.

The mechanism is called **transdifferentiation**, and it's one of biology's most remarkable tricks. In almost every animal, differentiated cells are locked into their identity: a muscle cell stays a muscle cell, a nerve cell stays a nerve cell. Differentiation is a one-way door. But in *T. dohrnii*, that door can swing both ways. Swimming cells and digestive cells from the adult body can "forget" what they are and reprogram into entirely different cell types suited to the polyp body plan — without first reverting to a neutral stem cell state. It's as if a trained surgeon could directly become a plumber without going back to school.

Transcriptome studies that compared gene expression across the cyst, polyp, and medusa life stages have revealed what's happening at the molecular level. During reversal, the jellyfish upregulates genes for **telomerase activity** (the enzyme that rebuilds chromosome tips eroded by aging), **DNA repair pathways**, and the regulation of transposable elements — the mobile genetic sequences that can destabilize a genome if not kept in check. At the same time, it dials down genes controlling cell division, differentiation, and signaling. The cell identity machinery is put into standby. The rejuvenation machinery takes over.

Even more striking, researchers have found that genes analogous to the **Yamanaka factors** — the four transcription factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, and c-Myc) that Shinya Yamanaka used to reprogram adult human cells into induced pluripotent stem cells, winning him a Nobel Prize in 2012 — appear to be active in *T. dohrnii* during life cycle reversal. Nature, it turns out, invented cellular reprogramming hundreds of millions of years before we did. The jellyfish also expresses genes like Lin28 and Nanog, both associated with embryonic stemness in mammals.

A landmark 2022 study published in *PNAS* by researchers who compared the genomes of *T. dohrnii* with closely related mortal cnidarians found that the immortal jellyfish has approximately 17,000 genes, with a distinctive molecular toolkit for aging and repair that its mortal relatives simply lack. The reversal rate in laboratory conditions can reach 100% after reproduction — every single individual can revert rather than die.

This does not make the jellyfish truly unkillable. In the wild, most individuals are eaten by predators, killed by disease, or succumb to environmental catastrophe long before old age would have been a concern. Biological immortality, in the technical sense, means only that there is no intrinsic upper limit on lifespan from senescence — not that death is impossible. But the underlying mechanism is real and profound.

For aging researchers, *T. dohrnii* is a proof of concept written in flesh. It demonstrates that senescence — the progressive cellular decay we call aging — is not an inevitable physical law. It is a biological program, and programs can be rewritten. The jellyfish already rewrites its own, every time conditions demand it.

---

## Key Facts

- *Turritopsis dohrnii* can reverse from adult medusa to juvenile polyp stage through a process called transdifferentiation — the only known animal capable of this.
- The reversal is triggered by physical damage, starvation, or aging, and takes 24–72 hours via an intermediate "cyst" stage.
- Genes analogous to Yamanaka factors (Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc), Lin28, and Nanog are active during reversal — the same factors used in Nobel Prize-winning stem cell reprogramming.
- During reversal, telomerase activity and DNA repair genes are strongly upregulated; the jellyfish essentially rebuilds its genome's protective structures.
- Laboratory reversal rates can reach 100% post-reproduction; the 2022 PNAS comparative genomics study confirmed the immortal species has a unique molecular toolkit absent in mortal relatives.
