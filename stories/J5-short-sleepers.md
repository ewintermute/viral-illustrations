# J5: The People Who Only Need Six Hours

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

Sleep research over the past two decades has established, with increasing rigor, that most adults need between 7 and 9 hours of sleep per night for optimal cognitive and physical health. People who claim to function well on 6 hours or less are, in the majority of cases, chronically sleep-deprived and cognitively impaired in ways they no longer perceive — the subjective sense of alertness is itself impaired by sleep deprivation. But there is an exception: a small fraction of the population, perhaps 1–3%, are genuine natural short sleepers, who sleep 6 hours or less per night, function at full cognitive capacity, and show no signs of the sleep deprivation consequences that afflict everyone else.

These individuals are not simply suppressing the urge to sleep or powering through on caffeine. Their physiology is different. Two genes have been identified that, when mutated in specific ways, produce the natural short sleeper phenotype.

The first was discovered in 2009 by Ying-Hui Fu and Louis Ptáček at the University of California, San Francisco, in a mother and daughter who habitually slept about 6 hours per night and felt completely rested. The mutation was in *DEC2*, a gene encoding a transcriptional repressor involved in circadian clock regulation. When the same mutation was introduced into mice, they also slept less and showed no signs of sleep deficit — confirming the causal relationship. The gene edit made the animals efficient sleepers, not just light ones.

The second natural short sleeper gene, *ADRB1*, was reported by the same UCSF group in 2019. The *ADRB1* gene encodes the beta-1 adrenergic receptor, a protein involved in the regulation of arousal through adrenergic signaling. The specific mutation found in natural short sleeper families causes altered receptor function, and mice carrying the equivalent mutation showed reduced total sleep time with maintained cognitive performance. Importantly, single neurons in the dorsal pons — a brain region known to regulate REM and NREM sleep — were unusually active in these mice, suggesting the mutation changes the neural circuitry governing sleep initiation and maintenance.

Both mutations are rare — occurring in less than 1% of the population — and both are inherited in families, rather than arising spontaneously. Natural short sleeper families typically show the phenotype across multiple generations, with the same short-sleep, early-waking pattern running through grandparents, parents, and children. The mutations are gain-of-function in the sense of enabling efficient sleep — producing the same restorative benefit in fewer hours.

The research opens a conceptual question about sleep that researchers are still grappling with: if sleep duration can be altered by single-gene mutations without health consequences, sleep duration itself may be a tunable parameter of biology rather than a fixed biological requirement. The question is not "how do we train people to need less sleep" — the experimental evidence is clear that normal people cannot do so safely — but rather "what specific molecular mechanism makes some people's sleep 30% more efficient, and can that be pharmacologically mimicked?"

No drug candidate has yet achieved the natural short sleeper profile in normal humans. But the UCSF discoveries have established that human sleep duration is genetically tractable, and that the consequences of efficient sleep — rather than suppressed sleep — could in principle be reproduced without sacrifice.

## Key Facts

- Natural short sleepers carry rare mutations in *DEC2* or *ADRB1* that allow full restoration in 6 hours or less without cognitive deficit
- *DEC2* mutation was identified in 2009 in a mother-daughter pair; mice with the same mutation slept less and showed no deficit
- *ADRB1* mutation was identified in 2019; it causes altered arousal signaling and hyperactivation of dorsal pons neurons during sleep
- Both mutations are familial, inherited across generations — natural short sleep runs in families
- The findings suggest sleep duration is a genetically tunable biological parameter, not a fixed requirement
