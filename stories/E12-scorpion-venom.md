# E12: The Most Expensive Liquid on Earth

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

The deathstalker scorpion (*Leiurus quinquestriatus*) is one of the most dangerous animals in the Middle East and North Africa—a pale yellow creature barely 10 centimeters long whose venom contains a cocktail of neurotoxins capable of killing children or immunocompromised adults. It is also, by some measures, the source of one of the world's most valuable liquids. Scorpion venom—obtained drop by microscopic drop, by hand, from animals that cannot be industrially milked—has been cited as costing as much as $39 million per gallon equivalent. The figure is often disputed and probably inflated for any particular venom type, but the underlying economics are real: each milking yields perhaps 2 milligrams of crude venom from a single scorpion, the process requires trained handlers working carefully with live animals, and the demand from research laboratories, antivenom manufacturers, and pharmaceutical developers is genuine and growing.

The primary reason for the scientific interest is chlorotoxin, a 36-amino-acid peptide isolated from deathstalker venom in 1993. Chlorotoxin is a chloride channel blocker with a peculiar property: it binds selectively to glioma cells—the cells that form glioblastoma, the most lethal and treatment-resistant primary brain tumor. Healthy brain cells largely ignore it. This selectivity is unusual and valuable. In a tissue type as complex and heterogeneous as the brain, distinguishing tumor cells from normal cells is one of the hardest problems in neurosurgery.

In 2014, researchers at the Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center developed a compound called BLZ-100, or "tumor paint"—a synthetic version of chlorotoxin linked to a near-infrared fluorescent dye. When injected into a patient, the chlorotoxin portion binds to glioblastoma cells while the dye makes them glow under near-infrared light. The technology entered Phase 1 clinical trials for glioma surgery with FDA IND approval, in centers in the US and Australia. Surgeons who could see tumor tissue fluorescing could potentially achieve better margins—removing more of the tumor while sparing healthy tissue. No actual venom is used in BLZ-100; the chlorotoxin is chemically synthesized.

A more recent application involves CAR-T cell therapy. In 2024, City of Hope researchers published Phase 1 results in *Cell Reports Medicine* of a trial using T-cells engineered with a chimeric antigen receptor containing the chlorotoxin binding domain, designed to seek out and kill glioblastoma cells that had spread through the brain. In four patients, 75% achieved stable disease after treatment. Glioblastoma is so aggressive that even this temporary stabilization is considered a signal worth pursuing; the trial is continuing with modifications designed to improve potency.

The deathstalker's venom, like all venoms, is an evolutionary product—millions of years of molecular refinement directed at the paralysis of prey and the deterrence of predators. The selectivity of chlorotoxin for glioma cells was not a design goal; it is an accidental consequence of structural properties shaped by entirely different evolutionary pressures. That same accidental structure happens to solve one of the most pressing problems in neurooncology.

The scorpion did not evolve to treat brain cancer. But it has given us, unexpectedly, one of the most promising leads we have.

## Key Facts

- Scorpion venom costs thousands of dollars per milliliter to produce; each milking yields ~2 mg of crude venom, requiring careful manual extraction from live animals
- Chlorotoxin, a 36-amino acid peptide from deathstalker venom, binds selectively to glioblastoma cells—a property useful for tumor visualization and targeting
- "Tumor paint" (BLZ-100) links synthetic chlorotoxin to a near-infrared dye to make brain tumor cells glow during surgery; Phase 1 trials began in 2014
- City of Hope's 2024 Phase 1 trial used chlorotoxin-guided CAR-T cells against glioblastoma; 75% of patients achieved stable disease
- Medical chlorotoxin applications use synthesized peptide, not raw venom
