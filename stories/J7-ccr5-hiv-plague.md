# J7: The Plague That Made Europeans HIV-Resistant

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

In the early 1990s, researchers studying HIV discovered a phenomenon that illuminated both modern medicine and medieval history. A small number of people repeatedly exposed to HIV simply didn't get infected. When scientists examined their genomes, they found that these individuals carried a variant of a gene called *CCR5* — specifically, a deletion of 32 base pairs that renders the CCR5 protein nonfunctional. HIV-1, the virus responsible for the AIDS epidemic, uses the CCR5 protein as a co-receptor to enter and infect CD4+ T-cells. Without functional CCR5, the virus has no doorway.

The mutation is called CCR5-Δ32. Individuals who inherit two copies — roughly 1% of northern European ancestry populations — are almost completely resistant to HIV-1 infection. Those who inherit one copy have elevated resistance and, if infected, progress to AIDS more slowly. The protein appears to be largely dispensable for normal immune function; CCR5-Δ32 homozygotes are healthy. This discovery launched the gene as a therapeutic target: the 2007 "Berlin patient," Timothy Ray Brown, was cured of HIV after receiving a bone marrow transplant from a CCR5-Δ32 donor, demonstrating that eliminating CCR5 could functionally cure HIV infection.

The mutation's frequency in European populations — approximately 10% allele frequency, or roughly 10% of people carrying at least one copy — requires an evolutionary explanation. Random mutations don't reach 10% frequency without help from selection. The question was: what selected for CCR5-Δ32 in Europe?

The prime candidate is the Black Death — the bubonic plague pandemic caused by *Yersinia pestis* that killed 30–50% of Europe's population between 1346 and 1353, with subsequent waves continuing for centuries. A 2003 study modeling the population genetics of CCR5-Δ32 concluded that its current European frequency was consistent with a strong selective advantage during a population bottleneck event about 700 years ago — exactly when the Black Death arrived. The hypothesis is that *Y. pestis* or a related pathogen used CCR5 as part of its entry mechanism into human cells, or that CCR5 influenced immune signaling in ways that affected plague survival, giving CCR5-Δ32 carriers a survival advantage.

The plague-selection hypothesis remains contested. Direct evidence that *Y. pestis* interacts with CCR5 is limited and inconsistent. Alternative hypotheses include selection during smallpox outbreaks (the smallpox virus variola has been shown to have some interactions with CCR5-related signaling) or selection during repeated hemorrhagic fever epidemics over European history. A 2022 PNAS analysis of historical frequencies using ancient DNA found that CCR5-Δ32 frequency rose sharply in European genomes precisely during the Black Death period, providing the strongest genomic evidence yet that plague was the primary selective force.

The CCR5-Δ32 story illustrates a broader principle: the pathogen environment our ancestors survived shaped our genome in ways we are still discovering. The ancient plague that shaped European immunity through one mutation also, by chance, created natural resistance to a virus that would not emerge for another six centuries.

## Key Facts

- CCR5-Δ32 homozygotes (~1% of northern Europeans) are nearly completely resistant to HIV-1 infection because the virus requires the CCR5 co-receptor to enter CD4+ T-cells
- The 2007 "Berlin patient" was functionally cured of HIV by a bone marrow transplant from a CCR5-Δ32 donor
- CCR5-Δ32 allele frequency is ~10% in European populations — far too high for random drift, implying historical positive selection
- Ancient DNA analysis (2022) found CCR5-Δ32 frequency rose sharply in Europe during the 14th century Black Death, supporting plague-driven selection
- The mutation appears dispensable for normal immune function; CCR5-Δ32 homozygotes are otherwise healthy
