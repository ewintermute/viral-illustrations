# D4: The Most Extreme Marriage in Nature

*Category: Evolutionary Marvels*

## The Story

In the deep ocean, finding a mate is one of the hardest problems in biology. The water is cold, pitch black, and nearly featureless for miles in every direction. Densities of any given species are vanishingly low. A female deep-sea anglerfish, drifting through this void, might encounter only one or two males in her lifetime. The deep sea ceratioid anglerfish evolved a solution to this problem so extreme that it abolished the biological distinction between two individuals.

When a male ceratioid anglerfish hatches, he is tiny—often just a few centimeters long—and equipped with nothing but extraordinarily sensitive chemoreceptors and a set of large eyes adapted to detect the faint bioluminescent lure of a female. He doesn't eat. He can barely swim. His entire existence is organized around one task: find a female before he starves. When he does, he bites down.

What happens next depends on the species. In some ceratioid families, the male attaches temporarily and mates, then releases. In others—the ones that have fascinated biologists since their discovery—he never lets go. Enzymes from the male's bite site dissolve the skin of both animals simultaneously. The wound heals around him. The male's circulatory system begins to merge with the female's. Over weeks, his eyes degenerate. His fins atrophy. His digestive system disappears. His brain reduces to a minimal structure. He loses his independent existence completely, retaining only his testes—functioning, supplied with blood from the female's heart, waiting to receive the hormonal signal to release sperm when she ovulates. He is, as one researcher described him, "a penis with a heart."

A single female can have up to eight males fused to her body simultaneously.

The immunological puzzle this poses is profound. Any human tissue transplant surgeon knows that merging tissues from two genetically distinct individuals triggers immune rejection—the recipient's T cells and antibodies attack the foreign tissue as non-self. Anglerfish that permanently fuse should die from this immune rejection within days. They don't. A 2020 study in *Science* by researchers from the University of Washington and their collaborators provided the answer: ceratioid anglerfish that practice permanent fusion have lost the genes encoding the adaptive immune system components responsible for this rejection—specifically, genes for MHC class II molecules, antibodies, and killer T cells. They have essentially dismantled their adaptive immune system to make permanent tissue fusion possible, compensating with a robust innate immune system.

This is an unprecedented evolutionary trade-off. In every other known vertebrate, losing adaptive immunity would be catastrophic—the animal would die quickly from infection. In the deep sea, presumably, pathogens are scarce enough and the reproductive advantage of fusion great enough that the trade was worth making. The anglerfish traded disease resistance for guaranteed mating success.

The most extreme relationships in nature are not always the most visible ones. In a cold black abyss, two animals dissolved into each other, sharing blood across all genetic barriers—this is what evolution produces when the problem demands it badly enough.

## Key Facts

- Male ceratioid anglerfish permanently fuse to females by biting; enzymes dissolve the skin of both and their circulatory systems merge
- The fused male loses eyes, fins, digestive system, and brain—retaining only testes—becoming entirely dependent on the female's circulation
- A single female can have up to 8 males permanently fused to her body
- Permanently-fusing ceratioid anglerfish have lost key adaptive immune genes (MHC II, antibody, killer T cell genes) to enable fusion without immune rejection
- This is the only known vertebrate group to have abandoned adaptive immunity, compensating with enhanced innate immunity
