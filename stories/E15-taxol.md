# E15: The Tree That Divided the World

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

In 1962, a USDA botanist named Arthur Barclay was collecting plant samples in Washington State as part of the National Cancer Institute's systematic screening of natural products for anticancer activity. One of his collections was bark from the Pacific yew (*Taxus brevifolia*)—a slow-growing, shade-tolerant tree that had been largely ignored by the timber industry because its wood was too small and twisted to be commercially useful. The bark was shipped to the NCI, screened, and found to contain cytotoxic activity. The active compound was isolated in 1971 and named paclitaxel.

Nobody knew what to do with it. The mechanism turned out to be remarkable—paclitaxel stabilized microtubules, the protein polymers that form the mitotic spindle during cell division, preventing them from depolymerizing. Cancer cells couldn't complete cell division and died. But the compound had terrible solubility, was extraordinarily difficult to synthesize chemically, and was present in Pacific yew bark at concentrations so low that obtaining clinical quantities would require harvesting enormous numbers of trees.

The NCI let the compound sit in its libraries for nearly two decades. Then, in 1989, a clinical trial in patients with advanced ovarian cancer—patients who had failed all existing chemotherapy—showed 30% response rates. In a disease that killed most patients within months, 30% was stunning. The compound worked where nothing else did. In 1992, the FDA approved paclitaxel (Taxol, licensed to Bristol-Myers Squibb) for ovarian cancer. A treatment that extended median survival from 24 to 38 months had arrived. So had the crisis.

Each treatment course required bark from approximately 3–6 Pacific yew trees. The trees grow slowly—up to 200 years to reach a useful size—and bark harvesting kills them. Simple arithmetic showed that meeting the projected demand for Taxol would require stripping hundreds of thousands of Pacific yews per year. The Pacific yew grows in the old-growth forests of the Pacific Northwest, the same forests that were simultaneously the focus of one of the defining environmental battles of the era—the effort to save the old-growth habitat of the northern spotted owl. Suddenly, environmentalists and cancer patients found themselves in direct opposition. The media coverage was brutal. The 1992 Pacific Yew Act attempted to regulate but not prohibit harvesting.

The resolution came from chemistry. Researchers discovered that another compound, 10-deacetylbaccatin III, present in much higher concentrations in European yew needles and clippings (which don't kill the tree), could serve as a semi-synthetic precursor to paclitaxel. Bristol-Myers Squibb and academic chemists developed the semi-synthetic route in 1994, making large-scale production possible without old-growth tree mortality. Later advances in plant cell fermentation allowed paclitaxel to be produced from yew cell cultures without any tree harvesting at all.

The Pacific yew conflict became a textbook case in bioethics and environmental policy: how do you weigh the rights of individual patients against the protection of ecosystems? The answer arrived not from the policy arena but from the laboratory—the chemists synthesized their way out of the contradiction.

## Key Facts

- Paclitaxel was isolated from Pacific yew bark in 1971 after NCI screening; its clinical potential remained overlooked until 1989 ovarian cancer trials showing 30% response rates
- Approved 1992 for ovarian cancer; paclitaxel+cisplatin extended median survival from 24 to 38 months
- Each treatment course required bark from 3–6 slow-growing Pacific yew trees; projected demand threatened hundreds of thousands of old-growth trees annually
- The conservation conflict pitted cancer advocates against environmental protection of spotted owl habitat until semi-synthesis (1994) using non-lethal European yew clippings resolved the supply crisis
- Paclitaxel is now on the WHO Essential Medicines list and produced via plant cell fermentation without tree harvesting
