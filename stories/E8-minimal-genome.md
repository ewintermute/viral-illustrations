# E8: The Minimum Required for Life

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

What is the smallest genome that can run a living cell? How many genes does life actually need? These are among the most fundamental questions in biology, and in 2016, the J. Craig Venter Institute answered them—at least approximately—by building a cell from scratch and stripping it down to its essentials.

The work built on two earlier milestones from the same laboratory. In 2003, JCVI had synthesized the genome of a small bacteriophage virus from scratch. In 2010, they had synthesized the complete 1.08-megabase genome of *Mycoplasma mycoides*, a simple bacterial pathogen, and transplanted it into an empty cell shell—the first organism "controlled by a chemically synthesized genome." Now they asked: how much of that genome is actually required?

The answer required a design-build-test cycle that took years. The team systematically tested which genes could be removed without killing the cell, then combined the deletions, then tested again. The result was JCVI-syn3.0: a *Mycoplasma*-based synthetic cell with a genome of 531,560 base pairs encoding 473 genes—about half the original—and the smallest genome of any known self-replicating organism.

Of those 473 genes, 149—nearly one third—encode proteins of completely unknown function. They cannot be assigned to any known biochemical pathway, have no detectable sequence similarity to characterized proteins, and their molecular roles are unidentified. Yet they are essential: when the researchers tried to delete them, the cell died or failed to divide normally. The cell needs them to live. Biology does not yet know what they do.

This result reframes the question of what we understand about the minimum requirements for life. Scientists can now build the minimum viable cell from scratch, but they cannot explain what approximately 31% of it actually does. The JCVI team categorized these mystery genes into "unknowns" (complete functional mystery), "generics" (function vaguely implied by distant sequence similarity but not specifically characterized), and "equivalogs" (similar proteins in distantly related organisms with known function, but the function was not definitively demonstrated here).

Subsequent work at JCVI, published in 2021, probed some of these unknown genes by further engineering the minimal cell. Researchers found that certain unknown genes were required specifically for cell division—the basic process of one cell becoming two. Without them, cells grew but produced bizarre filamented or clustered shapes instead of dividing normally. The genes were apparently encoding machinery for the physical mechanics of bacterial cell division, but machinery so unusual that it shared no recognizable sequence with any previously characterized cell division proteins.

The practical value of the minimal cell extends beyond basic science. A cell with the smallest possible genetic overhead is a potential chassis for synthetic biology: a blank platform onto which desired genetic circuits can be added without the metabolic noise of a complex wild-type bacterium. The fewer genes present, the less interference with engineered functions, and the more predictably the cell behaves as a programmable machine.

The philosophical implication is more troubling, and more interesting. We can synthesize life. We can run it on an artificial genome of our own design. We can reduce it to its minimum requirements. And at the minimum, there is a core of biology we do not understand—genes that are essential for life whose functions remain, as of 2026, completely unknown.

## Key Facts

- JCVI-syn3.0 (2016) contains 473 genes in 531,560 base pairs—the smallest genome of any known self-replicating organism, built through systematic removal of nonessential genes
- 149 genes (~31%) in the minimal genome have completely unknown biological functions yet are essential for cell viability
- Subsequent work found some unknown genes are required specifically for correct cell division—encoding machinery with no recognizable sequence similarity to known division proteins
- The minimal cell serves as a synthetic biology chassis: a genomic blank slate for engineering predictable cellular functions
- JCVI-syn3.0 built on JCVI-syn1.0 (2010, the first synthetic cell) by removing ~54% of the original genome through iterative design-build-test cycles
