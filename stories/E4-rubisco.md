# E4: The World's Worst Good Enzyme

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

The most important chemical reaction sustaining life on Earth is run by the most abundant protein on Earth, and that protein is remarkably bad at its job. RuBisCO—ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase—is the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation, the process by which plants pull CO₂ from the atmosphere and incorporate it into the sugars that power the entire food web. Without RuBisCO, photosynthesis stops. Without photosynthesis, life as we know it ends. RuBisCO is, in the most literal sense, the enzyme that all of us depend on.

It is also infuriatingly slow. A typical RuBisCO molecule fixes between 3 and 10 CO₂ molecules per second—making it orders of magnitude slower than most enzymes, which typically catalyze reactions thousands of times per second. A 2025 analysis in *PNAS* confirmed that RuBisCO is slow across essentially all branches of the tree of life; the handful of faster variants that have evolved in certain marine phytoplankton do not appear to have spread to land plants. Because the enzyme is so slow, plants compensate by producing enormous quantities of it: RuBisCO constitutes roughly 50% of all soluble protein in the leaves of C3 plants and accounts for 20–30% of total leaf nitrogen. A 2019 *PNAS* study revised the global mass of RuBisCO to approximately 0.7 billion tonnes—making it, by a wide margin, the most abundant protein on Earth.

But slow catalysis is only half the problem. The enzyme cannot reliably distinguish between CO₂ (its intended substrate) and O₂ (the other abundant atmospheric gas that has been present since the Great Oxidation Event 2.4 billion years ago). When RuBisCO mistakenly binds oxygen instead of CO₂, it produces a toxic molecule called 2-phosphoglycolate instead of the useful sugar 3-phosphoglycerate. The cell must then run a complex, energetically expensive salvage pathway—photorespiration—to detoxify the 2-phosphoglycolate and recover some of the wasted carbon. This mistake happens more than 20% of the time in C3 plants (which include wheat, soybeans, and rice), and the energy wasted in photorespiration can reduce effective photosynthetic productivity by 20–50% under warm, high-oxygen conditions.

The evolutionary explanation for RuBisCO's poor oxygen discrimination is historical. The enzyme evolved billions of years ago when Earth's atmosphere was largely devoid of oxygen and rich in CO₂. There was no selective pressure to distinguish between the two gases because oxygen was vanishingly rare. By the time oxygen became abundant—after cyanobacterial photosynthesis had transformed the atmosphere—the enzyme's basic architecture was locked in by billions of years of optimization in every other direction. Evolution rarely demolishes a working solution, even an inefficient one.

The agricultural consequence is enormous. Field trials published in 2018 by the RIPE project (Realizing Increased Photosynthetic Efficiency) showed that engineering a photorespiration bypass in tobacco plants—essentially providing a shortcut to dispose of 2-phosphoglycolate without running the entire wasteful salvage pathway—increased crop yields by 27–47%. Applying the same approach to food crops is a global food security priority. If the yield penalty from photorespiration could be halved in wheat, rice, and soybeans, the extra food produced would feed hundreds of millions of people.

The world runs on an enzyme that, by any reasonable engineering standard, should have been replaced long ago. It hasn't been, because evolution doesn't replace; it patches. The patches for RuBisCO are now the focus of some of the most ambitious crop engineering projects in history.

## Key Facts

- RuBisCO fixes only 3–10 CO₂ molecules per second—among the slowest known enzymes; plants compensate by making enormous quantities (~50% of leaf protein)
- Its global mass is estimated at ~0.7 billion tonnes, making it almost certainly the most abundant protein on Earth
- RuBisCO makes a "mistake" 20%+ of the time in C3 plants by binding O₂ instead of CO₂, triggering photorespiration that wastes 20–50% of photosynthetic energy
- The enzyme evolved when Earth's atmosphere lacked oxygen; by the time O₂ became abundant, the architecture was locked in
- Engineering photorespiration bypasses in tobacco boosted yields 27–47% in 2018 field trials (RIPE project); food crop applications are a major active research focus
