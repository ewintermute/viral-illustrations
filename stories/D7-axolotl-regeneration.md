# D7: The Salamander That Rebuilds Itself

*Category: Evolutionary Marvels*

## The Story

Cut off a human arm and you will spend the rest of your life with one arm. Cut off an axolotl's arm and, within weeks, the entire limb will regrow—bone, muscle, cartilage, skin, blood vessels, and nerves—in the precise pattern of the original, down to the correct number of digits in the correct positions. Amputate it again; it regrows again. The axolotl (*Ambystoma mexicanum*), a neotenic salamander endemic to a single lake system near Mexico City, can regenerate its limbs, its tail, parts of its spinal cord, and significant portions of its heart and eyes. It does this throughout its adult life, without scarring, with near-perfect fidelity.

The cellular mechanism is unlike wound healing in mammals. When an axolotl limb is amputated, the wound closes quickly but does not scar. Instead, cells from the surrounding tissue—muscle, cartilage, and connective tissue—dedifferentiate: they reverse their specialized identity and become mesenchymal progenitor cells again, similar to embryonic cells. These cells accumulate at the wound site to form a blastema—a mass of rapidly dividing progenitor cells that functions like a developmental organizer, reading positional information and rebuilding the limb from the stump outward.

The critical insight from recent research is that these dedifferentiated cells retain positional memory. A muscle cell that was in the upper arm knows it was in the upper arm; a cartilage cell from a digit knows it was a digit. This "cellular GPS" is encoded in the expression patterns of HOX genes and other positional regulators that are maintained through dedifferentiation. When the blastema cells redifferentiate and rebuild the limb, they use this retained information to reconstruct the correct positional identity—shoulder cells rebuild the shoulder, wrist cells rebuild the wrist.

Mammals are not completely incapable of such feats—mouse neonates can regenerate a damaged heart in the first week of life, and the tips of human fingertips can partially regenerate under certain conditions in young children—but this capacity is lost within days of birth in mice, apparently as a developmental switch is thrown. The genes required for regeneration are present in mammals; they're just not activated in adult tissues.

Why? One hypothesis is that the inflammatory response and rapid scarring that evolved in mammals to prevent infection at wound sites actively suppresses the conditions needed for blastema formation. The axolotl's regenerative capacity may be maintained partly because its immune response at wound sites has different characteristics—less scarring-promoting cytokine activity, different macrophage behavior. Recent work has shown that transplanting specific types of macrophages into mammalian wound environments can extend the window of regenerative potential, suggesting the immune microenvironment is a key regulator.

The axolotl genome is enormous—approximately 32 gigabases, roughly ten times the human genome—and was only fully sequenced in 2018. That sequencing revealed that most of the regeneration-relevant genes are shared with humans. The difference is not which genes are present but which are expressed, when, and in response to what signals.

The axolotl is critically endangered in the wild. Its native lake, Xochimilco, has been reduced by urbanization and pollution to a fraction of its original extent. The species survives primarily in captivity, in laboratory colonies worldwide maintained specifically for regeneration research. Its biology may one day provide the insights needed to restore regenerative capacity in injured human tissues—but only if the animal that carries those insights doesn't disappear first.

## Key Facts

- Axolotls can fully regenerate amputated limbs, spinal cord segments, and portions of the heart throughout adult life
- Regeneration proceeds through dedifferentiation of mature cells into blastema progenitors that retain positional identity (HOX gene expression patterns)
- Most regeneration-relevant genes are shared with mammals—the difference is regulation, not gene presence
- Neonatal mice can regenerate heart tissue in the first week of life; this capacity is lost as the inflammatory/scarring program develops
- The axolotl genome (~32 gigabases, 10× human genome) was fully sequenced in 2018, revealing extensive conservation with mammalian regeneration genes
