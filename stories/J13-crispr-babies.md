# J13: The Children Who Changed Everything

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

On November 25, 2018, Chinese scientist He Jiankui announced at a genome editing conference in Hong Kong that he had used CRISPR to edit the genomes of human embryos that were subsequently implanted and born as two baby girls. He named them Lulu and Nana — pseudonyms to protect their identity. A third CRISPR-edited child, a boy, was later revealed to have been born in 2019.

He's stated goal was to protect the children from HIV. He targeted the gene *CCR5*, which encodes the co-receptor that HIV-1 uses to enter T-cells. Individuals with a naturally occurring 32-base-pair deletion in *CCR5* — the CCR5-Δ32 variant found in some European populations — are nearly immune to HIV-1 infection. He reasoned that deleting the same gene in embryos would give the children lifelong HIV resistance.

The response from the scientific community was immediate and nearly universal in condemnation. Multiple problems were identified rapidly. The children's father was HIV-positive, but the probability of HIV transmission from father to child during IVF — where sperm are washed before use — is vanishingly small. There was no compelling medical justification for germline editing, which alters not just the children themselves but any children they might have in the future. The families may not have given fully informed consent, given the complexity of the science and the reported pressure applied in recruitment. And the editing itself, it emerged, had not worked as intended — not all cells in the embryos carried the CCR5 deletion, creating "mosaic" children whose protection status was unclear.

A 2019 study in Berkeley by Rasmus Nielsen's group added a dimension that made the story more disturbing. Analyzing the UK Biobank — a large genomic cohort with mortality data — they found that individuals who were homozygous for the natural CCR5-Δ32 deletion had 21% higher all-cause mortality before age 76 compared to individuals with normal CCR5. The deletion that He had tried to recreate was not simply neutral; it appeared to increase susceptibility to influenza and West Nile virus, among other pathogens. The HIV protection came with a cost that He had not disclosed to families and possibly had not fully considered himself.

He Jiankui was sentenced to three years in prison by a Chinese court in 2019 for illegal medical practice. The three children remain subjects of ongoing debate about who is responsible for their monitoring and welfare. Their actual genetic status — whether the edits worked, whether they are truly mosaic, whether they carry the intended deletion homozygously — has not been publicly disclosed.

The case crystallized a global debate about the ethics of heritable human genome editing. The key objections are not primarily to genome editing itself — somatic gene therapy, which edits cells in a living person without affecting future generations, is accepted medical practice. The objection is to germline editing: changes made to embryos propagate through the edited person's descendants permanently and irreversibly, affecting people who cannot consent and producing consequences across generations that cannot be recalled. The He Jiankui case demonstrated that the technology to make such changes already exists, that oversight mechanisms were insufficient to prevent unauthorized use, and that the scientific community was not prepared for this moment even though they had been discussing it for years.

## Key Facts

- He Jiankui used CRISPR to delete *CCR5* in human embryos in 2018, producing three children with edited genomes — the first known heritable human genome edits
- The stated goal (HIV protection) had no compelling medical justification given the minimal risk of HIV transmission through washed-sperm IVF
- CCR5-Δ32 homozygotes have 21% higher all-cause mortality than non-carriers, partly due to increased susceptibility to influenza and West Nile virus
- The editing produced mosaic results — not all cells in the children carried the intended deletion — making protection status uncertain
- He Jiankui was sentenced to 3 years in prison; the case catalyzed international calls for binding governance of heritable human genome editing
