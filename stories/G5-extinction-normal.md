# G5: Extinction Is the Normal Outcome for Species

*Category: Origins & Identity*

## The Story

Of all the species that have ever existed on Earth, an estimated 99% are extinct. The biodiversity we see today — the 8.7 million estimated species currently living — is a small remnant of what has been. Life on Earth has been generating and losing species for 3.8 billion years, and the losses vastly outnumber the survivors.

This is the baseline against which the current extinction crisis should be understood. Extinction is not an aberration. It is the default trajectory of any species. The question has never been whether extinction will happen but how fast.

The natural background extinction rate — the rate at which species would be lost in the absence of major disturbance — is estimated at roughly 0.1 to 2 extinctions per million species-years (E/MSY) for most groups. In practical terms, this means roughly 1 to 2 vertebrate species would go extinct per century under natural conditions. What researchers have found instead is a rate 100 to 1,000 times higher. Since 1500 AD, 338 vertebrate extinctions have been documented, with an additional 279 species classified as possibly extinct. The losses that would normally accumulate over 800 to 10,000 years of background extinction have occurred in the last 500 years.

The planet has seen five mass extinction events in its history — moments when more than 75% of species disappeared in geologically brief intervals. The most recent, 66 million years ago, ended the non-avian dinosaurs when an asteroid struck what is now the Yucatán Peninsula. The most severe, the Permian-Triassic event 252 million years ago, eliminated an estimated 96% of all marine species and 70% of terrestrial vertebrate species. Life recovered each time — but recovery took tens of millions of years, and the ecosystems that emerged bore little resemblance to those that had been lost.

Biologists are now in substantial agreement that we are in the early stages of a sixth mass extinction event, driven not by asteroid impact or volcanic winter but by a single species. Habitat destruction, overexploitation, climate change, invasive species, and pollution are the proximate mechanisms. The underlying driver is human population and consumption at scales the biosphere has never encountered from a single organism.

The current situation has features that distinguish it from all five previous mass extinctions. The driving cause is biological — a species — rather than geological or astronomical. The timescale is decades to centuries rather than thousands to millions of years. And uniquely, the cause of the extinction is aware of what it is doing and theoretically capable of choosing otherwise.

Whether that awareness translates into action is the question. The Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services estimated in 2019 that 1 million species are currently at risk of extinction within decades. Population-level losses are even more severe: a 2020 study found that vertebrate populations have lost ~94-95% of their geographic range since 1900, with over 237,000 populations vanished across 515 species.

The normal state of species is extinction. The sixth mass extinction differs from the previous five in that it has a cause that can read this sentence.

## Key Facts

- ~99% of all species that have ever existed are extinct — extinction is the statistical norm, not the exception.
- The background extinction rate is ~0.1–2 species per million species-years; current rates are 100–1,000 times higher.
- 338 documented vertebrate extinctions since 1500 AD; this represents 800–10,000 years of background extinction compressed into 500 years.
- Five previous mass extinctions (>75% species loss) occurred over Earth history; the sixth is ongoing and human-caused.
- An estimated 1 million species are at risk of extinction within decades according to the IPBES 2019 Global Assessment.
