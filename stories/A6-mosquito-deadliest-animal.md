# A6: The Deadliest Animal That Ever Lived

*Category: Evolutionary Biology & Human History*

## The Story

When people list the most dangerous animals, they tend to think of sharks, lions, crocodiles — creatures with teeth and size and the obvious machinery of violence. These animals kill, in aggregate, a few hundred to a few thousand humans per year. The mosquito kills between 725,000 and a million people annually, making it, by an enormous margin, the deadliest animal on the planet.

And that's just today. Stretch the view across human history and the numbers become almost incomprehensible.

Roughly 108 billion humans have ever lived. Estimates published in *Nature* and elsewhere suggest that mosquito-borne diseases — primarily malaria — have killed approximately **50 to 52 billion** of them. That is roughly **half of every human being who has ever existed**, erased by an insect that weighs about 2 milligrams.

The primary killer within that number is *Plasmodium falciparum*, the malaria parasite, transmitted almost exclusively by female *Anopheles* mosquitoes. The parasite's relationship with *Anopheles* is exquisitely evolved: it reproduces sexually inside the mosquito's gut, migrates to the salivary glands, and is injected into the next human host during feeding. The mechanics are ancient. Malaria has been infecting humans for at least **5,500 years**, documented in Egyptian mummies, ancient Chinese medical texts, and the writings of Hippocrates. It has shaped human genetics — the persistence of sickle cell trait in malaria-endemic populations is a direct evolutionary response to infection pressure from *P. falciparum*.

The historical footprint is visible in military records as much as in genetics. During the Crusades, an estimated **35% of European soldiers** died from mosquito-borne disease rather than combat. When the British invaded Cartagena in 1741, **84% of the invasion force** died — mostly from yellow fever and malaria, not enemy action. In 1780, **85% of Horatio Nelson's troops** in Nicaragua succumbed to mosquito-borne illness. During the Spanish-American War, mosquito diseases killed approximately **4,700 U.S. soldiers**, compared to 379 killed in combat. Mosquitoes didn't just accompany wars; they decided them.

Perhaps the most disturbing example is from World War II. In a deliberate act of biological warfare, **Nazi forces in Italy drained the Pontine Marshes' drainage systems** and released *Anopheles labranchiae* mosquitoes in 1943, deliberately re-creating conditions for malaria. In the Tiber Valley region of Italy, malaria cases jumped from 1,217 in 1943 to **54,929 in 1944**. Civilian populations were targeted with a vector that required no delivery system, no bomb, no visible weapon.

In 2023, malaria alone caused **597,000 deaths**, the vast majority in sub-Saharan Africa, and the vast majority of those deaths were children under five. Malaria kills, in other words, roughly one child every 90 seconds — a toll so regularized it rarely makes headlines. The CDC and the Gates Foundation estimate that control and treatment efforts since 2000 have saved approximately **12 million lives** — a figure that would be celebrated as one of the greatest achievements in human history if it applied to any other cause of death.

The Guinness World Records officially certified *Anopheles* mosquitoes as the deadliest animal known to man in 2017. The title is not metaphorical or approximate. It is supported by better mortality data than any other ranking, across more disease categories, over a longer historical period, than any competitor.

Humans kill approximately 475,000 other humans per year. Mosquitoes kill twice that number. And they've been doing it, without pause, for thousands of years.

## Key Facts

- Mosquitoes kill an estimated 725,000–1,000,000 people annually, more than any other animal — and about twice the human homicide rate.
- Mosquito-borne diseases, primarily malaria via *Anopheles* mosquitoes transmitting *Plasmodium falciparum*, may have killed ~50–52 billion people historically — roughly half of all humans who have ever lived.
- Malaria caused 597,000 deaths in 2023 alone, mostly children under five in sub-Saharan Africa.
- Military history repeatedly shows mosquito-borne illness deciding battles: 84% of a British invasion force in Cartagena (1741) died from yellow fever and malaria.
- In WWII, Nazis deliberately used *Anopheles labranchiae* as a biological weapon in Italy, spiking regional malaria cases from 1,217 (1943) to 54,929 (1944).
