# J3: The Ghost People Who Gave Tibetans Their Lungs

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

The Tibetan Plateau sits at an average elevation of over 4,500 meters — the highest large land mass on Earth, where atmospheric oxygen is roughly 40% lower than at sea level. Most people who ascend to these altitudes develop altitude sickness, and at sufficient heights, chronic mountain sickness eventually leads to polycythemia — excess red blood cells, thickened blood, and ultimately heart failure. Yet Tibetans have lived on the plateau for thousands of years in good health. They accomplish this without the dangerous blood thickening that afflicts other high-altitude dwellers.

The genetic basis of this adaptation is a story about people we know almost entirely through a single finger bone.

The Denisovans are an extinct human lineage discovered in 2010 from a finger bone fragment found in Denisova Cave in Siberia. Ancient DNA extracted from the bone revealed a distinct population that separated from Neanderthals roughly 400,000 years ago, spread across Asia, interbred to some degree with modern humans, and left a small but significant genetic legacy. Modern populations from Melanesia, South Asia, and East Asia carry 2–6% Denisovan DNA. Tibetans carry a specific Denisovan variant that explains their altitude adaptation.

The gene is *EPAS1*, which encodes hypoxia-inducible factor 2-alpha (HIF-2α) — a transcription factor that acts as the master regulator of the cellular response to low oxygen. In most humans, reduced oxygen activates HIF-2α, which stimulates erythropoietin production, driving red blood cell production upward. This response is adaptive in the short term but dangerous long-term at high altitude. The Tibetan variant of *EPAS1*, derived from Denisovans, blunts this response — Tibetans maintain hemoglobin concentrations similar to sea-level populations even on the plateau, avoiding the polycythemia that kills other populations living at similar altitudes.

The *EPAS1* haplotype in Tibetans is so unusual that it appears in less than 1% of Han Chinese (the most closely related lowland population) but in over 80–90% of Tibetan highlanders. The selective sweep — the rapid increase in frequency of an advantageous variant — is one of the strongest signals of recent natural selection in the entire human genome. Modeling suggests the variant may have risen to near-fixation in the Tibetan population in just a few thousand years of high-altitude living — among the fastest documented cases of human adaptation.

The Denisovan origin of this haplotype was established by comparing the Tibetan *EPAS1* sequence to all known modern human and archaic hominin genomes. The sequence was absent in Han Chinese, Neanderthals, and other human groups, but matched the Denisovan genome fragment. The adaptive haplotype was not generated de novo in Tibetans — it was acquired by interbreeding with Denisovans whose populations were already living in (and adapted to) high-altitude Asian environments.

The story of the EPAS1 haplotype is arguably the clearest case of adaptive introgression in human evolution: an ancient human lineage, adapted to harsh environments over hundreds of thousands of years, passed a specific genetic solution to modern humans through a brief period of interbreeding. A fragment of Denisovan DNA, in the lungs and blood of every Tibetan, keeps them alive at altitude.

## Key Facts

- Tibetans carry a Denisovan-derived variant of *EPAS1* that blunts the normal high-altitude response of increasing red blood cell production, preventing dangerous polycythemia
- The *EPAS1* variant is present in 80–90% of Tibetans but less than 1% of Han Chinese, representing one of the strongest recent natural selection signals in the human genome
- The Denisovan lineage is known primarily from a single finger bone from Siberia; their broader Asian range is inferred from ancient DNA signatures in living populations
- The adaptive haplotype was acquired through interbreeding with Denisovans who were already altitude-adapted, not independently evolved by Tibetans
- This is the clearest documented case of adaptive introgression in human evolution: an archaic lineage's adaptation directly saving its modern human inheritors
