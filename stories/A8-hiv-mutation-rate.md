# A8: Evolution Happening in Real Time

*Category: Virology & Evolution*

## The Story

Every year, the influenza virus circulates across the entire human population — billions of people, across dozens of countries — and through that global sweep, it accumulates a certain amount of genetic diversity. Mutations build up, strains diverge, and the virus gradually shifts enough that last year's vaccine becomes partially obsolete.

HIV achieves the same degree of genetic diversity **in a single patient**.

This is not a rough analogy or an order-of-magnitude estimate. The genetic variation of HIV found within one infected individual is approximately equal to the yearly global genetic variation of influenza across the entire human population. It is one of the most striking statements in all of evolutionary biology, and it is supported by well-characterized molecular data.

HIV is an RNA virus, and RNA viruses are generally fast-mutating because the enzyme that copies RNA — **RNA-dependent RNA polymerase**, or in HIV's case, **reverse transcriptase** (RT) — lacks the proofreading function that DNA polymerases use to catch and fix errors. HIV's RT makes approximately **3 to 5 errors per 100,000 bases** per replication cycle. Since the HIV genome is about 9,700 bases long, this translates to roughly **0.34 mutations per genome per replication cycle**. HIV replicates fast: an infected person may produce **10 billion new viral particles per day**. Run those numbers together and the scale of mutation becomes overwhelming.

But a 2015 study published in *PLOS Pathogens* revealed something even more startling: the RT error rate accounts for only about **2% of HIV's actual in vivo mutation rate**. The other **98% comes from the host itself**. The human immune system includes a family of enzymes called **APOBEC3** (apolipoprotein B mRNA-editing enzyme catalytic polypeptide-like 3) that are part of the innate antiviral defense system. These enzymes edit viral RNA by converting cytidine to uridine, causing a cascade of G-to-A mutations — a process called **hypermutation**. APOBEC3G alone accounts for ~88% of HIV's in vivo mutations; other APOBEC3 family members contribute the remaining 10%. HIV has evolved partial countermeasures (the viral protein Vif degrades APOBEC3G in infected cells), but the editing continues at massive rates.

The result is a viral population called a **quasispecies** — not a single, genetically uniform entity, but a swirling cloud of billions of related but distinct variants, all coexisting within one body. Some variants have mutations that help evade specific antibodies. Others have mutations conferring resistance to particular antiretroviral drugs. The immune system mounts a response to one variant; others fill the niche. The virus introduces a drug; variants with pre-existing resistance mutations, already present in the quasispecies cloud, proliferate. This is why HIV — unlike most infections — cannot be controlled by a single drug. Combination antiretroviral therapy (cART) works by requiring the virus to simultaneously accumulate multiple independent resistance mutations, a feat that even HIV's mutation engine cannot reliably accomplish quickly enough.

The diversity accumulates differently across the course of infection. A 2013 study in *PLOS Genetics* found that in **early infection** (the first year), HIV diversity increases rapidly at a rate approximating the raw mutation rate — unchecked replication, no immune brake. In **chronic infection**, the rate slows by 30-fold, as purifying selection — the elimination of variants that are too damaged to replicate effectively — kicks in. About half of all non-synonymous mutations (those that change the protein sequence) carry fitness costs greater than 10%. The quasispecies is constrained, but never frozen.

Intrapatient HIV genetic divergence can reach 17–35% at the amino acid level across different genomic regions — a figure that rivals the divergence between entirely different HIV subtypes globally. The virus evolving inside one person is, in a meaningful sense, as genetically diverse as the entire global pandemic.

---

## Key Facts

- The genetic variation of HIV within a single infected patient equals the annual global genetic variation of influenza across the entire human population.
- HIV's reverse transcriptase makes ~0.34 mutations per replication cycle; at 10 billion particles per day, this generates staggering diversity.
- 98% of HIV's actual in vivo mutation rate comes from host **APOBEC3** enzymes (primarily APOBEC3G), not the virus's own copying errors.
- HIV exists inside each patient as a **quasispecies** — a cloud of billions of genetically distinct variants — enabling simultaneous immune evasion and drug resistance.
- Intrapatient amino acid divergence across HIV genomic regions can reach 17–35%, rivaling divergence between HIV subtypes globally.
