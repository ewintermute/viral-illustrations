# E16: The Ancient Cure That Western Science Missed for 1,600 Years

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

In a Chinese medical text written around 340 AD, the physician Ge Hong described a treatment for intermittent fevers—the characteristic pattern of chills and sweats that marks malaria: take a handful of *qing hao*, a sweet-smelling wormwood plant, soak it in two liters of water, wring out the juice, and drink it all. Do not boil it.

That last instruction was the key. It was also, for 1,600 years, the clue that nobody followed up.

Malaria has killed more humans throughout history than any other infectious disease—estimates range into the billions. In the 20th century, chloroquine and quinine had provided effective treatments, but by the late 1960s, drug-resistant strains of *Plasmodium falciparum* were spreading through Southeast Asia. The Vietnam War was underway, malaria was killing more Vietnamese soldiers than combat, and both the North Vietnamese and the US militaries were desperately searching for new treatments.

The Chinese government, at the request of the North Vietnamese, launched a secret military research program in 1967 called Project 523. A young pharmacologist named Tu Youyou was assigned to lead a team searching traditional Chinese medicine texts for antimalarial compounds. Over several years, her team screened hundreds of plant extracts. They kept returning to *qing hao*—*Artemisia annua*, sweet wormwood—which appeared in numerous historical texts as a fever remedy. But initial laboratory extracts showed inconsistent and often weak activity.

Tu Youyou returned to Ge Hong's 1,600-year-old instruction: don't boil it. Standard extraction protocols used heat, which, she hypothesized, might be destroying the active compound. She switched to a low-temperature ether extraction. The resulting extract was highly active against malaria parasites in rodent models. The active compound—artemisinin—was isolated in 1972 and its structure characterized in 1975.

The mechanism of artemisinin is unusual for a drug. It contains an endoperoxide bridge—a bond between two oxygen atoms—that reacts with heme iron inside the malaria parasite's food vacuole. The reaction generates free radicals that damage the parasite's proteins and membranes. Unlike chloroquine, which targets the parasite at a specific stage, artemisinin is active against multiple stages of the parasite's lifecycle, including the critical early stages before clinical symptoms develop. This contributes to its extraordinary efficacy: artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) typically clear parasitemia within three days.

ACTs are now the WHO-recommended first-line treatment for uncomplicated *P. falciparum* malaria worldwide. An estimated 200+ million doses are administered annually, predominantly in sub-Saharan Africa where the disease burden is greatest. Since the widespread adoption of ACTs beginning in the early 2000s, malaria mortality has fallen by approximately 40% globally.

Tu Youyou was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2015, jointly with William Campbell and Satoshi Ōmura. She was the first Chinese scientist to receive a Nobel Prize in a scientific field. At 84, she was also among the oldest first-time Nobel laureates in history.

The irony of artemisinin's discovery is complete: a drug that saves hundreds of millions of lives per year was described in functional terms in a 4th-century medical text, and required only the insight of a 20th-century scientist to follow a single ancient instruction—don't boil it—to recover it.

## Key Facts

- Artemisinin was derived from *Artemisia annua* (sweet wormwood), which appears in Chinese medical texts as a fever remedy since at least 340 AD
- Tu Youyou recovered the compound in 1972 by switching from standard hot extraction to low-temperature ether extraction—preserving the heat-labile active compound, as Ge Hong's 4th-century text had implicitly described
- Artemisinin contains an endoperoxide bridge that reacts with heme iron in the malaria parasite, generating free radicals that destroy parasite proteins
- Artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs) are the WHO-recommended first-line treatment for *P. falciparum* malaria; ~200+ million doses annually
- Global malaria mortality declined ~40% following widespread ACT adoption; Tu Youyou received the Nobel Prize in 2015
