# J10: The Person Who Is Two People

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

In 2002, a woman in Boston was tested as part of a routine evaluation for a kidney transplant. The DNA test came back with a puzzling result: some of her cells carried one genetic profile, and others carried a completely different one. She was not the product of a single fertilization event. She had two distinct cell lines, each with its own DNA sequence. She was, genetically speaking, two people occupying one body.

Her condition is called tetragametic chimerism — the result of two separate fertilization events in the same pregnancy, producing two early embryos that fused into one during development. Each embryo contributed cells to the developing organism; those cells proliferated separately and ended up distributed across different tissues. The woman's blood carried one genome, while her uterus — the organ that had given birth to her children — carried another.

The custody case that made chimerism famous involved a different woman. In 2002, a Washington State woman named Lydia Fairchild attempted to prove maternity for her children in a custody dispute. DNA testing of her blood showed she was not the genetic mother of her own children — the test said someone else had given birth to them. The case became a legal nightmare until a physician familiar with chimerism ordered testing of other tissues. Cervical cells and skin cells from her arm matched her children's DNA perfectly. Her blood cells did not. She was a chimera, with her blood-forming stem cell lineage descending from one of the original twin embryos and her reproductive tissues from the other.

Tetragametic chimeras are formed when two eggs are fertilized by two different sperm — a natural event that occurs in perhaps 1–2% of twin pregnancies — and the resulting embryos fuse before full implantation, typically very early in development. The resulting person carries two complete genomes distributed across their tissues. The proportions vary widely: some chimeras have equal distributions; others have one cell line dominant in most tissues and the other present only in isolated organs.

The frequency of human chimerism is unknown and almost certainly underestimated. Most chimeras are never detected — if both cell lines carry the same blood type, ethnicity, and general physical characteristics, there is nothing to trigger testing. Some chimeras are detected incidentally through organ transplant workup, infertility evaluation, or — as happened with Lydia Fairchild — legal proceedings that require genetic proof of identity.

The discovery of chimerism has significant implications beyond the merely biological. DNA evidence is considered near-infallible in forensic and legal contexts. A chimeric individual could, in principle, leave different DNA evidence at the same crime scene if different tissues were sampled — blood versus epithelial cells, for example. The percentage of forensic cases where chimerism might produce misleading evidence is vanishingly small but not zero.

Most chimeras live entirely normal lives. The two genomes, distributed across different tissues, typically coexist without apparent immune conflict — presumably because immune tolerance is established early in development when both cell lines are present simultaneously, preventing each lineage from treating the other as foreign.

## Key Facts

- Tetragametic chimeras form when two fertilized embryos fuse in early development, producing an individual with two distinct DNA profiles in different tissues
- The discovery is usually accidental: Lydia Fairchild's blood DNA didn't match her own children, but cervical and skin cells did
- Chimerism is almost certainly underdiagnosed — most chimeras carry two cell lines with no outwardly detectable difference
- A chimera's uterus can carry a different genetic profile than their blood, potentially creating paradoxical maternity test results
- Two cell lines coexist without immune conflict because mutual tolerance develops in early embryonic life
