# B1: Cancer Was Always Going to Happen

*Category: Evolutionary Biology & Cancer*

## The Story

Cancer is not a malfunction. It is not a mistake. It is not a disease in the traditional sense of something invading from outside or going wrong in a random, arbitrary way. Cancer is, from a rigorous evolutionary perspective, the entirely predictable consequence of building a body out of cells that once knew how to live on their own.

The core argument is this: multicellular life requires cooperation between cells. Every cell in your body carries the same genome and, in principle, could try to replicate independently. What keeps them from doing so is a vast regulatory architecture — cell adhesion molecules, signaling pathways like Wnt, TGF-β, and Notch, differentiation programs, contact inhibition, apoptosis, immune surveillance — all of which suppress cell-level selection in favor of organism-level function. This is the **social contract of multicellularity**, and it is imposed on cells that, evolutionarily, have every reason to defect.

And eventually, some of them do.

A 2018 paper in *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B* titled "How the evolution of multicellularity set the stage for cancer" makes the case clearly: the genes that control cancer are, in many cases, the very genes that were repurposed or newly evolved to manage the transition from single-celled to multicellular life. When those genes are disrupted — by somatic mutation, epigenetic silencing, or microenvironmental changes — cells don't go haywire in a random direction. They revert. They re-express ancient, unicellular-style programs: grow fast, ignore your neighbors, consume resources, resist death signals. This has been called **atavism** — a return to an ancestral state. The cancer cell is, in a sense, a cell that has remembered what it used to be.

The evolutionary framing turns on the concept of **defection**. In game theory, a cooperator follows the rules of a shared system and contributes to collective goods; a defector exploits those goods without contributing. In the body, cooperative cells produce shared signaling molecules — growth factors like VEGF, matrix proteins, metabolic intermediates — that other cells benefit from. A cell that secretes VEGF to benefit its neighbors is cooperating. A tumor cell that free-rides on VEGF secreted by normal tissue, or that commandeers nearby cells to produce it on its behalf, is defecting. The fitness advantage of defection is real and local, even when it's catastrophic for the organism.

A 2017 paper in *PNAS* by Rowhani-Rahbar and colleagues modeled this formally and came to a stark conclusion: even with perfect selection against cancer-promoting mutations, intercellular competition will inevitably generate defectors over a long enough lifespan. Aging and cancer are not separable phenomena — they are both consequences of the same underlying dynamic. The longer a multicellular organism lives, the more somatic mutations accumulate, the more microenvironmental changes (inflammation, fibrosis, oxidative damage) tip the fitness balance toward defectors, and the more likely cancer becomes. Epigenetic changes alone — like promoter methylation silencing DNA repair genes such as MLH1 and MSH2 — can accelerate genetic instability by up to **100-fold**, creating feedback loops that accelerate the accumulation of further mutations.

None of this means cancer is unstoppable, or that attempts to prevent and treat it are futile. Evolution also shaped cancer suppression mechanisms — elephants have extra copies of the p53 tumor suppressor gene; naked mole rats have an unusual form of contact inhibition mediated by high-molecular-weight hyaluronan. The evolutionary arms race between cancer and cancer suppression is real, ongoing, and varied across species. But the arms race exists precisely because cancer is such a persistent evolutionary threat.

The framing matters for therapy, too. If cancer is an evolutionary process, then treating it requires thinking like an evolutionary biologist. Applying maximum selective pressure with one drug simply selects for resistant clones. Evolutionary medicine approaches — adaptive therapy, exploiting competitive dynamics within tumors, inducing defector-defector conflict — offer strategies that work with evolutionary logic rather than against it.

Cancer is not a bug in the design. It may be one of the unavoidable costs of having a design at all.

## Key Facts

- Cancer is the consequence of somatic **cellular defection** — cells that break the cooperative social contract of multicellularity by reverting to unicellular-style behavior.
- The regulatory machinery that keeps cells cooperative (Wnt, TGF-β, Notch signaling; apoptosis; contact inhibition) was the same machinery co-opted when multicellularity evolved — disrupting it causes reversion to ancient programs.
- A 2017 *PNAS* model showed that intercellular competition makes aging and cancer evolutionarily **inevitable**, even with perfect selection against cancer mutations.
- Epigenetic silencing of DNA repair genes (e.g., MLH1, MSH2) can increase genomic instability by up to **100-fold**, accelerating the accumulation of further mutations.
- Evolutionary medicine approaches — adaptive therapy, exploiting intra-tumor competition — aim to work with cancer's evolutionary logic rather than fighting it head-on.
