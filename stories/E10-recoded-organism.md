# E10: Rewriting the Code of Life

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

The genetic code is one of the most ancient things in all of biology. The same 64 three-letter codons encoding the same 20 amino acids—plus stop signals—has governed protein synthesis in virtually every organism on Earth for at least 3.5 billion years. It is one of the deepest conserved features of life, a molecular instruction set that all living things share. Changing it, even slightly, has been considered extraordinarily dangerous to any organism that tried.

In 2022, a synthetic biology team at Cambridge led by Jason Chin published something remarkable: a living *Escherichia coli* bacterium with a rewritten genome in which multiple codons had been systematically reassigned. The strain, called Syn61Δ3, had every occurrence of two serine codons (TCG and TCA) and one stop codon (TAG) replaced with synonymous alternatives throughout its 4-megabase genome. The bacterial cell then had the tRNA molecules and release factor that recognized those three codons deleted. The genetic code had effectively been compressed from 64 codons to 61—those three codons no longer had any meaning in this organism.

The consequences were profound. When a bacteriophage—a virus that infects bacteria—tries to infect Syn61Δ3, the virus uses the universal genetic code. Its protein-coding sequences contain TCG, TCA, and TAG codons in their natural frequencies. But in the recoded bacterium, those codons are blank. The virus's proteins cannot be properly translated. The phage cannot replicate. The bacterium is immune to a broad range of phages that would easily kill its unmodified relatives.

A 2023 extension of this work went further. The team introduced viral tRNA molecules into the recoded cell, but reprogrammed to read the formerly-serine TCG codon as leucine instead. This created a "swapped" genetic code: in this organism, TCG means leucine. Any virus that infects using the standard code and has TCG in its genes will have those genes translated with leucines substituted for serines—producing fundamentally different (and non-functional) proteins. The broad-spectrum virus resistance was confirmed against environmental phage mixtures. The strain, Ec_Syn61Δ3-SL, resisted infection from every phage tested.

A 2025 study pushed even further, producing an *E. coli* with 57 codons—seven standard codons replaced, freeing up seven codon slots that can now be reassigned to encode non-canonical amino acids: synthetic amino acids that don't exist in nature. Proteins made by this organism can contain chemical groups—fluorescent labels, crosslinkers, reactive handles—that proteins from any other organism cannot.

The biomanufacturing applications are significant. Industrial fermentation facilities are vulnerable to phage contamination—viral infections can destroy entire fermentation batches worth millions of dollars in hours. Recoded organisms resistant to any phage in the wild environment would be immune to these catastrophic losses. They would also be naturally biocontained: genes transferred to wild bacteria would be translated using the standard code, producing garbled or nonfunctional proteins that provide no selective advantage.

The genetic code that governed all life for 3.5 billion years is no longer universal. In a laboratory in Cambridge, and increasingly in others around the world, cells are running on different codes—and doing fine.

## Key Facts

- Recoded *E. coli* strains (Syn61Δ3 and descendants) have specific codons deleted from the entire genome and the corresponding tRNAs removed, compressing the genetic code
- The Ec_Syn61Δ3-SL strain assigns a formerly-serine codon to leucine; viral proteins translated with this code are mistranslated (serine→leucine), preventing virus replication
- Broad-spectrum resistance to environmental phage mixtures was demonstrated experimentally
- A 57-codon *E. coli* (2025) frees up 7 codon slots for encoding non-canonical amino acids with novel chemistry
- Applications include phage-resistant industrial biomanufacturing and biocontained synthetic organisms whose genes cannot function if transferred to wild bacteria
