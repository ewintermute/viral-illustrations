# E20: The Molecule That Might Slow Aging

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

In 1964, a Canadian medical expedition to Easter Island—a remote volcanic speck in the South Pacific—collected soil samples at 67 sites across the island. The goal was mundane: a microbiologist named Georges Nógrády was investigating why barefoot Easter Islanders seemed unusually resistant to tetanus, despite the island's abundant horse population and presumably tetanus-contaminated soil. The samples were shipped to Ayerst Laboratories in Montreal, where researchers began screening the microbial communities they contained for pharmacologically interesting compounds.

One sample contained a bacterium from the genus *Streptomyces*—a spectacularly productive natural product factory that has given medicine antibiotics like streptomycin and erythromycin, immunosuppressants, and antifungal compounds. This particular strain produced a compound with unusual properties: it was antifungal, antiproliferative (it inhibited cell division), and immunosuppressive. The compound was named rapamycin, after Rapa Nui—the Polynesian name for Easter Island.

For a decade, rapamycin sat in laboratory freezers. The Ayerst program was wound down, and the compound's development was intermittent. Suren Sehgal, one of the key researchers involved, reportedly kept his own personal sample frozen when the program was paused. Eventually, the immunosuppressive properties drove clinical development: rapamycin (sirolimus, trade name Rapamune) was approved by the FDA in 1999 for prevention of organ transplant rejection.

The mechanism that made rapamycin commercially valuable was also the mechanism that made it scientifically extraordinary. Rapamycin inhibits a protein kinase called mTOR—mammalian target of rapamycin (or, retrospectively, mechanistic target of rapamycin). mTOR is a central regulatory node in cell biology: it senses nutrient availability, growth factors, and energy status, and controls cell growth, protein synthesis, autophagy (cellular self-cleaning), and metabolic rate in response to these signals. When nutrients are plentiful, mTOR drives growth and suppresses autophagy. When nutrients are scarce, mTOR is inhibited, triggering cellular repair and resource conservation.

Caloric restriction—eating less—extends lifespan in virtually every organism tested, from yeast to mice to rhesus monkeys. The leading hypothesis for why involves mTOR: reduced food intake reduces mTOR signaling, shifting the cell toward repair rather than growth. Rapamycin mimics this state pharmacologically.

The evidence for rapamycin's anti-aging effects in mice is now robust and reproducible. In a landmark 2009 study by the National Institute on Aging's Interventions Testing Program, rapamycin fed to genetically heterogeneous mice beginning at 600 days of age—roughly equivalent to late middle age in humans—extended median lifespan by 9–14% and maximum lifespan by similar amounts. The mice were, by conventional measures, old when treatment started. The drug still worked.

Subsequent studies showed that even short courses of rapamycin—3 months of treatment in middle-aged mice—could extend life expectancy by up to 60% and improve measurable health outcomes including cardiac function, cognitive performance, and immune function. One male mouse in a 2016 eLife study survived to approximately 1,400 days—nearly 4 years, extraordinarily old for a mouse.

Human trials have begun cautiously. Small studies in older adults have tested rapamycin or its analogs (rapalogs) for improvements in immune function and markers of aging. The results are preliminary but have attracted serious attention. Side effects—including hyperlipidemia, impaired wound healing, and potential metabolic effects—mean that the risk-benefit calculation for human use is not straightforward. No large-scale clinical trial of rapamycin for anti-aging in humans has been completed.

A compound discovered in Easter Island soil in 1964, filed away, and nearly forgotten, may turn out to be one of the most consequential pharmaceutical molecules in the history of aging research. The investigation continues.

## Key Facts

- Rapamycin was discovered from *Streptomyces hygroscopicus* in Easter Island soil in 1964; named after Rapa Nui; FDA-approved for organ transplant rejection in 1999
- Mechanism: inhibits mTORC1, shifting cells from growth mode to repair/autophagy mode—pharmacologically mimicking caloric restriction
- 2009 NIA study: rapamycin started at 600 days (late middle age) extended median lifespan 9–14% and maximum lifespan in genetically heterogeneous mice
- 2016 study: 3-month treatment extended life expectancy up to 60% and improved healthspan; short-term treatment as effective as lifelong dosing
- Human trials underway but limited; side effects include hyperlipidemia and impaired wound healing; no approved anti-aging indication yet
