# K9: The Thread That Heals

*Category: Biomimetics & Materials*

## The Story

Silk has been spun into fabric for at least 5,000 years, making it one of the oldest biotechnological industries in human history. For millennia, the interest was purely aesthetic and commercial: the luster, strength, and lightweight drape of silk fabric made it the most valuable textile in the ancient world, driving trade routes across Asia and the Middle East. The chemistry that makes silk remarkable as a fabric turns out to make it equally remarkable as a biomedical material.

Silk fibroin — the structural protein that makes up the core of the silkworm *Bombyx mori* fiber — is a large protein with an unusual architecture: long stretches of repetitive hydrophobic amino acid sequences (primarily glycine-alanine-glycine-alanine-glycine-serine repeats) that form tightly packed beta-sheet crystal domains, interspersed with amorphous regions of more varied sequence. The crystalline domains give silk its tensile strength — roughly 500 megapascals, comparable to synthetic nylon — while the amorphous regions allow flexibility and processing into diverse forms.

Processed away from the sericin coating (which causes immune reactions) and formed into films, hydrogels, electrospun fibers, or particles, silk fibroin is biocompatible, biodegradable, and mechanically tunable. The degradation rate can be controlled from weeks to years by altering the crystallinity — more beta-sheet content degrades slower. This tunability makes silk fibroin a versatile platform for implantable devices with different required lifetimes.

The FDA cleared silk sutures decades ago; *Bombyx mori* silk has been used in surgery for over a century. But modern silk biomedical applications go far beyond sutures. Research groups, particularly those led by Fiorenzo Omenetto and David Kaplan at Tufts University, have developed silk-based platforms for:

Drug delivery: silk fibroin particles loaded with pharmaceutical agents release their payload as the protein degrades, with release kinetics controllable by crystallinity. Temperature-sensitive drugs that cannot survive conventional processing can be encapsulated in silk at mild conditions and protected from degradation.

Tissue engineering: silk scaffolds support the growth of cartilage, bone, tendon, ligament, and corneal tissue in vitro, and implanted scaffolds degrade as native tissue grows in to replace them. Clinical trials for silk-based meniscus replacements and bone substitutes are underway.

Perhaps most surprisingly, resorbable electronics. Omenetto's group demonstrated that electronic circuits — transistors, antennas, sensors — fabricated on silk fibroin substrates can be implanted in tissue and dissolve completely over days to weeks, leaving no hardware to retrieve. Transient implants that monitor neural activity, track wound healing, or deliver localized drug or electrical stimulation after surgery, then disappear entirely, represent a category of medical device that was previously impossible. Several FDA-cleared silk-based implants are now commercially available, including absorbable nerve wraps and drug delivery matrices.

The 5,000-year-old textile industry has produced the raw material for twenty-first century medicine. The silkworm spent 50 million years evolving a protein optimized for structural performance. Biomedical engineers are borrowing it.

## Key Facts

- Silk fibroin has tensile strength comparable to nylon (~500 MPa), is biocompatible, biodegradable, and processable into films, hydrogels, particles, and fibers
- Degradation rate is tunable from weeks to years by controlling beta-sheet crystallinity — critical for matching implant lifetime to clinical application
- Applications include drug delivery particles, tissue engineering scaffolds for cartilage/bone/tendon, and resorbable electronics that dissolve after implant
- Tufts University groups (Omenetto, Kaplan) demonstrated complete silk-based electronic circuits that implant, function, and dissolve in tissue
- Multiple FDA-cleared silk fibroin implants are commercially available, including nerve wraps and drug delivery matrices
