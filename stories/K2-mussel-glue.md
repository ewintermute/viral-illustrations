# K2: The Glue That Works Underwater

*Category: Biomimetics & Materials*

## The Story

Any marine biologist who has tried to remove a mussel from a rock knows the problem: they don't want to come off. Mussels attach to intertidal rocks, ship hulls, and dock pilings in conditions that would defeat every conventional adhesive — constant wave action, complete immersion in salt water, fouling by algae and biofilm. They hold on through all of it, in some cases with forces measured in the tens of newtons. The mechanism they use, identified and decoded over the past three decades, may become the basis for surgical glues that save lives.

The mussel byssus is the attachment apparatus — a bundle of protein threads, each anchored to a hard substrate by an adhesive plaque about 3 millimeters wide. The threads themselves are remarkable viscoelastic structures that combine stiffness and extensibility in a gradient, preventing breakage under the shock loading of waves. But the adhesive plaque is the critical innovation.

Mussels produce a set of proteins called mussel adhesive proteins (MAPs), secreted through a groove in the foot and deposited in the plaque region. These proteins are rich in an unusual amino acid: 3,4-dihydroxy-L-phenylalanine, universally abbreviated DOPA. DOPA is formed by the post-translational modification of ordinary tyrosine residues by an enzyme called DOPA oxidase. What makes DOPA remarkable is its chemistry: it has two adjacent hydroxyl groups that can simultaneously form hydrogen bonds with oxide-rich mineral surfaces, form covalent bonds with proteins via oxidation reactions, and chelate metal ions. In a single amino acid, the mussel achieves anchoring to mineral, cross-linking to protein matrix, and coordination to metal ions — a three-mode adhesion chemistry.

Water, normally fatal to adhesive performance because it disrupts the hydrogen bonds that most adhesives depend on, does not disrupt DOPA-based adhesion. The covalent bonds and coordination chemistry that DOPA forms are water-stable. This property is what makes mussel adhesion the focus of intense biomedical interest: surgeons routinely need to seal and bond tissues in wet, protein-rich, mechanically stressed environments — exactly the conditions that defeat current tissue adhesives.

Clinical surgery currently relies on fibrin glues (which have limited strength and are slow to polymerize), cyanoacrylates (which are cytotoxic at many concentrations), and sutures and staples (which create additional tissue trauma). A DOPA-inspired surgical adhesive that bonds strongly to wet tissue, is biocompatible, biodegrades as the wound heals, and can be deployed rapidly would represent a significant advance.

Multiple research groups have produced DOPA-containing polymers with promising properties. A Purdue University group developed a non-toxic underwater adhesive based on a DOPA-modified polymer that achieved adhesion strength on wet tissue comparable to commercial surgical glues with better biocompatibility. Applications being tested include incision closure, attachment of hernia mesh, and — most critically — sealing ruptures in fetal membranes. Premature rupture of membranes is a major cause of preterm birth; currently there is no reliable way to seal the amniotic sac once it ruptures. A DOPA-based adhesive that bonds the fetal membrane to itself in the uterine environment could address an unmet clinical need with enormous consequences.

## Key Facts

- Mussel adhesion depends on DOPA — 3,4-dihydroxy-L-phenylalanine — which forms hydrogen bonds, covalent bonds, and metal coordination simultaneously, allowing strong wet adhesion
- DOPA-based adhesion is water-resistant because its covalent and coordination bonds are not disrupted by aqueous environments
- Synthetic DOPA-containing polymers have achieved adhesion strength on wet tissue comparable to commercial surgical glues with better biocompatibility
- Key applications under development include incision closure, hernia mesh attachment, and sealing ruptured fetal membranes to prevent preterm birth
- The mussel byssus thread architecture also provides a graded stiffness from plaque to body — an additional biomimetic concept being applied to impact-resistant fibers
