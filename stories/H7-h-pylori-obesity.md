# H7: The Bacterium We Killed — and What Came Next

*Category: The Microbiome*

## The Story

For the better part of the twentieth century, stomach ulcers were understood as the result of stress, spicy food, and excess stomach acid — a lifestyle disease managed with antacids and lifestyle changes. In 1984, Barry Marshall did something dramatic to prove otherwise: he drank a solution of *Helicobacter pylori*, developed gastritis, treated himself with antibiotics, and recovered. The message was clear. A bacterium caused ulcers. The Nobel Prize followed in 2005.

The public health response was swift. *H. pylori* eradication became standard treatment for peptic ulcer disease and later for early-stage gastric cancer. Antibiotic regimens combining clarithromycin, amoxicillin, and a proton pump inhibitor became routine. In the 1990s and 2000s, *H. pylori* infection rates in developed countries dropped dramatically as eradication therapy spread and as improved sanitation reduced transmission. The story seemed like a straightforward triumph of microbiology over disease.

Then researchers began noticing some unusual trends. Obesity rates in Western countries had been rising since the 1980s. Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), which had been relatively uncommon, became epidemic. And the timing tracked suspiciously with the decline of *H. pylori*.

The connection runs through ghrelin. Ghrelin is the hunger hormone — the primary appetite-stimulating signal produced by cells in the stomach lining, responsible for the sensation of hunger before meals. *H. pylori* infection suppresses ghrelin secretion, particularly after eating; infected individuals show a blunted post-meal ghrelin peak. When *H. pylori* is eradicated, ghrelin signaling is restored to baseline — or beyond. Multiple studies have documented significant increases in fasting and post-meal ghrelin levels following successful *H. pylori* eradication.

A 2011 study published in BMC Gastroenterology examined meal-associated ghrelin changes in patients before and after eradication therapy. Patients showed significantly increased acylated ghrelin responses after eradication, along with increased appetite and — in some subgroups — weight gain over follow-up. Body mass index increases of one to two units over the year following eradication have been reported in several cohort studies, though the effect is inconsistent and varies with pre-treatment BMI, diet, and other factors.

The GERD connection involves a different mechanism. *H. pylori* infection, by stimulating gastric inflammation, reduces stomach acid secretion over time. Eradication removes this suppression, and acid secretion normalizes or rebounds. For patients with pre-existing conditions making them susceptible to reflux, this restoration of normal acid can trigger or worsen GERD. The 1990s rise in GERD diagnoses tracks closely with the spread of *H. pylori* eradication in North America and Europe.

Martin Blaser at New York University has been the most prominent advocate for reconsidering *H. pylori* as a partially beneficial commensal rather than a pure pathogen. His argument, presented in his 2014 book *Missing Microbes*, is that *H. pylori* co-evolved with humans for at least 60,000 years and that its disappearance — along with other "old friend" microbes lost to modern hygiene and antibiotics — has contributed to the rise of obesity, GERD, asthma, and inflammatory bowel disease. The hypothesis remains controversial; *H. pylori* also causes gastric cancer in a substantial fraction of infected individuals, and the cancer prevention benefits of eradication are real and large.

The story is not that *H. pylori* should not be eradicated. It is that eliminating a microbe with a 60,000-year relationship to human physiology produces consequences that were not anticipated — a lesson that will apply to every microbiome intervention that follows.

## Key Facts

- *H. pylori* suppresses ghrelin (the hunger hormone) post-meal; eradication restores and can elevate ghrelin, driving increased appetite and modest weight gain in some patients
- Multiple studies document BMI increases of 1–2 units in the year following *H. pylori* eradication, though effects are variable
- The temporal rise of GERD in Western countries tracks the decline of *H. pylori*, linked to acid hypersecretion following eradication
- *H. pylori* has co-evolved with humans for at least 60,000 years; its disappearance from most Westernized guts is a recent and rapid change
- The tradeoff is real: eradication prevents gastric cancer but may contribute to obesity and GERD risk
