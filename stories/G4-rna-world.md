# G4: Life May Have Started With RNA — And the Evidence Is Still Inside You

*Category: Origins & Identity*

## The Story

Here is the chicken-and-egg problem at the heart of the origin of life: DNA encodes the instructions for making proteins. Proteins are required to copy DNA. Neither can exist without the other. So which came first?

The answer biology has converged on is: neither. Something older came first. Something that could do both jobs at once. That something is RNA.

The RNA world hypothesis, first coherently articulated by Alexander Rich in 1962 and named by Walter Gilbert in 1986, proposes that the earliest life on Earth was based entirely on RNA molecules. RNA has two properties that make this possible. It can store genetic information, like DNA. And it can catalyze chemical reactions, like proteins. RNA molecules with catalytic activity — called ribozymes — were discovered by Thomas Cech and Sidney Altman in the early 1980s, a finding that won them the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1989.

The existence of ribozymes proved that RNA could do what the hypothesis required. But the most compelling piece of evidence is something that has been sitting in every cell on Earth since before there were cells with nuclei: the ribosome.

The ribosome is the universal machine for protein synthesis. Every organism that has ever lived — bacteria, archaea, eukaryotes — uses ribosomes to translate genetic code into protein. And at the heart of every ribosome, performing the actual chemistry of linking amino acids together to form a protein chain, is not a protein but an RNA molecule. The ribosome is, fundamentally, a ribozyme. The proteins surrounding it are scaffolding. The RNA is the engine.

This is a relic. It is the molecular fossil of an era when RNA did everything. When the ribosome first evolved, there were no proteins to do the work — because the ribosome is the machine that makes proteins. The ribosome had to be an RNA machine first, before proteins existed. It has been running ever since, and nothing has replaced it, because the system is too deeply embedded in the metabolism of all life to be rewired.

In 2001, when researchers finally solved the three-dimensional structure of the ribosome at atomic resolution — work that earned a Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 2009 — they found that the active site where peptide bonds form contains no protein side chains within catalytic distance. The reaction is performed entirely by RNA. The ribosome is 2.5 million daltons of molecular machinery, and the part that actually does the chemistry is the ancient RNA core.

More recent work has pushed the origin story back further. Researchers have identified a small, conserved pocket-like RNA structure deep in the ribosome's active site that may represent a prebiotic proto-ribosome — a minimal RNA-based peptide-bonding machine that predated the full ribosomal apparatus. Laboratory experiments have produced RNA molecules that can nearly self-replicate by copying themselves and their complements. The gap between chemistry and life, at the RNA level, is narrowing.

The challenges remain real: synthesizing RNA from prebiotic chemicals is difficult, and explaining how complex RNA sequences could arise without existing enzymes requires careful experimental support. But the core logic of the RNA world — that RNA came before DNA and proteins, and that the ribosome is the surviving evidence — is now as well-supported as any hypothesis about events 4 billion years in the past can be.

Every time a ribosome in your cells stitches two amino acids together, it is running a molecular machine that is older than DNA, older than cells as we know them, and older than anything we can see in the fossil record. Life began with RNA. The RNA is still there.

## Key Facts

- The RNA world hypothesis: RNA preceded both DNA and proteins, acting as both genetic material and catalyst in the first living systems.
- Ribozymes (catalytic RNA molecules) were discovered by Thomas Cech and Sidney Altman, Nobel Prize 1989.
- The ribosome — universal to all life — catalyzes protein synthesis using RNA, not protein, at its active site; it is a molecular fossil of the RNA world.
- The ribosomal active site contains no protein side chains within catalytic distance of the peptide bond-forming reaction.
- Walter Gilbert coined the term "RNA world" in 1986; the hypothesis was first articulated by Alexander Rich in 1962.
