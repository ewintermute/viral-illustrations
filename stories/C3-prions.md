# C3: The Fold That Kills

*Category: Dangerous Biology*

## The Story

In 1982, Stanley Prusiner published a paper in *Science* proposing that a class of fatal neurological diseases was caused by a protein. Not a virus. Not a bacterium. Not a fungus or a parasite. A protein—a molecule entirely devoid of nucleic acid, with no genome, no gene, no code to copy. The scientific establishment reacted with disbelief so profound that Prusiner spent the following decade defending his hypothesis against ridicule. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in 1997. The hypothesis was correct.

Prions are misfolded versions of an ordinary cellular protein called PrP (prion protein), normally found on the surface of neurons throughout the mammalian brain. In its normal form, PrP^C, the protein is alpha-helical and harmless—what it does under normal conditions remains incompletely understood. But under certain circumstances, PrP^C undergoes a dramatic conformational change, shifting from its normal alpha-helical structure into a beta-sheet-rich form called PrP^Sc (from "scrapie," the sheep prion disease). This misfolded form is resistant to degradation, accumulates in neural tissue, and—most disturbingly—acts as a template. When PrP^Sc comes into contact with normal PrP^C, it induces the same conformational change, converting the normal protein to misfolded form. The process propagates exponentially.

The end result is catastrophic. Accumulating PrP^Sc aggregates trigger neuronal death, immune activation, and the characteristic "spongiform" pathology visible under the microscope—holes throughout the brain tissue, a spongy appearance that gives prion diseases the collective name transmissible spongiform encephalopathies (TSEs). The diseases are invariably fatal, and no effective treatment exists.

Human prion diseases include Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD), fatal familial insomnia (FFI), Gerstmann-Sträussler-Scheinker syndrome (GSS), and kuru. Kuru was once epidemic among the Fore people of Papua New Guinea, transmitted through ritual cannibalism—the consumption of deceased relatives' brains as a mark of respect. When the practice was abandoned in the 1950s following identification of the disease cause, kuru slowly disappeared, though cases continued for decades as the incubation period of prion disease can last 30 or more years.

In the United Kingdom in the 1990s, variant CJD emerged in young people who had eaten beef products contaminated with bovine spongiform encephalopathy (BSE, "mad cow disease"). BSE had spread through the British cattle population via feed containing rendered meat-and-bone meal from infected animals—essentially, cattle were being fed other cattle, and the prion propagated through the food supply before the practice was identified and banned. The vCJD outbreak ultimately killed 178 people in the UK, with additional cases in France and other countries.

Prions are extraordinarily resistant to conventional sterilization. Standard autoclaving at 121°C for 20 minutes does not inactivate them. UV radiation does not damage them—they have no nucleic acid to damage. Formalin fixation preserves their infectivity in tissue samples. Effective decontamination requires extended autoclaving at 134°C for 18 minutes in a prevacuum sterilizer, soaking in 1N sodium hydroxide for an hour, or treatment with 20,000 ppm sodium hypochlorite—protocols that would destroy most conventional pathogens many times over. For this reason, prion contamination of neurosurgical instruments is considered among the most serious infection control challenges in hospital settings.

As of 2026, no disease-modifying treatment exists. Research has shown that certain experimental approaches—including silencing the PRNP gene using a technology called CHARM, and delivery of antiprion antibodies—can halt or reverse prion disease progression in mice. Human trials remain distant. The disease that is caused by nothing more than a folded protein remains, nearly five decades after Prusiner's insight, essentially untreatable.

## Key Facts

- Prions are misfolded versions of the normal cellular protein PrP^C, with no nucleic acid; they propagate by converting normal PrP^C into the misfolded PrP^Sc form
- All prion diseases (CJD, kuru, BSE/vCJD, fatal familial insomnia) are invariably fatal with no approved curative treatment
- Standard autoclave (121°C, 20 min) and UV radiation do not inactivate prions; decontamination requires 134°C extended protocols or NaOH treatment
- Kuru spread via ritual cannibalism among the Fore people of Papua New Guinea; variant CJD emerged from BSE-contaminated beef in the 1990s UK outbreak (178 deaths)
- Experimental CHARM gene-silencing technology suppresses PrP in neurons and halts prion disease in mice; no approved human therapy exists
