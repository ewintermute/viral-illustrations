# E18: The Strongest Thread

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

Pound for pound, dragline spider silk is one of the strongest materials in nature—with tensile strength up to 1.65 gigapascals and elasticity that allows it to stretch to four times its original length before breaking. A thread of dragline silk half a millimeter thick could suspend a small child. A hypothetical web of fibers scaled up to the diameter of a pencil could stop a Boeing 747.

The properties that make spider silk so remarkable emerge from its molecular architecture: a hierarchical structure of crystalline beta-sheet domains (providing tensile strength) embedded in amorphous regions (providing extensibility and toughness), self-assembled from proteins called spidroins through a sophisticated spinning process involving pH changes and shear forces in the spider's silk duct. Steel is stronger in absolute terms—the strongest steels can exceed 6 GPa—but steel is also 7 times denser than silk. By weight, silk wins dramatically.

For at least two centuries, humans have tried to harvest spider silk for industrial purposes. The difficulty is that spiders are intensely territorial and cannibalistic. You cannot farm them in high-density conditions the way silkworms are farmed; they will eat each other. The French tried spider silk fabric in the 18th century (with limited success) and again in the early 20th (a spectacular cape at the Victoria and Albert Museum, produced from one million hand-collected Madagascar golden silk orb-weaver spiders). The economics have always defeated the biology.

The solution is to stop trying to use spiders and instead put spider genes into organisms that can actually be farmed. Researchers have expressed spider silk protein genes in *E. coli*, *Bacillus megaterium*, yeast, and even tobacco plants. *E. coli* is the most common host; engineered strains can produce spider silk proteins up to 378 kilodaltons in size, with yields reaching into grams per liter under optimized fermentation conditions. *Bacillus megaterium* has an advantage: it secretes the protein into the culture medium rather than accumulating it intracellularly, simplifying purification.

The main commercial approach has been genetically engineered silkworms. Companies like Kraig Biocraft Laboratories have inserted spider silk protein genes into silkworms, producing hybrid fibers—"Dragon Silk"—that are stronger and more flexible than natural silkworm silk. The silkworm's native spinning machinery already handles the mechanics of fiber formation, addressing one of the hardest challenges in recombinant silk production: the proteins must be assembled under specific conditions into ordered structures. Silkworms do this naturally.

Applications are multiple and compelling: lightweight body armor stronger and tougher than Kevlar; biodegradable surgical sutures that match the mechanical properties of tendons; scaffolds for tissue engineering that mimic the extracellular matrix better than synthetic polymers; high-performance parachute cords and aerospace fabrics. The military connection has been present from the beginning—the US Army has funded Kraig Biocraft's development work, interested in body armor that outperforms existing materials.

Commercial-scale production remains challenging. The spinning process cannot simply be replicated in a tank: the silk protein must be kept in solution until it encounters the specific mechanical and chemical conditions—the pH drop, the ion concentration change, the extensional flow—that trigger it to crystallize into fiber. Getting this right at industrial scale is a materials science and engineering challenge that has not yet been fully solved. But the fundamental biology—growing the protein in bacteria or silkworms—is working. The thread that stops bullets may be closer than it seems.

## Key Facts

- Dragline spider silk has tensile strength up to 1.65 GPa and can extend to 4× its length before breaking; pound-for-pound stronger than most steels
- Spider farming is impossible due to territorial behavior and cannibalism; recombinant production in *E. coli*, silkworms, and other organisms is the viable path
- AMSilk (*E. coli* fermentation) and Kraig Biocraft (transgenic silkworms) have reached small commercial scale; US Military contracts for Dragon Silk™ have been signed
- The spider silk protein must be assembled under specific pH, ion, and mechanical conditions to form ordered fibers—replicating this at industrial scale remains the key unsolved challenge
- Applications include body armor, biodegradable sutures, tissue engineering scaffolds, and aerospace materials
