# D5: Why Sex Exists

*Category: Evolutionary Marvels*

## The Story

Sexual reproduction is, from a strictly arithmetic perspective, a terrible strategy. Consider the arithmetic: in an asexual population, every individual produces offspring. Every female in a sexual population produces only half as many reproductive daughters as an otherwise identical asexual female, because half her offspring are sons—who produce no eggs of their own. An asexual mutant appearing in a sexual population would double its reproductive output every generation and sweep through the population in a matter of dozens of generations. And yet, across a billion years of animal evolution, sex has persisted almost universally.

This is called the twofold cost of sex, and it is one of the central paradoxes in evolutionary biology. The question is not why asexual reproduction exists—it clearly works, and bdelloid rotifers, some lizard species, and certain plants do quite well with it—but why sexual reproduction, with its enormous overhead, is so dominant. There must be something that sex provides that is worth paying twice as much for.

The leading answer, supported by both theoretical work and experimental data, is the Red Queen hypothesis, named after the character in Lewis Carroll's *Through the Looking-Glass* who must keep running to stay in the same place. The hypothesis was formalized in the early 1970s by biologists Leigh Van Valen and later developed by W.D. Hamilton and others. Its core claim: parasites are the reason sex exists.

Parasites coevolve with their hosts. A parasite that evolves the ability to exploit a host genotype will spread rapidly through a population of clones carrying that genotype—because clones are all vulnerable to the same attack. Sexual reproduction, which creates new genotype combinations every generation through recombination and independent assortment, keeps the host population genetically diverse. No single parasite can adapt fast enough to attack every novel combination simultaneously. Sex is a moving target.

The experimental evidence for this is striking. In 2011, Levi Morran and colleagues at Indiana University published a study in *Science* showing that populations of the nematode *Caenorhabditis elegans*—which can reproduce either asexually (by self-fertilization) or sexually (by outcrossing)—rapidly evolved toward increased outcrossing when coexposed to their pathogen *Serratia marcescens*. Populations under parasite pressure became more sexual; populations without the parasite did not. Self-fertilizing strains went extinct. The parasites drove the evolution of sex in real time.

Field evidence from New Zealand snail populations (*Potamopyrgus antipodarum*) provided complementary support: sexual reproduction in this species is most prevalent in exactly those lake habitats where parasitic trematode infection rates are highest. Sexuality tracks parasitism across ecologies.

The Red Queen does not mean that sex evolved solely to fight parasites—other hypotheses, including the benefit of purging deleterious mutations (Muller's ratchet) and the advantage of combining beneficial mutations across lineages, are also supported. But the parasite coevolution arm of the explanation has now been tested experimentally in ways that make it among the best-supported answers to a question that seemed, for decades, potentially unanswerable.

Evolution pays a price for sex every generation. Parasites make it worth paying.

## Key Facts

- The "twofold cost of sex" means asexual females produce twice as many reproductive daughters per generation as sexual females—sex should be eliminated by selection, but isn't
- The Red Queen hypothesis proposes that parasite coevolution maintains sex by ensuring common genotypes are constantly attacked, making genetic diversity via sex essential
- *C. elegans* populations exposed to *Serratia marcescens* parasites evolved rapidly toward sexual outcrossing; self-fertilizing strains went extinct under parasite pressure (Morran et al., *Science* 2011)
- New Zealand freshwater snail (*Potamopyrgus antipodarum*) sexual reproduction rates correlate with local trematode parasite prevalence
- Additional benefits of sex include purging deleterious mutations (Muller's ratchet) and combining beneficial mutations that would otherwise stay in separate lineages
