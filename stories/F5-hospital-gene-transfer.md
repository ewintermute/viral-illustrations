# F5: The Resistance That Travels Between Species

*Category: Stranger Than Fiction*

## The Story

In a hospital room in Pittsburgh, two patients occupy beds on the same ward. One is infected with *Klebsiella pneumoniae* carrying a plasmid encoding resistance to carbapenem antibiotics—the last-resort drugs reserved for infections that nothing else can kill. The other patient is colonized with *Escherichia coli*, ordinarily sensitive to carbapenems. Within days, both patients' bacteria carry the resistance plasmid. It was not transmitted from one patient to the other through contaminated surfaces or unwashed hands. The plasmid moved directly from *Klebsiella* into *E. coli*—between different species of bacteria—in the gut of one of the patients.

This is horizontal gene transfer in action, and it is happening in hospitals around the world, continuously and largely invisibly.

The mechanism is conjugation. A donor bacterium extends a protein appendage called a pilus to make physical contact with a recipient bacterium. Through this connection, a copy of the resistance plasmid is transferred from donor to recipient. The transferred DNA integrates into the recipient's plasmid pool and is replicated along with the rest of its genome. Within hours, the recipient bacterium is resistant. Unlike a mutation, which requires the fortunate accident of random DNA change, conjugation is a directed process: the plasmid contains genes that encode its own transfer machinery. Resistance genes are, in a sense, infectious.

A 2020 study published in *eLife* tracked 2,173 bacterial isolates from a Pittsburgh hospital across two years, using genomic sequencing to identify identical plasmids in different bacterial species. They found clear evidence of horizontal transfer events within individual patients—the same resistance plasmid appearing in two different genera of bacteria isolated from the same patient's gut microbiome. About 80% of the sequences shared between different bacterial species encoded HGT-related functions (plasmid replication, transfer genes, transposases), and about 25% of those shared sequences carried resistance to multiple antibiotic classes.

The same year, German researchers published a 3-year study of a hospital outbreak involving carbapenem-resistant bacteria: a resistance plasmid had fused with another mobile element and transferred between *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* and *Citrobacter freundii*—genera that are not at all closely related—within the hospital environment. The Oxford University group has developed methods to detect conjugation events in mixed bacterial communities in real time, finding that resistance genes spread more widely between species than previously estimated.

The implications reshape how we think about antibiotic resistance. The naive model is that resistant bacteria spread from patient to patient—through contaminated hands, surfaces, medical equipment. Infection control practices are designed to prevent this. But horizontal gene transfer within patients creates a different route: a plasmid can move from a commensal bacterium in your gut microbiome into a pathogen you've just been colonized with, converting a sensitive pathogen into a resistant one entirely within your body. The plasmid travels, not just the bacterium.

A hospital ward is not just a collection of individuals with different bacteria. It is a bacterial ecosystem with a shared genetic pool. Antibiotic treatment selects for resistance, but horizontal transfer distributes resistance across species boundaries in ways that no amount of individual patient hygiene can prevent. The solution, when it comes, will probably require not just controlling which bacteria patients carry, but understanding and managing the mobile genetic elements those bacteria trade.

The plasmid does not know it is crossing a species boundary. It is simply doing what it evolved to do.

## Key Facts

- Conjugation transfers resistance plasmids directly between different bacterial species within single patients' gut microbiomes—without requiring transmission between patients
- A 2020 Pittsburgh hospital study found the same carbapenem-resistance plasmids in *Klebsiella pneumoniae* and *E. coli* isolated from the same patient, confirming in-patient cross-species transfer
- German hospital study documented resistance plasmid transfer between *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* and *Citrobacter freundii* (distantly related genera) over 3 years
- ~80% of sequences shared between different hospital bacterial species encode HGT functions; ~25% carry multi-class resistance
- Antibiotic use in hospitals selects for resistance and drives plasmid acquisition; infection control designed to limit patient-to-patient spread does not prevent intra-patient cross-species transfer
