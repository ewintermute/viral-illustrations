# H3: The Bacteria That Know Before You Do

*Category: The Microbiome*

## The Story

Colorectal cancer kills around 900,000 people per year worldwide. A significant portion of those deaths are preventable with early detection — caught at stage I, the five-year survival rate exceeds 90%; caught at stage IV, it drops below 15%. The problem is that colonoscopy, the current gold standard for detection, is invasive, expensive, and poorly utilized. In the United States, roughly one-third of eligible adults are not up to date with screening. A simple, accurate, non-invasive test for early colorectal cancer would be one of the most valuable tools in cancer prevention.

The gut microbiome may provide one.

Research over the past decade has identified distinctive bacterial signatures in the gut communities of people with colorectal cancer — and, crucially, in people with precancerous polyps, before any cancer has developed. The signatures are detectable in stool samples, making them accessible without any invasive procedure. The bacterium *Fusobacterium nucleatum*, a normally oral microbe, is consistently elevated in colorectal tumors and in the stool of patients with colorectal cancer. *Peptostreptococcus anaerobius*, *Parvimonas micra*, and several other species are similarly enriched. The community shift is not subtle; colorectal cancer patients' microbiomes differ measurably from those of healthy controls in multiple independent cohorts.

The mechanistic relationship between these bacteria and cancer is partially understood. *F. nucleatum* expresses surface adhesins that bind to E-cadherin on colorectal epithelial cells, promoting invasion and activating Wnt signaling — a pathway that drives cell proliferation. The bacterium also suppresses anti-tumor immune responses within the tumor microenvironment. Whether *F. nucleatum* is a cause of colorectal cancer, an opportunistic colonizer of emerging tumors, or both remains debated, but the association is robust across dozens of studies and multiple continents.

Diagnostically, the bacterial signatures have shown promising but imperfect performance. A systematic review published in 2022 found that microbiome-based classifiers achieved area-under-the-curve values of roughly 0.75–0.85 in distinguishing colorectal cancer patients from healthy controls — meaningful discrimination, but not yet superior to the fecal immunochemical test (FIT) for blood detection. The key advance is combination: when microbiome data are combined with FIT or other biomarkers, detection sensitivity rises substantially. A multi-marker panel approach can push performance into the range needed for practical screening.

Early-onset colorectal cancer — cases in patients under 50, a growing trend — shows distinct microbiome signatures from late-onset cases, suggesting that the microbial contribution may be especially prominent in younger patients. Studies presented at ASCO 2024 noted that the gut microbiome signatures associated with early-onset colorectal cancer differed significantly from late-onset cases and may help explain the otherwise puzzling rise in incidence among younger adults who have not yet reached standard screening age.

The practical goal is a test that patients would actually take: a stool sample analyzed for a panel of bacterial markers, inflammatory proteins, and possibly shed DNA, capable of identifying individuals who need colonoscopy follow-up. Several such tests are in development or early clinical deployment. The microbiome is not just a bystander in colorectal cancer. It may be the earliest warning signal the disease produces.

## Key Facts

- Distinctive bacterial signatures — particularly *Fusobacterium nucleatum* elevation — are detectable in stool before colorectal cancer becomes symptomatic
- Microbiome classifiers achieve AUC of 0.75–0.85 alone; combining microbiome data with existing tests like FIT substantially improves early detection
- *F. nucleatum* binds to colorectal epithelial cells, activates Wnt proliferation signaling, and suppresses anti-tumor immunity within tumors
- Early-onset colorectal cancer (under age 50) shows distinct microbiome signatures from late-onset cases
- A non-invasive stool microbiome test combined with other biomarkers is the goal; several multi-marker panels are in clinical development
