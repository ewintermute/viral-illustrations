# C5: The Drug Pipeline Running Dry

*Category: Dangerous Biology*

## The Story

In 1928, Alexander Fleming noticed that a mold contaminating his petri dish was killing the bacteria around it. Penicillin followed, then streptomycin, then tetracycline, chloramphenicol, erythromycin. From the 1940s through the 1970s, new antibiotic classes arrived at roughly one per decade, each tackling a different bacterial target. Bacterial infections that had killed humans throughout history were suddenly treatable. Surgical procedures that would have been suicidal without infection control became routine. The age of antibiotics reshaped medicine more completely than almost any other development.

Then, around 1980, the flow of genuinely new antibiotic classes effectively stopped. The phenomenon has a name: Eroom's Law—a wry inversion of Moore's Law. Where Moore's Law describes the exponential improvement in computing power, Eroom's Law describes the inverse trend in drug development productivity: each decade, fewer new drugs reach approval per dollar and per year of research invested. The pharmaceutical industry, having harvested the most accessible molecules from soil bacteria and fungi, found itself facing diminishing returns and retreating from antibiotic development entirely. Between 1987 and 2000, not a single new antibiotic class was approved by the FDA.

Bacteria, meanwhile, were not waiting. Evolution that had required millions of years in natural environments was now occurring in months in hospital wards, animal farms, and communities where antibiotics were freely prescribed or sold without prescription. The mechanisms bacteria use to resist antibiotics are themselves diverse and elegant: enzymes that destroy antibiotic molecules (beta-lactamases, which cleave the core ring of penicillin-class drugs), pumps that export antibiotics from the cell before they can act, mutations in the target molecules that reduce antibiotic binding, and acquisition of resistance genes from neighboring bacteria through horizontal gene transfer.

The scope of the crisis has been quantified in escalating estimates. A 2014 report commissioned by the UK government, led by economist Jim O'Neill, projected that antibiotic-resistant infections would kill 10 million people per year by 2050 if current trends continued—more than cancer. A 2022 study in *The Lancet* estimated that 1.27 million deaths in 2019 were directly attributable to antibiotic-resistant infections, with another 3.7 million deaths associated with such infections. A 2024 update projected that by 2050, AMR (antimicrobial resistance) could be directly responsible for up to 1.91 million deaths annually, with 8.22 million deaths associated. The World Bank estimates AMR could cost the global economy up to $3.4 trillion annually by 2050.

The drug pipeline crisis compounds the biological crisis. The major pharmaceutical companies have largely exited antibiotic development because the business model doesn't work: antibiotics are prescribed for days or weeks and generate far less revenue than drugs for chronic conditions taken for decades. The few antibiotic drugs that do reach approval are typically held in reserve as last-resort treatments—meaning their commercial use is deliberately limited, further reducing the financial return on the billion-dollar development investment.

The responses being developed are varied: new phage therapies that use viruses to target bacteria, attempts to revive interest through public-private partnerships and guaranteed purchase commitments, genomics-guided discovery of novel antibiotic targets in bacterial genomes, and antimicrobial peptides from natural sources. Some of these will succeed. The question is whether the pace of innovation will outrun the pace of resistance evolution in the organisms that cause the infections that now kill more people each year than HIV, tuberculosis, and malaria combined.

## Key Facts

- No new antibiotic class was approved by the FDA between 1987 and 2000; the "antibiotic golden age" effectively ended around 1980—a trend called Eroom's Law
- A 2022 *Lancet* study attributed 1.27 million deaths in 2019 directly to antibiotic-resistant infections, with 3.7 million associated deaths
- Projections suggest AMR could directly cause 1.91 million deaths annually and be associated with 8.22 million deaths per year by 2050
- Resistance mechanisms include beta-lactamase enzymes (cleave penicillin), efflux pumps, target mutations, and horizontal gene transfer of resistance genes
- Major pharmaceutical companies have largely withdrawn from antibiotic R&D because short-course drugs generate far less revenue than chronic-condition treatments
