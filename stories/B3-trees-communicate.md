# B3: The Forest Internet

*Category: Hidden Complexity*

## The Story

Beneath every temperate forest lies a communications network older than the internet by several hundred million years. Thread by thread, fungal hyphae—filaments far thinner than a human hair—weave between soil particles and root tips, linking trees into a continent-spanning web that can carry chemical signals, nutrients, and even warnings from one tree to another. Scientists call it the mycorrhizal network. Journalists call it the Wood Wide Web. Both names stick because both capture something genuinely astonishing.

The fungal partners in this network are mycorrhizae, organisms that live in intimate association with plant roots. In temperate forests dominated by Douglas fir, pine, and beech, ectomycorrhizal fungi (EM fungi) are the key players. Their hyphae physically fuse with root cells, forming a shared interface where carbon from the tree flows out and minerals like phosphorus and nitrogen flow in. But the relationship extends well beyond nutrition. When a Douglas fir is attacked by western spruce budworm, it doesn't suffer in silence. Within hours, chemical signals—likely jasmonic acid or salicylic acid derivatives—travel through the mycorrhizal network to neighboring ponderosa pines, prompting those trees to upregulate their own defensive enzymes and tannins. The recipient trees were never touched by the pest. They simply received the warning.

Experiments published in *Scientific Reports* have demonstrated this directly: tree pairs connected by EM networks show synchronized defensive responses after one is stressed, while pairs with severed networks do not. The signals travel through fungal mycelium, not the air or the soil water. In parallel, above-ground chemical conversations are happening simultaneously. Infested trees release airborne volatile organic compounds—α-pinene, β-pinene, and other monoterpenes with that sharp pine-forest smell—that neighboring trees detect through leaf surfaces. These volatiles prime systemic acquired resistance in receivers, activating genes like CPK11 that prepare cells for attack before any caterpillar arrives.

The network is not purely cooperative. The same underground channels that carry defense signals also carry allelochemicals—compounds designed to suppress competitors. Juglone, secreted by walnut trees, travels through the soil and kills or stunts the roots of nearby plants. Some trees use the network to shade out rivals rather than warn them. A 2023 critical review in *Undark* noted that popular accounts have romanticized the Wood Wide Web into something like maternal care, when in reality it's more like a stock market: sometimes cooperative, often competitive, always strategic.

What's clear is that forest trees are not passive individuals. A Douglas fir under attack in one part of the forest is already changing the biochemistry of pines 30 meters away. A seedling pushed into a gap in the canopy may tap into an existing fungal network and receive a small subsidy of carbon from established neighbors—or it may be chemically suppressed by a dominant tree protecting its own resource territory. The forest is alive in ways that the word "alive" barely covers.

Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi, which dominate tropical forests, form smaller, less extensive networks with different signaling properties. The massive, continent-spanning webs of EM fungi are largely a feature of cool-temperate biomes. But wherever they occur, they add a dimension of complexity to ecosystems that ecologists are only beginning to map. The first global atlas of mycorrhizal networks, published by Science in 2019, revealed that EM fungi-dominated forests store vastly more carbon than AM-dominated ones—not because of the signaling, but because EM fungi produce an enzyme profile that slows litter decomposition, locking carbon into the soil.

Trees talk. The conversation is chemical, fungal, and ancient. And we've barely begun to translate it.

## Key Facts

- Douglas fir infested with budworm transmits defense signals via ectomycorrhizal networks to linked ponderosa pines, inducing enzyme upregulation within hours
- Trees also release volatile compounds like α-pinene and β-pinene above ground that prime systemic resistance in neighbors before attack
- Ectomycorrhizal (EM) fungi dominate temperate forests and create the largest, most signal-active networks; tropical forests rely more on arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM)
- The same networks that carry warning signals can carry allelochemicals for competition—the Wood Wide Web is a marketplace, not just a mutual aid society
- The first global mycorrhizal atlas (2019) found EM-dominated forests sequester dramatically more carbon than AM-dominated ones
