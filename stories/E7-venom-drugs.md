# E7: Poison as Medicine

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

Evolution has spent hundreds of millions of years solving biochemical problems. Every venom is a product of that process—a highly targeted mixture of compounds refined by natural selection to disrupt specific proteins, channels, or receptors in prey or predators. The molecular precision that makes venoms deadly also makes them, when properly understood, extraordinarily valuable as drug leads. Three of the most important drugs of the last 50 years came directly from animals that most people fear.

The Brazilian pit viper *Bothrops jararaca* has been responsible for deaths across South America for centuries. In the 1940s, Brazilian pharmacologist Maurício Rocha e Silva noticed that the snake's venom caused an unusual drop in blood pressure. In the 1960s, his student Sérgio Ferreira identified the active compounds: bradykinin-potentiating peptides (BPPs) that inhibited an enzyme called angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE). ACE is a critical regulator of blood pressure—it converts inactive angiotensin I into the potent vasoconstrictor angiotensin II. Inhibiting ACE lowers blood pressure. Working from the snake peptide's structure, chemists at Squibb developed captopril, the first oral ACE inhibitor, approved by the FDA in 1981. It launched the most prescribed class of cardiovascular drugs in history.

The cone snail *Conus magus* preys on fish by firing a harpoon-like tooth loaded with venom that paralyzes its prey almost instantly. The venom contains hundreds of distinct peptides—conotoxins—each targeting a specific ion channel or receptor. In the early 1980s, Baldomero Olivera at the University of Utah began systematically cataloguing conotoxins and testing their biological activities. One peptide, omega-conotoxin MVIIA, blocked N-type voltage-gated calcium channels—exactly the channels that transmit pain signals through the nervous system. After two decades of development, ziconotide (Prialt) was approved by the FDA in 2004 as the first non-opioid drug for the treatment of severe, intractable chronic pain. It cannot be taken as a pill because the peptide would be digested; it must be delivered directly into the spinal fluid via intrathecal pump. It is the most powerful non-opioid painkiller known.

The Gila monster (*Heloderma suspectum*) delivered exendin-4, the peptide that became exenatide and launched the GLP-1 receptor agonist revolution—covered in a separate story. But the principle extends beyond these three examples. Cone snails alone contain an estimated 70,000 to 140,000 distinct venom peptides across their hundreds of species, the vast majority never characterized. Snake venoms, spider venoms, and scorpion venoms are similarly unexplored. The diversity of molecular targets these organisms have evolved to hit—receptors, channels, enzymes, clotting factors—spans almost the entire pharmacopeia of human disease.

Ziconotide and captopril demonstrate that the path from venom to drug is long, difficult, and unpredictable. Captopril took nearly 20 years from the identification of the snake peptide to FDA approval. Ziconotide took roughly the same time. Both required substantial chemistry to convert a natural peptide into a stable, manufacturable, deliverable therapeutic. Both required clinical trials demonstrating safety in humans.

What the venoms provided was not a drug—it was a proof of concept. They showed researchers which receptor or enzyme to target, and roughly what a molecular key to that lock should look like. The rest was engineering, and decades of work. The pharmacies of the future may be built, at least in part, on compounds found in the bodies of animals that humans have historically only been afraid of.

## Key Facts

- Captopril, the first ACE inhibitor (FDA approved 1981), was developed from bradykinin-potentiating peptides found in Brazilian pit viper (*Bothrops jararaca*) venom
- Ziconotide (Prialt, FDA approved 2004), derived from cone snail *Conus magus* omega-conotoxin MVIIA, is the most powerful non-opioid painkiller known
- Exenatide (Byetta, FDA approved 2005), derived from Gila monster salivary peptide exendin-4, launched the GLP-1 receptor agonist drug class
- Cone snails alone contain an estimated 70,000–140,000 distinct venom peptides across ~700 species, representing a largely untapped pharmacological library
- Each venom-derived drug required roughly 15–20 years from initial discovery to clinical approval
