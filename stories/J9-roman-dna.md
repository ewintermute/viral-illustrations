# J9: Rome Was Not Built by Romans

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

If you could extract DNA from the citizens of Imperial Rome at the height of the Empire and compare it to the DNA of modern Italians, you would find a puzzling discontinuity. The ancient Romans were genetically diverse — a cosmopolitan mix of Near Eastern, Eastern Mediterranean, North African, and European ancestry reflecting the empire's enormous geographic reach. Modern Italians are genetically distinct from that ancient population in ways that reveal a massive population turnover between the fall of Rome and the medieval period.

This finding, assembled from ancient DNA recovered from dozens of Roman-era burial sites across Italy, has rewritten our understanding of who actually populated the world's most famous city.

The ancient DNA story of the Italian peninsula begins in the Neolithic, when farmers from the Near East arrived roughly 8,000 years ago and admixed with the small hunter-gatherer populations already present. Bronze Age migrations from the Eurasian Steppe added another genetic layer — the ancestry associated with the spread of Indo-European languages. By the Republican and early Imperial period (roughly 200 BCE to 300 CE), the genetic profile of people buried in central Italy had become predominantly Eastern Mediterranean and Near Eastern, reflecting centuries of migration from across the Roman world. Studies examining burials from Rome itself found that a majority of individuals had genetic ancestry most similar to modern populations from the eastern Mediterranean — Greece, Turkey, the Levant, Egypt.

The Empire actively moved people. Slaves, freedmen, soldiers, merchants, and administrators flowed from North Africa, Syria, Greece, Asia Minor, and beyond into the capital and its territories. The Roman Empire was genetically what it was politically: diverse, connected, and built from contributions across three continents.

Then Rome fell. The 5th and 6th centuries CE saw invasions by Goths, Vandals, Lombards, and other Germanic peoples. The plague of Justinian in the 6th century killed an estimated 25–50 million people across the Mediterranean and may have disproportionately affected urban, densely connected populations. The genetic evidence, drawn from post-Roman burials, shows a substantial shift — increased Northern European ancestry (consistent with Germanic migration) and decreased Eastern Mediterranean ancestry. The cosmopolitan Roman genetic mix was replaced by a more European-skewed profile.

Modern Italians sit between these historical extremes — genetically more similar to the medieval post-Roman population than to Imperial Roman citizens, with some regional variation: northern Italians show more Northern European ancestry, southern Italians more Eastern Mediterranean, Sardinians a uniquely ancient Neolithic-derived profile. The unbroken cultural lineage from Roman civilization to modern Italy conceals a substantial genetic discontinuity driven by plague, invasion, population collapse, and migration between the 5th and 10th centuries.

The findings from a 2021 study of rural Imperial Roman-era burials in the Vagnari agricultural estate in southern Italy found that even the agricultural working population — not just urban elites — showed significant Eastern Mediterranean and Near Eastern ancestry, demonstrating that the cosmopolitan genetics of Rome extended beyond the capital into the provincial countryside.

## Key Facts

- Ancient DNA from Imperial Roman-era burials in central Italy shows predominantly Eastern Mediterranean and Near Eastern ancestry — not ancestry similar to modern Italians
- The Roman Empire actively relocated people from across its territory; the genetic diversity of Rome's population reflected its geographic breadth
- Post-Roman medieval burials show a substantial genetic shift toward Northern European ancestry, consistent with Germanic migrations and the Justinianic plague
- Modern Italians are genetically closer to the post-Roman medieval population than to Imperial Roman citizens
- Sardinians uniquely retain Neolithic farmer ancestry with minimal post-Neolithic admixture, making them a genetic outlier within Italy
