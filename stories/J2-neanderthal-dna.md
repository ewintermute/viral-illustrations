# J2: The Ancient Upgrade

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

When Svante Pääbo's team at the Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology sequenced the first Neanderthal genome in 2010, they discovered something that reframed the story of human origins: modern humans outside of Africa carry 1–4% Neanderthal DNA. The meeting between anatomically modern humans expanding out of Africa and the Neanderthals who had occupied Europe and western Asia for hundreds of thousands of years was not a story of simple replacement. It was a story of interbreeding — and, crucially, of adaptation.

Not all Neanderthal DNA is equally distributed across the modern human genome. If the introgressed sequences were random evolutionary noise, we would expect them to be scattered with roughly equal frequency everywhere. They are not. Certain regions are dramatically enriched for Neanderthal variants in modern populations outside Africa; certain other regions are almost entirely devoid of Neanderthal DNA. The pattern tells a story of natural selection: some Neanderthal variants were beneficial and spread through the modern human population; others were incompatible with modern human biology and were eliminated.

The immune system is the clearest example of beneficial introgression. Several Neanderthal-derived variants in HLA genes — the molecules that present foreign antigens to immune cells — are highly prevalent in non-African populations. These variants were presumably selected because they provided improved immune recognition of pathogens that Neanderthals, after hundreds of thousands of years in Eurasia, had evolved defenses against. When modern humans arrived in those environments, they were immunologically naive; acquiring Neanderthal immune genes through interbreeding provided an immediate advantage. A specific Neanderthal-derived haplotype in the OAS gene cluster, which encodes antiviral proteins, appears to provide protection against certain RNA viruses and occurs at high frequency in East Asians.

Skin and hair adaptations are a second category of beneficial introgression. Keratin-related gene variants of Neanderthal origin are enriched in East Asian and European populations, suggesting they provided better adaptation to the skin, hair, and nail requirements of colder, less UV-intense environments. Variants in *BNC2* and several other keratin-pathway genes with Neanderthal origins occur at frequencies suggesting positive selection over tens of thousands of years.

The case for UV-light adaptation is supported by studies of Neanderthal ancestry in Japanese and Chinese populations. Certain Neanderthal-derived genomic regions appear in high frequencies specifically in populations from higher latitudes, consistent with selection for traits useful at reduced UV levels — possibly related to vitamin D synthesis, skin pigmentation, or DNA repair mechanisms that differ between high and low UV environments.

The regions depleted of Neanderthal DNA tell a complementary story. Genes involved in spermatogenesis and brain development show striking Neanderthal depletion — consistent with hybrid incompatibility, the idea that Neanderthal variants in these gene categories were maladaptive in a modern human genetic background and were purged by selection. The modern human genome carries Neanderthal DNA selectively, retaining the adaptations and discarding the incompatibilities.

The 50,000-year-old admixture is not ancient history. Neanderthal-derived immune alleles are part of the reason that COVID-19 hit certain populations harder than others — a Neanderthal haplotype on chromosome 3 was associated with both increased severe COVID-19 risk and decreased risk in different populations, depending on which Neanderthal-derived variants were inherited. Our ancient relatives are still with us, still influencing our biology.

## Key Facts

- Non-African modern humans carry 1–4% Neanderthal DNA, representing interbreeding ~50,000–70,000 years ago
- Neanderthal immune genes (particularly HLA variants and the OAS antiviral gene cluster) are enriched in non-African populations due to positive selection
- Keratin-pathway genes of Neanderthal origin are enriched in East Asian and European populations, suggesting skin and hair adaptation to colder environments
- Regions depleted of Neanderthal DNA cluster around spermatogenesis and brain development genes, suggesting hybrid incompatibility in these categories
- A Neanderthal chromosome 3 haplotype influenced COVID-19 severity outcomes, demonstrating ongoing health relevance of ancient introgression
