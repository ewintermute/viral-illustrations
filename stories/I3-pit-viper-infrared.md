# I3: Seeing Heat in the Dark

*Category: Extraordinary Senses*

## The Story

Between the nostril and the eye on the face of a rattlesnake, there is a pit. It is small — a few millimeters wide, in some species barely visible — and it is one of the most sensitive thermal sensors in the animal kingdom. The loreal pit, and the analogous pit organs found in pythons and boas, allows these snakes to detect temperature differences as small as 0.003°C from objects in their environment. A human hand held near a pit viper in complete darkness is, from the snake's perspective, a brilliantly obvious target — a hot object radiating infrared radiation against a cooler background.

The mechanism begins with physics. Warm objects radiate infrared energy — photons with wavelengths longer than visible light — at a rate that scales with their temperature. The membrane inside the pit organ, stretched thin across a small air-filled cavity, absorbs incoming infrared radiation. The membrane is extraordinarily thin, roughly 15 micrometers — thinner than a sheet of plastic wrap — which allows it to respond to small energy inputs with meaningful temperature changes. The absorbed energy warms the membrane slightly, and this warming activates a molecular sensor: the ion channel TRPA1, expressed in dense concentrations in the trigeminal nerve endings that innervate the membrane.

TRPA1 is a temperature-sensitive channel originally characterized for its role in pain response to cold in mammals. In pit vipers, it has been co-opted for a fundamentally different purpose: detecting warmth from ambient infrared. Research published in 2010 in Nature demonstrated that TRPA1 channels in the pit organ membrane are activated by the warming caused by infrared absorption, triggering action potentials in the trigeminal nerve that carry the signal to the brain.

The brain receives two streams of sensory input: visual from the optic system, and infrared from the trigeminal pit-organ pathway. These signals are integrated in the optic tectum, a midbrain structure that in rattlesnakes contains a region specifically devoted to processing infrared input in the same spatial coordinate frame as visual input. The result is a bimodal map — neurons that respond to both visual and infrared stimuli from the same direction in space. The snake does not "see" heat separately from visual input; the two are merged into a single spatial representation.

This integration explains one of the most remarkable hunting behaviors of pit vipers: the ability to strike warm-blooded prey in total darkness with precision comparable to a daylight strike. Experimental work with rattlesnakes wearing opaque goggles confirms that they maintain accurate strike distances and angles using pit-organ information alone. The thermal image, though much lower in resolution than the visual image, is sufficient to localize a mouse at striking distance.

Pythons and boas — which independently evolved similar labial pit organs — provide a convergent case. In these snakes, the pits are distributed along the lips rather than concentrated between eye and nostril, but the same TRPA1-based mechanism operates. Two evolutionary lineages arrived at the same solution to the same problem: detecting warm-blooded prey in the dark.

The sensitivity figure — 0.003°C — comes from electrophysiological recordings of individual pit-organ nerve fibers, which respond to warming of the membrane at that threshold. It represents one of the most refined thermal sensors known in vertebrates.

## Key Facts

- Pit vipers detect temperature differences as small as 0.003°C via the TRPA1 ion channel in the thin membrane of the loreal pit
- The pit membrane is approximately 15 micrometers thick — thinner than plastic wrap — enabling it to warm detectably from low-level infrared radiation
- Infrared and visual signals are integrated in the optic tectum, creating a bimodal spatial map used for accurate strikes in total darkness
- Rattlesnakes wearing opaque goggles strike accurately at mice using thermal information alone
- Pythons and boas independently evolved similar labial pit organs using the same TRPA1 molecular mechanism — convergent evolution of thermal sensing
