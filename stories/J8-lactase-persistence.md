# J8: The Mutation That Changed What We Eat

*Category: Human Variation*

## The Story

Virtually all infant mammals produce lactase — the enzyme that breaks down lactose, the primary sugar in milk. Virtually all of them stop producing it after weaning. The gene encoding lactase is turned off, and the ability to digest milk sugar disappears. This default state is called lactase non-persistence, and it is the ancestral human condition. Most of the world's adults are lactase non-persistent: roughly 65% of the global adult population cannot digest significant amounts of lactose without gastrointestinal distress.

The remaining 35% carry a mutation that overrides this default. Lactase persistence — the continued production of lactase into adulthood — is caused by specific regulatory variants upstream of the lactase gene (*LCT*) that prevent its developmental silencing. The most common European variant, a single nucleotide change at position −13910 in the regulatory region, keeps the gene active throughout life. The person who carries it can drink milk as an adult without any digestive consequence.

This mutation is, by the standards of human genetics, astonishingly recent. Using the pattern of genetic variation around the −13910 variant — the "haplotype block" that accumulated before random mutation broke up the surrounding sequence — researchers have estimated that the European lactase persistence allele arose approximately 7,500 years ago in a pastoralist population associated with cattle herding, most likely in central Europe. That is not ancient history in genetic terms. Modern humans have been anatomically present for over 300,000 years. The lactase persistence mutation emerged when agriculture was already established, long after humans had domesticated cattle.

The mutation's selective advantage must have been substantial to reach its current frequency — roughly 80–90% in northern European populations — in only 7,500 years. This is among the fastest and most dramatic selective sweeps documented in any human population, exceeded only by a handful of other disease-resistance variants. Researchers have modeled the advantage and estimate that lactase persistent individuals in early pastoral societies had approximately 6% higher survival or reproductive success than non-persistent individuals — a large advantage in evolutionary terms.

The mechanism of the advantage is debated. Direct nutritional benefit from dairy consumption is one hypothesis: in times of famine or disease, access to a calorie-rich liquid food source that non-persistent individuals cannot fully utilize would provide a survival margin. Calcium absorption is another: milk provides absorbable calcium, important for bone density in populations with limited vitamin D from sunlight (northern latitudes). A 2022 analysis published in Nature challenged the simple "nutrition" hypothesis, finding that dairy consumption in ancient populations was often high even before lactase persistence was common — suggesting that fermented dairy products (cheese, yogurt), which have reduced lactose content, may have provided the initial dietary advantage, with the lactase mutation providing additional benefit.

Lactase persistence has evolved independently in multiple populations — in East Africa among pastoralist Fulani, Maasai, and Beja peoples (different regulatory variants, same functional outcome), in the Middle East, and in parts of South Asia. Each evolution was triggered by the same cultural context: cattle herding, access to fresh milk, and selection pressure strong enough to drive a rare mutation to high frequency in just a few thousand years. It is one of the clearest examples of gene-culture co-evolution in the human story.

## Key Facts

- The European lactase persistence variant (−13910*T) arose approximately 7,500 years ago in central European pastoralists, one of the most recent major selected variants in human evolution
- The selective sweep is among the strongest documented in human populations, requiring ~6% fitness advantage per generation to explain current frequencies of 80–90% in northern Europe
- Lactase persistence evolved independently in East African pastoralist populations (Fulani, Maasai, Beja) via different regulatory variants — convergent evolution driven by cattle herding
- 65% of global adults are lactase non-persistent; the persistent minority represents the derived, culturally co-evolved state
- The selective advantage mechanism may involve direct milk nutrition, calcium absorption, or fermented dairy supplementing the baseline before the mutation spread
