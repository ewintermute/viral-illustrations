# E22: The Bacterium in the Corn

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

*Bacillus thuringiensis* is a gram-positive, spore-forming soil bacterium that most people have never heard of, yet it has been used as an insecticide since the 1930s and is today genetically embedded in crops grown on hundreds of millions of acres worldwide. Its story is one of the most consequential in the history of agriculture—and one of the most contested.

Bt was first isolated in Japan in 1901 from diseased silkworm larvae. Its defining feature is the production, during sporulation, of crystalline proteins called Cry proteins (for "crystal")—insecticidal toxins that dissolve in the alkaline pH of insect midguts, bind to specific receptors on gut cells, and create pores in the cell membranes. The cells lyse, the gut barrier fails, the insect dies of septicemia or starvation within days. The key feature is specificity: different Cry protein variants target different insect orders. Cry1A proteins are highly toxic to lepidoptera (moths and butterflies, including their larval caterpillars); Cry3 proteins target coleoptera (beetles); Cry4 proteins target diptera (flies and mosquitoes). Mammalian and human gut cells lack the receptors that the toxins bind, so Bt is essentially non-toxic to vertebrates.

For decades, Bt was used as a spray insecticide in organic agriculture. It degrades rapidly in sunlight and does not accumulate in the environment. By the 1990s, it was the most widely used biological pesticide in the world.

Then came genetic engineering. In 1996, the FDA and EPA approved the first commercialized Bt crops—corn and cotton engineered to constitutively express Cry proteins in their own tissues. The value proposition was straightforward: instead of spraying an insecticide that would break down and need to be reapplied, the crop would produce the active compound continuously in every cell, protecting itself from pest damage without additional inputs.

The adoption was remarkable. From 1996 to 2013, Bt crops were planted on a cumulative total of 570 million hectares across 25 countries by 14 million farmers. In India alone, Bt cotton covered more than 26 million acres by 2014, producing a dramatic reduction in pesticide use and an increase in yields that transformed the country from a cotton importer to a major exporter. Bt corn adoption in the United States allowed farmers to reduce insecticide spraying for European corn borer by over 90% in some regions, with substantial economic and environmental benefits.

The technology is not without complications. Insect pests can evolve resistance to Cry proteins—and they have, in multiple agricultural systems where Bt crops were adopted without adequate management practices. The US EPA mandated "refuge" requirements—areas planted with non-Bt crop varieties surrounding Bt fields—to maintain susceptible insect populations that would interbreed with any resistant individuals that emerged, slowing resistance evolution. Where these refuges were properly maintained, resistance has been manageable. Where they were neglected or circumvented, resistance has become problematic.

Bt crops also raised ecological concerns about effects on non-target organisms. Early studies suggested that Cry proteins in pollen could harm monarch butterfly caterpillars under laboratory conditions. Subsequent field studies found that the risk was much lower under realistic exposure conditions, but the controversy established a template for ongoing debates about ecological risk assessment for GM crops that continues decades later.

*Bacillus thuringiensis* did not choose to be controversial. It was just a soil bacterium making crystals that happened, by accident of evolution, to dissolve in insect guts. What humans did with that accident became one of the most globally impactful agricultural technologies in history.

## Key Facts

- *Bacillus thuringiensis* produces Cry proteins (crystal toxins) during sporulation; these dissolve in the alkaline insect midgut, bind specific receptors, and lyse gut cells—harmless to vertebrates
- Different Cry protein variants target different insect orders: Cry1A (lepidoptera/caterpillars), Cry3 (beetles), Cry4 (flies/mosquitoes)
- Bt crops (expressing Cry proteins in plant tissues) were commercialized in 1996; from 1996–2013, planted on 570 million cumulative hectares across 25 countries
- Bt cotton adoption in India covered >26 million acres by 2014, dramatically reducing pesticide use and increasing cotton yields
- Insect resistance to Bt toxins has emerged where refuge requirements were not maintained; it represents the primary management challenge for the technology
