# B2: You Are More Microbe Than Human

*Category: Microbiology & Human Biology*

## The Story

For decades, science communicators cited a striking figure: the human body contains ten times more microbial cells than human cells. Ten to one. You are, in a real quantitative sense, more microbe than person.

It turns out the actual number is more modest — and still remarkable.

In 2016, researchers Ron Sender, Shai Fuchs, and Ron Milo published a paper in *Cell* that carefully recalculated these figures using better anatomical data and more current estimates of microbial density. Their conclusion: the typical adult human body contains approximately **30 trillion human cells** and approximately **38 trillion bacteria**. The ratio is roughly **1.3 to 1**, not 10 to 1. You are slightly more microbe than human, not overwhelmingly so.

This correction matters for accuracy, but it doesn't make the story less interesting. Thirty-eight trillion is still an almost unimaginable number of microorganisms — and crucially, they are not passive passengers. The human microbiome — particularly the gut microbiome — is now understood to be a metabolically active organ in its own right, with profound and specific effects on human physiology, including brain function and mood.

Consider serotonin, the neurotransmitter most commonly associated with mood, well-being, and depression. Serotonin is almost universally described as a "brain chemical." But approximately **95% of the body's total serotonin** is produced not in the brain, but in the gut — specifically in **enterochromaffin cells** lining the intestinal wall. And the microbiome plays a direct role in regulating how much serotonin those cells produce. Gut bacteria ferment dietary fiber into **short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)** like butyrate, propionate, and acetate; these SCFAs signal enterochromaffin cells to increase serotonin synthesis. A disrupted or impoverished gut microbiome means less SCFA production, which means less gut serotonin.

This gut-produced serotonin doesn't cross the blood-brain barrier to directly "make you happy" — the brain produces and regulates its own serotonin independently. But the gut-brain relationship is bidirectional and more complex than a simple pipeline. The **gut-brain axis** is a multi-channel communication network involving: the **vagus nerve**, a direct neural highway carrying signals from gut to brainstem; the **hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis**, the body's primary stress response system; immune signaling molecules that can pass from gut to circulation to brain; and the microbiome's own direct production of other neuroactive compounds including GABA and dopamine precursors.

When the gut microbiome is disrupted — through antibiotics, poor diet, illness — the downstream effects are measurable. Gut inflammation alters the **kynurenine pathway**, which competes with tryptophan (the building block of serotonin), effectively shunting serotonin precursors away from serotonin synthesis. Dysbiosis in the gut correlates statistically with increased rates of depression and anxiety, and animal studies (particularly germ-free mouse models) have shown that removing gut bacteria entirely can produce measurable changes in stress hormones and anxiety-like behaviors. Reintroducing specific bacterial species can reverse those changes.

None of this means that depression is simply a gut problem, or that probiotics are a cure for mental illness. The relationship is real but complex, involving many interacting systems. What it does mean is that the conceptual boundary between "your body" and "the microbes living in it" is far blurrier than anyone assumed a generation ago.

The human gut contains roughly **500 to 1,000 distinct bacterial species**, depending on the individual. The collective genome of the gut microbiome — sometimes called the **metagenome** — contains approximately **150 times more genes** than the entire human genome. We are not just carrying passengers. We are, in some sense, a collaborative venture between human cells and microbial cells that have been evolving together for the entire history of our species — shaping each other's biochemistry, immune function, metabolism, and perhaps even behavior.

You are not just you. You are an ecosystem.

## Key Facts

- The human body contains ~30 trillion human cells and ~38 trillion bacteria — a revised ratio of 1.3:1, not the often-cited 10:1.
- Approximately **95% of the body's serotonin** is produced in the gut by enterochromaffin cells, regulated in part by gut microbial metabolites (short-chain fatty acids).
- The gut-brain axis is a bidirectional communication network using the vagus nerve, the HPA axis, immune signaling, and microbial neurotransmitters.
- Gut microbiome disruption (dysbiosis) correlates with depression and anxiety; germ-free animal models show altered stress hormones and anxiety-like behaviors.
- The collective microbiome genome contains ~150 times more genes than the human genome; each individual carries a microbial community of 500–1,000 bacterial species.
