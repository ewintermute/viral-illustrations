# E1: The Lizard That Started a Drug Revolution

*Category: Biotechnology & Medicine*

## The Story

In 1992, endocrinologist John Eng was working at the Veterans Affairs Medical Center in the Bronx, studying peptides from animal venoms and secretions for potential pharmaceutical activity. His attention fell on the Gila monster (*Heloderma suspectum*), a venomous lizard native to the American Southwest that has fascinated biologists for decades. Unlike most animals, Gila monsters eat infrequently—large meals weeks apart—and secrete large quantities of digestive hormones when they do eat. Eng identified a peptide in the lizard's salivary secretions and noticed something unusual: it bore 53% structural similarity to glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1), a human gut hormone involved in regulating blood sugar and appetite.

The similarity was not coincidental. Hundreds of millions of years of evolutionary pressure had independently produced, in a lizard's salivary gland, a molecule very similar to a human metabolic hormone. But the lizard's version had a critical property the human version lacked: it was resistant to degradation. Human GLP-1 is broken down by an enzyme called DPP-4 within minutes of being secreted. The Gila monster peptide—which Eng named exendin-4—remained active for hours.

Eng patented the molecule and pitched it to pharmaceutical companies throughout the 1990s. Most passed. Eventually Amylin Pharmaceuticals licensed the compound, developed it into a synthetic form called exenatide, and in 2005, the FDA approved exenatide (branded as Byetta) as the first GLP-1 receptor agonist for the treatment of type 2 diabetes. It was the first diabetes drug derived directly from a wild animal's venom.

The mechanism of GLP-1 receptor agonism is elegant. The GLP-1 receptor sits on the surface of pancreatic beta cells, gut cells, and neurons throughout the brain, including appetite-regulating centers in the hypothalamus. When activated by GLP-1 or an agonist like exenatide, it stimulates insulin secretion in proportion to blood glucose levels (helpfully, this means it doesn't cause hypoglycemia at normal blood glucose), suppresses glucagon (which would otherwise raise blood sugar), slows gastric emptying so glucose enters the bloodstream more slowly, and—crucially—signals satiety to the brain. People taking GLP-1 agonists feel full faster and stay full longer.

This last property launched the weight loss revolution. Exenatide and its successors—liraglutide, dulaglutide, semaglutide—showed progressively stronger effects on body weight. Semaglutide (Ozempic for diabetes, Wegovy for obesity), developed by Novo Nordisk from a modified human GLP-1 sequence, produces average weight losses of 15–20% of body weight in clinical trials, numbers previously only achievable with bariatric surgery. The GLP-1 agonist market, driven by semaglutide and tirzepatide (a dual GLP-1/GIP receptor agonist from Eli Lilly), has grown to exceed $30 billion annually and is projected to reach $100 billion by the early 2030s.

All of it traces back to a Gila monster in a desert, and a researcher who thought an unusual peptide might be worth investigating. The most commercially successful drug class of the 2020s began as a curiosity from an animal whose venom most people thought was simply dangerous.

## Key Facts

- Exenatide (Byetta), approved 2005, was developed from exendin-4—a peptide in Gila monster saliva with 53% homology to human GLP-1 but resistant to DPP-4 degradation
- GLP-1 receptor agonists stimulate insulin secretion in proportion to blood glucose, suppress glucagon, slow gastric emptying, and signal satiety to the brain
- Semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy) produces average weight loss of 15–20% of body weight—comparable to bariatric surgery results
- The GLP-1 agonist market exceeded $30 billion in 2023 and is projected to surpass $100 billion by the early 2030s
- John Eng discovered the connection in the early 1990s; most pharmaceutical companies initially passed on the compound
