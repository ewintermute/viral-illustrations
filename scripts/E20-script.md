# E20: The Molecule That Might Slow Aging
*~230 words*

---

In 1964, a Canadian microbiologist went to Easter Island looking for something that might explain why barefoot islanders seemed unusually resistant to tetanus. The soil samples came back, went into the screening program, and one of them contained a Streptomyces bacterium producing something unusual.

They named the compound rapamycin, after Rapa Nui, the Polynesian name for the island.

Then they filed it away for a decade.

Rapamycin is an inhibitor of mTOR — mammalian target of rapamycin — which is a central relay in cell biology that responds to nutrient availability, growth factors, and energy levels, and controls whether cells grow or repair. High nutrients: mTOR is active, cells grow, autophagy is suppressed. Low nutrients: mTOR is inhibited, cells shift into repair and cleanup mode.

Caloric restriction extends lifespan in virtually every organism tested. The leading hypothesis for why: reduced food shifts mTOR toward repair. Rapamycin does that pharmacologically.

The FDA approved it in 1999 for organ transplant rejection. The anti-aging interest came later.

In 2009, the National Institute on Aging tested rapamycin in genetically heterogeneous mice starting at 600 days of age — late middle age. Median lifespan extended 9 to 14%. Maximum lifespan extended similarly. The mice were already old when treatment started. It still worked.

A 2016 study found three months of rapamycin extended life expectancy by up to 60% and improved cardiac and cognitive function.

Human trials are underway. No approved anti-aging indication yet. The side effects are real.

But a Streptomyces bacterium from an island in the South Pacific might be the most interesting molecule in aging biology right now.
