# F6: The Barnacle That Wears a Crab
*~240 words*

---

There's a barnacle that doesn't grow a shell. It grows a crab.

Sacculina starts as a tiny larva drifting through open water. When she finds a crab, she searches the seams of its armor until she finds a soft joint, molts her own outer shell, and injects her entire soft body — just a cluster of cells — directly into the crab. Her discarded shell sinks away. She's inside now.

What happens next is one of the more disturbing things in parasitology. She sends out root-like threads, called the interna, through the crab's entire body — surrounding the organs, tapping into the hepatopancreas where the crab absorbs nutrition from its food. Meanwhile a reproductive sac, the externa, pushes through the crab's abdomen and erupts to the outside. The crab's gonads are destroyed. It can no longer molt. It cannot escape.

If the crab is male, the manipulation goes further. Sacculina's chemicals feminize him: broader abdomen, smaller claws, altered hormones.

And then comes the part that makes my skin crawl. The parasite's tendrils wrap around the crab's nervous system and eyestalks, and the crab begins performing the egg-care behavior of a mother. When Sacculina's eggs are ready to hatch, the infected crab fans water over the parasite's egg mass, raises itself on its legs, rocks back and forth, and shoots larvae into the water.

It thinks it's taking care of its children. It has no children. It has been a host since the day that shell sank.
