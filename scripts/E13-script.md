# E13: The Fungus from Norway That Saved Transplant Medicine
*~220 words*

---

In 1969, a Sandoz employee traveling in Norway collected a small bag of soil from the Hardanger Vidda plateau — a windswept high-altitude landscape above the fjords, cold, nutrient-poor, the kind of place where only specialists survive. The sample went into the screening program. Most samples yielded nothing.

This one yielded organ transplantation.

From that soil, microbiologists isolated a fungus called Tolypocladium inflatum. It produced a family of cyclic peptides with modest antifungal activity. Borderline result. Standard procedure would have been to move on.

What saved the compound was a program reorganization in 1972 that added immunosuppression testing. When researchers tested cyclosporine against immune cells, it inhibited T lymphocyte proliferation without the general toxicity that characterized earlier immunosuppressants. A new pharmacological action.

The mechanism is precise: cyclosporine binds a cellular protein called cyclophilin. That complex inhibits calcineurin. Calcineurin can't activate NFAT. NFAT can't drive interleukin-2 production. T cells can't proliferate to reject the foreign organ. The immune response is blocked specifically at the rejection pathway, while most other immune functions are preserved.

FDA approval came in 1983. One-year kidney transplant graft survival improved by roughly 20 percentage points. Heart and liver transplantation, previously experimental, became established clinical practice.

Over 140,000 organ transplants are performed annually worldwide. That system was built on a compound from cold Norwegian soil that a screening program nearly skipped.
