# E16: The Ancient Cure That Western Science Missed for 1,600 Years
*~240 words*

---

In 340 AD, a Chinese physician named Ge Hong wrote down a malaria treatment. Soak sweet wormwood in cold water. Wring out the juice. Drink it.

Do not boil it.

That last line sat in a text for 1,600 years while malaria killed, by some estimates, half of everyone who ever lived.

By the late 1960s, drug-resistant malaria was spreading through Southeast Asia. The Vietnam War was killing more soldiers with fever than with bullets. Both sides were desperate for new treatments. The Chinese government launched a secret military program in 1967 called Project 523, and assigned a pharmacologist named Tu Youyou to search traditional medicine texts.

Her team kept returning to qing hao — Artemisia annua, sweet wormwood — which showed up across historical fever remedies. But their extracts were inconsistent. Then Tu Youyou went back to Ge Hong's instruction from 340 AD. Don't boil it.

The standard extraction protocol used heat. She switched to low-temperature ether extraction. The resulting extract was highly active against malaria parasites in rodent models.

The compound they isolated in 1972 was artemisinin. It works by reacting with heme iron inside the parasite, generating free radicals that destroy it. Active against multiple life cycle stages. Clears parasitemia in three days.

Artemisinin-based combination therapies are now the WHO first-line treatment for malaria. Over 200 million doses per year. Global malaria mortality fell 40% after widespread adoption.

Tu Youyou received the Nobel Prize in 2015. She was 84.

One instruction, ignored for sixteen centuries.
