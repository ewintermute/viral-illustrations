# C1: The Fungus That Drives
*~220 words*

---

There's a fungus that doesn't kill ants. It drives them.

Ophiocordyceps unilateralis infects a carpenter ant, spreads through its muscles, and over the next few weeks, rewires its behavior from the inside. The ant stops following its colony's trails. It starts wandering. It moves toward light. And then, around solar noon, it clamps its mandibles into a leaf or a twig at exactly 25 centimeters above the forest floor and dies. Every time. That height. That time of day.

A week later, a orange stalk erupts from the back of its head and rains spores onto the ant trail below.

Here's the part that genuinely impressed the researchers: the fungus doesn't touch the brain. The ant's brain is intact the whole time. What Ophiocordyceps does is grow hyphae directly through the mandibular muscles, physically commandeering the lockjaw. The chemicals it releases, including a compound called aflatrem, handle everything else: the wandering, the light-seeking, the timing. The brain is just... watching.

The ant has been turned into a spore delivery vehicle by a puppet master who never went near the cockpit.

The colony knows something's wrong. Workers have been observed dragging infected nestmates away from the nest before the end. And there's even a second fungus that parasitizes Ophiocordyceps itself, suppressing outbreaks.

Evolution already ran the countermeasures. It always does.
