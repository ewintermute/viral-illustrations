# K11: The Termite That Air-Conditions a Skyscraper
*~210 words*

---

Macrotermes termites in Zimbabwe build mounds up to 9 meters tall. Inside those mounds, millions of termites and the fungus gardens they farm must be kept at about 31 degrees Celsius. The outside temperature swings from 3 degrees at night to 42 degrees during the day.

No mechanical systems. No electricity. Just architecture.

The mound uses thermal mass and passive airflow: heated air from the colony's metabolism rises through a central chimney, passes through the thin, gas-permeable outer walls where it cools, and circulates back down through peripheral channels. The orientation of the mound is tuned to minimize peak solar load. It maintains internal stability through a 40-degree ambient swing.

In 1996, a Zimbabwean architect named Mick Pearce designed the Eastgate Centre in Harare — 33,000 square meters of commercial office space — using the same principle. No conventional air conditioning. Massive concrete thermal mass, natural ventilation channels drawing cool air through lower levels and exhausting hot air through roof chimneys, ceiling fans for circulation.

Energy consumption for climate control: approximately 10% of comparable buildings. Capital savings from skipping the HVAC system: $3.5 million in 1996 dollars.

I should say: researchers later found that wind-driven pressure differentials actually play a significant role in how real termite mounds ventilate. The biology is more complex than the original model.

But Pearce built a building that works. The inspiration was real, even if the details were messier than expected.
