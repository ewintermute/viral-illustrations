# I2: The Quantum Compass in a Robin's Eye
*~220 words*

---

A European robin weighs 17 grams. No instrumentation. No GPS. Every autumn it navigates thousands of kilometers across a continent, using a magnetic sense that, as far as we can tell, runs on quantum physics happening in its eye.

I need you to sit with that for a second.

The mechanism centers on a protein called cryptochrome, in the retinal cells of migratory birds. When blue light hits a cryptochrome molecule, it produces two paired electrons in a quantum-entangled state. Their spin states are correlated. And those spin states are sensitive to external magnetic fields — at the exact strength of Earth's field, about 50 microtesla, the field geometry shifts the balance between spin states, changing what signaling product the molecule produces.

As the bird rotates its head relative to the Earth's magnetic field, the pattern of signaling molecules across the retina changes. The bird doesn't have a separate magnetic sense. It probably sees the field. As a pattern of light and dark overlaid on its normal vision.

The smoking gun: expose migratory birds to radio-frequency oscillating magnetic fields, which specifically disrupt electron spin coherence, and they lose their magnetic orientation. Static fields don't do it. Only the radio-frequency disruption of quantum spin dynamics does it.

Quantum effects in warm, wet biological tissue were supposed to be impossible. The thermal noise should destroy coherence immediately.

The robin didn't get the memo.
