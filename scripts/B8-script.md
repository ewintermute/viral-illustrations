# B8: Cancer That Catches
*~240 words*

---

Cancer is a disease of one individual. One body, one set of mutations, contained. That's the rule.

Tasmanian devils broke the rule.

Devils bite each other constantly — during feeding, during mating, during every territorial dispute. In the 1980s, a single devil's cancer cell acquired the ability to survive being transferred between individuals via bite wound. That cell line has been spreading ever since. It's called devil facial tumor 1, DFT1, and it has caused an 80% population collapse across Tasmania.

The tumor grows in the face and mouth, metastasizes fast, and kills within months by blocking eating or spreading to internal organs. But here's what makes it extraordinary as biology: those tumor cells are genetically foreign to every animal they infect. Normally the immune system destroys foreign cells immediately. DFT1 survives by downregulating its MHC class I molecules, the surface proteins that flag cells as self or other. It makes itself invisible.

In 2015, researchers found a second, independently arising transmissible cancer in Tasmanian devils. A completely different cell of origin. DFT2. Two separate events in the same species.

For context on how unprecedented this is: the only other well-studied transmissible cancer in mammals is canine transmissible venereal tumor. CTVT is estimated to be 4,000 to 11,000 years old. It is one of the oldest continuously surviving cell lineages on Earth. The original dog is long gone. Its cancer is still out there, spreading.

A cell line that has outlasted every body it ever lived in. That's not nothing.
