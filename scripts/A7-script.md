# A7: The Blue Blood That Guards Every Drug on Earth
*~230 words*

---

Every vaccine you've ever gotten. Every IV drip. Every injectable drug approved in the US. All of them were tested for safety using the blood of an animal that has barely changed in 450 million years.

The horseshoe crab. Not actually a crab. More closely related to scorpions, and its blood is blue, because it runs on copper instead of iron.

Inside that blood are immune cells called amebocytes. When you crack them open, they release enzymes that do something no synthetic alternative has fully replaced: they detect bacterial endotoxins. Endotoxins are the chemical corpses of gram-negative bacteria — fragments that survive sterilization, that are invisible to most tests, and that, if they get into your bloodstream, cause septic shock. Before this test existed, the only way to check an IV drip was to inject it into a rabbit and watch for a fever. That took days.

The processed extract, called LAL, detects endotoxins at picogram-per-milliliter concentrations. The FDA approved it in 1977. It replaced the rabbit test entirely.

That extract sells for $60,000 a gallon. The global market is $112 million a year. To produce it, 500,000 to 600,000 crabs are caught, bled for 30% of their blood, and returned to the sea. Some don't make it back.

A synthetic alternative exists and works. Regulatory inertia is a real thing.

The horseshoe crab is 450 million years old. The pharmaceutical industry still needs it.
