# J7: The Plague That Made Europeans HIV-Resistant
*~230 words*

---

In the early 1990s, researchers noticed something strange: a small group of people who were repeatedly exposed to HIV just didn't get infected. When scientists looked at their genomes, they found a 32-base-pair deletion in a gene called CCR5.

HIV-1 needs the CCR5 protein to enter T-cells. No CCR5, no doorway.

People with two copies of this deletion — about 1% of Northern Europeans — are nearly completely resistant to HIV-1 infection. One copy gives elevated resistance and slower progression if infected. The protein appears to be largely dispensable: CCR5-delta32 homozygotes are otherwise healthy.

In 2007, Timothy Ray Brown — the Berlin Patient — was functionally cured of HIV after receiving a bone marrow transplant from a CCR5-delta32 donor. We went from observing a genetic curiosity to engineering a cure, using the same principle.

But here's what I find more remarkable: that mutation is present at about 10% allele frequency in European populations. Random mutations don't reach 10% without positive selection. Something was killing people, and the mutation was helping them survive.

Ancient DNA analysis published in 2022 found that CCR5-delta32 frequency rose sharply in European genomes during the 14th century Black Death. The Black Death killed 30 to 50% of Europe between 1346 and 1353. Whatever selective pressure was operating, it operated hard and fast.

Our ancestors survived the plague. The mutation they were carrying for it, by coincidence, turned out to block a virus that wouldn't exist for another six centuries.
