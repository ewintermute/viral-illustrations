# J13: The Children Who Changed Everything
*~250 words*

---

In November 2018, a scientist named He Jiankui announced at a conference in Hong Kong that he had done something no one had done before. He had used CRISPR to edit human embryos that were then implanted and born as children. Two girls first. A boy in 2019.

His stated goal was to protect them from HIV. He deleted a gene called CCR5, which the HIV virus uses to get into T-cells. There's a natural mutation that does this — some European populations carry it — and He thought he was giving these children a version of that protection.

The scientific community's response was, essentially, "you did not think this through."

The children's father was HIV-positive. But in IVF, sperm are washed first. The actual risk of transmission was vanishingly small. There was no compelling medical reason to edit their germline — meaning changes that will propagate to their children, and their children's children, forever, irreversibly.

Then a Berkeley study came out analyzing the very mutation He was trying to recreate. People who carry two copies have 21% higher all-cause mortality before age 76. The HIV protection comes with increased vulnerability to influenza and West Nile virus. This is not what He disclosed to the families.

The editing was also mosaic — not every cell in the children's bodies carried the deletion. Their actual protection status is unknown.

He Jiankui went to prison for three years. The children are still out there. Nobody publicly knows their genetic status. Nobody is responsible for monitoring them.

The technology got ahead of the governance. By a lot.
