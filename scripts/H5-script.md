# H5: The Gut Bacteria That Decide If Chemotherapy Works
*~240 words*

---

Immune checkpoint inhibitors are some of the most exciting drugs we have. They release the brakes on T-cell activity, letting the immune system attack tumors it was previously ignoring. In metastatic melanoma, five-year survival rates above 50% in some cohorts. Diseases that used to be a rapid death sentence.

But they only work in about 40 to 60% of melanoma patients. For other cancers — pancreatic, colorectal, prostate — the response rates are often far lower. Oncologists have struggled to predict who responds and why.

A significant part of the answer turns out to be living in the gut.

In 2018, two independent groups published simultaneously in Science. MD Anderson analyzed gut microbiomes from melanoma patients before treatment. Patients enriched in a bacterium called Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and related Ruminococcaceae species were significantly more likely to respond to checkpoint inhibitors. Patients enriched in Bacteroidales species were significantly less likely. A European group found complementary results with Bifidobacterium longum.

This runs through the immune microenvironment: gut bacteria produce compounds that enhance T-cell function systemically, promoting the cytotoxic T-cell activity that immunotherapy needs already primed.

The direct test came in 2021. Patients with metastatic melanoma who had failed prior immunotherapy received fecal transplants from super-responder donors. Several converted to responders.

You can transfer immunotherapy susceptibility from one human gut to another.

The implication no one loves: antibiotics given before checkpoint inhibitor therapy associate with significantly worse outcomes. The bacteria those antibiotics destroyed may have been load-bearing infrastructure.
