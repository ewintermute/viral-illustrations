# D4: The Most Extreme Marriage in Nature
*~240 words*

---

In the deep ocean, finding a mate might happen once in a lifetime. Maybe never.

The ceratioid anglerfish solved this problem by abolishing the distinction between two individuals entirely.

Male ceratioid anglerfish hatch tiny — a few centimeters — with enormous eyes, sensitive chemoreceptors, and no digestive system worth mentioning. They don't eat. They can barely swim. Their entire existence is a search: find a female before starving. When a male finds one, he bites down.

In some species, he never lets go.

Enzymes at the bite site dissolve the skin of both animals simultaneously. The wound heals around him. Their circulatory systems begin to merge. Over weeks, his eyes degenerate. His fins atrophy. His digestive system disappears. His brain reduces to a minimal stub. He loses independent existence completely, retaining only his testes — functioning, supplied with blood from the female's heart, waiting for the hormonal signal to release sperm.

One researcher described him as "a penis with a heart." A female can carry up to eight fused males simultaneously.

Now, you might think: wait. Two genetically distinct organisms sharing blood should trigger immune rejection and die within days. That's what happens in any transplant without immunosuppression.

A 2020 study in Science solved the puzzle: permanently-fusing anglerfish have lost the genes for adaptive immunity. MHC class II. Antibodies. Killer T cells. Gone. They traded their immune system for guaranteed reproductive success.

The deep sea is extreme enough that evolution made that deal and called it reasonable.
