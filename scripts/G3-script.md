# G3: Bacteria Invented Gene Editing. We Just Figured Out How to Steal It.
*~240 words*

---

In 1987, a Japanese researcher studying a gene in E. coli noticed something strange in the flanking sequence. Short repeated DNA elements, separated by spacers of non-repetitive sequence. He published it. Nobody knew what it meant. He moved on.

Thirty-five years later, that strange sequence is the basis for FDA-approved treatments for sickle cell disease and beta-thalassemia, and probably the most consequential tool in the history of biology.

The structure was named CRISPR. For two decades, researchers found similar sequences in bacteria and archaea around the world. In 2005, a Spanish microbiologist named Francisco Mojica noticed the spacers matched sequences from bacteriophages — viruses that infect bacteria — and proposed that CRISPR was an adaptive immune system. Bacteria were storing memories of past viral infections as molecular records in their own DNA.

Several journals rejected the paper.

The mechanism: survive a viral attack, clip a fragment of the viral DNA, insert it into your CRISPR array. Next time that virus shows up, produce a guide RNA matching the viral sequence. Direct the Cas9 protein to find and cut that DNA. Virus destroyed. Immune memory heritable by descendants.

In 2012, Emmanuelle Charpentier and Jennifer Doudna showed that Cas9 could be reprogrammed with a synthetic guide RNA to cut any DNA sequence you design. The bacterial scissors became a universal editing tool. Within months, it was working in human cells.

Nobel Prize in Chemistry, 2020.

Bacteria have been running this system for hundreds of millions of years. We watched long enough to figure out how to use it.
