# F2: The Parasite That Makes You Love Cats
*~250 words*

---

About one third of all humans on Earth are currently infected with a single-celled parasite called Toxoplasma gondii. Most of them have no idea.

The standard story is: you catch it from undercooked meat or cat feces, it forms dormant cysts in your muscles and brain, it does nothing. Benign. Asymptomatic. Move on.

Here's what it does to rats.

In 1994, researchers at Oxford found that Toxoplasma-infected rats lose their innate fear of cat urine. Normal rats exposed to the odor of feline urine freeze, retreat, hide. Infected rats don't. In some experiments, infected rats actually spend more time near the smell.

The evolutionary logic is elegant and deeply unsettling: Toxoplasma can only sexually reproduce inside a cat's gut. The parasite is inside a rat. The rat getting eaten by a cat would be great for the parasite's reproductive success.

The mechanism in rat brains: instead of activating fear circuits in the hypothalamus when cat urine is detected, infected rats activate the posterodorsal medial amygdala — the region that normally responds to sex pheromones. The smell of a cat is processed as attractive. Dopamine antagonists block the effect.

Now. What does it do to humans?

Multiple studies have found associations between Toxoplasma seropositivity and increased risk-taking, higher rates of traffic accidents, elevated impulsivity. More than 100 published studies find elevated Toxoplasma antibodies in schizophrenia patients — about 1.9 times the rate in uninfected controls.

Correlation is not causation. None of this is proof.

But a parasite that lives silently in a third of human brains, altering dopamine signaling in measurable ways, for decades — that is not nothing.
