# B10: Rewriting the Code in Real Time
*~230 words*

---

DNA makes RNA. RNA makes protein. The sequence is fixed. Information flows one way.

That's the central dogma. It's basically correct for every complex organism on Earth.

Then there are octopuses.

Octopuses, squid, and cuttlefish edit their own RNA — rewriting their genetic instructions in real time, in response to their environment, without touching the DNA. The mechanism exists in other animals at low levels. In cephalopods, it has been pushed to a scale that breaks the simple version of the story.

It works like this: enzymes called ADARs convert specific adenosine bases in messenger RNA to inosine, which gets read as guanosine. Net result: the protein sequence changes without any change to the DNA. No mutation. No inheritance. Just a reversible, environmental edit.

Researchers at University of Chicago acclimated Octopus bimaculoides to cold water — 13 degrees Celsius — and found over 13,000 protein-altering RNA edits in their neural tissue. Warm water? About 550. These weren't random edits. They were concentrated in neural proteins: ion channels, synaptic transmission, calcium signaling, molecular motors. The exact machinery you'd want to tune for cold conditions.

The edits appear within hours. Steady state in about four days.

Wild octopuses near Long Beach, California show higher editing frequencies in winter than summer. It's not a lab artifact. The animals use this.

What cephalopods seem to have done is trade genomic flexibility for transcriptomic flexibility. Instead of waiting for evolution to write new protein sequences into the genome, they write them in response to Tuesday's temperature.
